# Unit 3: History of Psychology

## Structuralism and Functionalism

Psychology's founding moment is usually dated to 1879, when **Wilhelm Wundt** — often called the "father of psychology" — opened the first dedicated laboratory for psychological research, in Leipzig, Germany, marking the field's formal break from philosophy. Wundt's approach, later developed into **Structuralism** by his student **Edward Titchener**, sought to analyze the adult mind into its simplest components (sensations, feelings, images) using **introspection**: trained observers systematically reporting their conscious experience in response to controlled stimuli.

**Functionalism**, led by **William James** and directly influenced by Darwinian evolution, asked a different question: not what the mind is made of, but what it is _for_. James's landmark textbook, _The Principles of Psychology_ (1890), described consciousness as a continuous, ever-shifting **"stream of consciousness"** that resists being broken into static elements — the opposite of the structuralist project. Functionalists studied how mental processes help an organism adapt to its environment, a framework that opened the door to studying learning, individual differences, and applied psychology far more readily than structuralism's narrow focus on trained introspection ever could.

| School | Central question | Method |
|---|---|---|
| Structuralism | What are the mind's basic elements? | Introspection |
| Functionalism | What is the mind's adaptive purpose? | Broader observation |

**G. Stanley Hall**, Wundt's student and the first American to earn a psychology PhD, founded the **American Psychological Association (APA)** in 1892 and served as its first president, institutionalizing the new science in the United States.

> **Common pitfall:** assuming structuralism and functionalism were about competing FACTS. They were really about competing QUESTIONS — one asking what the mind is made of, the other asking what the mind is for — and functionalism's question proved far more fruitful for psychology's later growth.

## Psychoanalysis

**Psychoanalysis**, founded by **Sigmund Freud** in the late 19th century, proposed that much of human behavior is driven by the **unconscious mind** — thoughts, urges, and memories inaccessible to conscious awareness but still shaping feeling and action. Freud modeled the psyche as three interacting systems: the **Id** (innate biological drives, operating on the "pleasure principle"), the **Ego** (the rational mediator, operating on the "reality principle"), and the **Superego** (internalized moral standards). Conflict among the three, especially repression of forbidden sexual or aggressive impulses, was thought to generate anxiety and, if unresolved, psychological symptoms.

Freud also proposed a sequence of **psychosexual stages** — Oral, Anal, Phallic, Latency, and Genital — arguing that unresolved conflict at any stage could leave a lasting "fixation" shaping adult personality. To manage anxiety, Freud proposed the ego relies on **defense mechanisms**: **repression** (pushing a threatening thought out of awareness entirely), **projection** (attributing one's own unacceptable impulse to someone else), and **sublimation** (redirecting an impulse into a socially valued activity), among others.

| System | Governing principle |
|---|---|
| Id | Pleasure principle — wants immediate gratification |
| Ego | Reality principle — mediates between id, superego, and the world |
| Superego | Morality principle — internalized rules and ideals |

Freud's inner circle later split over his heavy emphasis on sexuality: **Carl Jung** broke away to develop analytical psychology, centered on a **collective unconscious** of shared **archetypes**, while **Alfred Adler** developed individual psychology, centered on **feelings of inferiority** and the drive to compensate for them.

> **Common pitfall:** treating "defense mechanism" as a sign of weakness or dishonesty. In Freud's model, defense mechanisms are a normal, universal function of the ego — everyone uses them to manage anxiety; only their rigidity or extremity becomes a clinical concern.

## Behaviorism

**Behaviorism**, launched by **John B. Watson**'s 1913 manifesto "Psychology as the Behaviorist Views It," rejected introspection and the study of consciousness altogether, arguing that psychology should be a purely objective science of **observable behavior**. Watson's famous (and ethically troubling) "Little Albert" experiment (1920) conditioned a fear response to a white rat in an infant, demonstrating that even emotional responses could be built through **classical conditioning**.

Classical conditioning itself was discovered not by a psychologist but by the Russian physiologist **Ivan Pavlov**, studying digestion in dogs. Pavlov noticed his dogs salivated not only to food (an **unconditioned stimulus** producing an **unconditioned response**) but eventually to a previously neutral cue, like a bell, once it had been repeatedly paired with food — the bell became a **conditioned stimulus** producing a **conditioned response**. This stimulus-response (S-R) model suggested behavior could be explained as a direct product of environmental associations.

**B.F. Skinner** extended behaviorism through **operant conditioning**, which explains behavior through its _consequences_ rather than its antecedents. Using the "Skinner box," he showed that behavior followed by **reinforcement** becomes more likely to recur, while behavior followed by **punishment** becomes less likely — a framework Skinner extended into a thoroughgoing "radical behaviorism" that treated internal mental states as unnecessary for explaining behavior at all.

| Figure | Contribution |
|---|---|
| Watson | 1913 manifesto; founded behaviorism as a movement |
| Pavlov | Discovered classical conditioning (accidentally, studying digestion) |
| Skinner | Developed operant conditioning and radical behaviorism |

> **Common pitfall:** assuming Pavlov set out to study learning. He did not — he was a physiologist studying digestive secretions, and classical conditioning was an accidental discovery that redirected the course of an entire branch of psychology.

## Gestalt Psychology

**Gestalt Psychology**, emerging in Germany in the early 20th century, directly challenged Structuralism's project of decomposing experience into elements. Its central claim — that the **whole is different from the sum of its parts** — was demonstrated vividly by **Max Wertheimer**'s discovery of the **phi phenomenon** (1912): two stationary lights flashed in rapid alternating sequence are perceived as a single light in continuous motion. No individual stimulus contains that motion; the brain constructs it by organizing the sequence as a whole, which is exactly what a strictly element-by-element analysis could never predict.

The **Berlin School** — Wertheimer along with **Wolfgang Köhler** and **Kurt Koffka** — turned this holistic insight into a systematic account of **perceptual organization**, the **Law of Prägnanz** (simplicity): the mind spontaneously perceives the simplest, most stable interpretation of ambiguous sensory input available. Several specific laws follow from it:

| Gestalt law | Principle |
|---|---|
| Proximity | Nearby elements are grouped together |
| Similarity | Similar-looking elements are grouped together |
| Closure | The mind fills in gaps to perceive a complete shape |
| Continuity | The mind follows a smooth, continuous line over an abrupt break |
| Figure-ground | One region is perceived as the object, the rest as background |

Köhler also studied problem-solving in chimpanzees, famously showing that a chimp named Sultan could stack boxes or join sticks to reach an out-of-reach banana — not through trial and error, but through a sudden reorganization of the whole problem, an "aha" moment Köhler called **insight learning**.

> **Common pitfall:** quoting the Gestalt principle as "the whole is GREATER than the sum of its parts." The original claim is more precise: the whole is _different_ from the sum of its parts — a perceived whole (like apparent motion) can be a genuinely new experience, not just a bigger pile of the same elements.

Two lamp icons: both lamps static and unlit; they alternate flashing at a slow interval, clearly two separate blinks; the interval shortens to the phi-phenomenon range and a single accent dot appears to glide smooth.

$$\text{Perceived whole} \neq \text{sum of static parts}$$

## Humanistic Psychology

**Humanistic Psychology** emerged in the 1950s as a deliberate reaction against both Behaviorism (which it saw as mechanistic, reducing people to stimulus-response machines) and Psychoanalysis (which it saw as overly pessimistic, reducing people to unconscious conflict). Calling itself the **"Third Force,"** it emphasized the inherent goodness of people and their capacity for growth and self-direction.

**Abraham Maslow** proposed a **Hierarchy of Needs**, usually depicted as a five-tier pyramid: physiological needs (food, water) at the base, followed by safety, love and belonging, esteem, and finally **self-actualization** at the summit — the ongoing process of fulfilling one's own unique potential. Maslow argued lower needs generally have to be reasonably satisfied before higher ones become motivating priorities.

**Carl Rogers** developed **Person-Centered Therapy**, grounded in the belief that people possess an innate drive toward growth that flourishes best in a relationship offering **unconditional positive regard** — acceptance and support regardless of what a person says or does — along with genuineness and empathy. Central to Rogers's theory is the **self-concept**: the organized set of beliefs and perceptions a person holds about who they are. Psychological distress arises from **incongruence**, a mismatch between this self-concept and either one's actual experience or one's **ideal self** — the person one wishes to be.

| Figure | Central idea |
|---|---|
| Maslow | Hierarchy of needs, culminating in self-actualization |
| Rogers | Unconditional positive regard, self-concept, congruence |

> **Common pitfall:** treating Maslow's pyramid as a rigid staircase that must be climbed in strict lockstep. Maslow himself noted many exceptions — people regularly pursue esteem or self-actualization even amid unmet lower needs — the hierarchy describes a general tendency, not an iron law.

## Cognitive Psychology

The **Cognitive Revolution** of the 1950s and 60s redirected psychology's attention back to internal mental processes — memory, perception, reasoning, language — that behaviorism had deliberately treated as an unobservable "black box" unworthy of study. A pivotal moment was **Noam Chomsky**'s 1959 critique of Skinner's book _Verbal Behavior_, arguing that children acquire language far too rapidly and creatively to be explained by simple reinforcement alone, and must instead possess some innate capacity for language.

The new field adopted the **information-processing model**, treating the human mind as analogous to a computer: information is **encoded**, **stored**, and **retrieved**, much as a computer processes data through defined stages. **George Miller**'s 1956 paper "The Magical Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two" proposed that short-term memory holds only about seven discrete items at once, highlighting real capacity limits on mental processing. **Ulric Neisser**'s 1967 textbook, simply titled _Cognitive Psychology_, gave the new field its name.

**Jean Piaget** applied a related developmental lens, describing how children construct an understanding of the world through four qualitatively different stages:

| Stage | Approximate age | Key feature |
|---|---|---|
| Sensorimotor | 0-2 years | Learning through senses and motor action; object permanence develops |
| Preoperational | 2-7 years | Symbolic thought, but lacking logical operations |
| Concrete operational | 7-11 years | Logical thought about concrete objects |
| Formal operational | 11+ years | Abstract, hypothetical reasoning |

> **Common pitfall:** treating the "black box" metaphor as behaviorism's insult to the mind. It was actually a methodological choice — behaviorists did not deny that internal states might exist, they simply argued science should study only what can be directly observed, which cognitive psychology's new methods (like reaction-time measures) eventually made it possible to challenge.

A memory slot-rack: an empty rack with numbered slots; items load in one at a time up to slot 7, each slot lighting accent as it fills; two more items attempt to load past slot 9 and visibly spill off the rack.

$$\text{Short-term memory} \approx 5 \text{ to } 9 \text{ items}$$

## Biological Psychology

**Biological Psychology** (or behavioral neuroscience) investigates the physiological and neural mechanisms of behavior. One of its founding debates concerned the timing and cause of emotion. **William James** and Carl Lange independently proposed the **James-Lange theory**: physiological arousal comes first, and the SUBJECTIVE feeling of emotion follows as the brain's interpretation of that bodily state — we feel afraid because we notice ourselves trembling, not the reverse. **Walter Cannon** and Philip Bard challenged this with the **Cannon-Bard theory**: physiological arousal and the subjective experience of emotion occur _simultaneously_ and somewhat independently, both triggered together by the thalamus relaying signals to the cortex and the body at once.

Meanwhile, **Karl Lashley** searched for the **engram** — the physical trace of a memory in the brain — by training rats on a maze task and then surgically removing varying amounts of cortical tissue. He found that memory loss depended more on _how much_ cortex was removed than on precisely _which_ region, leading him to propose two principles: **mass action** (the cortex functions as an integrated whole for complex tasks) and **equipotentiality** (many regions of a functional area can substitute for one another after damage). Lashley never found a single, localized engram.

| Theory | Order of events |
|---|---|
| James-Lange | Arousal first, then subjective emotion |
| Cannon-Bard | Arousal and emotion simultaneously, both from the thalamus |

Later, **Donald Hebb** proposed a cellular mechanism for how experience changes the brain: "cells that fire together, wire together," now known as **Hebbian learning** — repeated co-activation of two neurons strengthens the synaptic connection between them, providing a physical basis for how the engram Lashley searched for might actually be stored, distributed across many strengthened connections rather than any single site.

> **Common pitfall:** assuming Lashley's failure to find a single engram meant memory has no physical basis at all. His conclusion was the opposite — memory has a distributed physical basis across many cortical regions working together, not a localized one.

## Evolutionary Psychology

**Evolutionary Psychology** applies the logic of evolutionary biology to the human mind, treating cognitive architecture as a product of **natural selection** shaped to solve recurrent problems faced by ancestral humans in the **environment of evolutionary adaptedness (EEA)** — the ancestral conditions under which a trait evolved, not necessarily the modern environment we now inhabit. **Leda Cosmides** and **John Tooby**, key architects of the field, along with **David Buss**, argued the mind is best understood as a collection of specialized information-processing mechanisms, each shaped to solve a specific adaptive problem, such as detecting social cheaters or selecting a healthy, high-quality mate.

The field distinguishes three types of traits: **adaptations** (features directly shaped by selection because they raised survival or reproduction), **by-products** (features that come along for the ride, riding on an adaptation without being independently selected for), and **noise** (random variation with no functional significance at all). Critics, notably Stephen Jay Gould, warned against assuming every trait must be an adaptation — some are merely **spandrels**, incidental by-products of other adaptations.

Buss's large cross-cultural studies across dozens of countries found broadly consistent patterns in mate preferences — for instance, resource-related traits mattering more, on average, to women's preferences and youth/fertility cues mattering more, on average, to men's — patterns evolutionary psychologists interpret through the lens of differing reproductive investment. Cosmides also demonstrated that people reason far more accurately about abstract logical rules when the same rule is framed as a **social contract** (detecting cheaters) than when framed abstractly, suggesting a specialized **cheater-detection** mechanism rather than a single, general-purpose logic module.

> **Common pitfall:** treating every evolutionary psychology claim as automatically an "adaptation." Evolutionary psychologists are explicit that some traits are by-products or noise — the discipline requires positive evidence that a trait was shaped BY selection FOR its current function, not just a plausible-sounding story.

## Social Psychology

**Social Psychology**, often traced to **Kurt Lewin**'s field theory ("behavior is a function of the person and their environment"), gained enormous momentum after World War II, as researchers sought to understand how ordinary people could participate in atrocity and mass compliance. Three landmark studies from this era still define the field's popular image.

**Solomon Asch** (1951) had participants judge simple line lengths in a group of confederates who unanimously gave an obviously wrong answer; on average, genuine participants conformed to the incorrect majority on about **37%** of the critical trials — a striking demonstration of social **conformity** even against the plain evidence of one's own eyes.

**Stanley Milgram** (1961-63) told participants they were delivering increasingly severe electric shocks to a learner (in fact an actor, never actually shocked) whenever the learner answered incorrectly. In the best-known baseline version, about **65%** of participants obeyed the experimenter's instructions all the way to the maximum, clearly dangerous shock level — revealing the surprising power of perceived legitimate authority over individual conscience.

**Philip Zimbardo**'s Stanford Prison Experiment (1971) randomly assigned student volunteers to play "guards" or "prisoners" in a simulated prison; the study was planned to run two weeks but was halted after just **six days** as guards became abusively authoritarian and prisoners showed severe distress, illustrating how quickly assigned social roles can override individual disposition.

| Study | Researcher | Key finding |
|---|---|---|
| Conformity | Asch (1951) | ~37% conformity on critical trials |
| Obedience | Milgram (1961-63) | ~65% obeyed to maximum shock |
| Simulated prison | Zimbardo (1971) | Halted after 6 of a planned 14 days |

These studies also reshaped psychology's **research ethics**: the psychological distress caused to participants in the Milgram and Zimbardo studies helped drive the field toward mandatory informed consent, the right to withdraw, and independent ethical review boards for any study involving human participants.

> **Common pitfall:** concluding from these studies that "anyone would do anything under pressure." The actual, more precise finding is that SITUATIONAL factors — perceived legitimate authority, gradual escalation, assigned social roles — reliably influence behavior far more than most people predict in advance, not that individual differences or resistance disappear entirely.

$$\text{Obedience rate} \approx 0.65 \text{ (baseline)}$$

## Contemporary Trends

Contemporary psychology is a large, diverse discipline built on the integration of its earlier schools and its expansion into **applied fields**. **Clinical psychology**, focused on diagnosing and treating mental disorders, remains the largest subfield. **Industrial-Organizational (I/O) psychology** applies psychological principles to the workplace, improving both productivity and employee well-being. **Educational psychology** studies how people learn and develops better instructional methods. The **biopsychosocial model**, proposed by George Engel in 1977, has become the standard integrating framework across these fields, treating health and behavior as the joint product of biological, psychological, and social factors rather than any single cause.

A major recent shift is the rise of **Positive Psychology**, championed by **Martin Seligman** (APA president in 1998), which redirects attention from pathology and dysfunction toward human strengths and flourishing. Central concepts include **resilience** (the capacity to adapt well after adversity), **flow** (Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi's term for deep, effortless absorption in a challenging activity), and subjective **well-being**.

Two further developments have reshaped the field's methodology. The **WEIRD samples critique** (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic) points out that psychology has historically drawn most of its participants — and thus much of its "universal" theory — from a narrow, unrepresentative slice of humanity, urging more cross-cultural research. The **replication crisis**, which gained wide attention in the 2010s after many classic findings failed to reproduce in careful follow-up studies, has driven an "open science" movement emphasizing **preregistration** of hypotheses and methods before data collection, to reduce the chance that flexible after-the-fact analysis manufactures false positive results.

| Development | Core idea |
|---|---|
| Biopsychosocial model | Health is biological + psychological + social |
| Positive psychology | Study strengths and flourishing, not just dysfunction |
| WEIRD critique | Most samples are unrepresentative of humanity |
| Replication crisis | Many classic findings fail to reproduce; open science responds |

> **Common pitfall:** treating positive psychology as simply "optimism" or self-help. It is a research program using the same scientific methods as the rest of psychology, just aimed at strengths, resilience, and flourishing rather than exclusively at dysfunction.
