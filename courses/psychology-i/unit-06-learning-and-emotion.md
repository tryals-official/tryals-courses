## Classical Conditioning

Beyond the basic acquisition of a conditioned response, classical conditioning has a full life cycle worth tracing in detail. **Acquisition** is the initial learning phase, when a neutral stimulus is repeatedly paired with an unconditioned stimulus until it becomes a conditioned stimulus; pairing works best when the neutral stimulus is presented shortly BEFORE the unconditioned stimulus, so it can serve as a predictive cue.

If the conditioned stimulus is later presented repeatedly WITHOUT the unconditioned stimulus, the conditioned response gradually weakens and disappears — a process called **extinction**. Extinction is not the same as forgetting, though: after a rest period, the extinguished response can suddenly reappear, weakly and temporarily, when the conditioned stimulus is presented again — a phenomenon called **spontaneous recovery**, showing that the original association was suppressed, not erased.

Two further processes shape how broadly a conditioned response applies. **Stimulus generalization** is responding to stimuli similar to the original conditioned stimulus as though they were the same (Little Albert, conditioned to fear a white rat, also showed fear of other white, fuzzy objects). **Stimulus discrimination** is the opposite: learning to respond ONLY to the original conditioned stimulus and not to similar ones, typically through selectively pairing the true CS with the US while presenting similar stimuli without it.

| Process | What happens |
|---|---|
| Acquisition | Initial pairing establishes the CS-CR link |
| Extinction | CR weakens when CS is repeatedly presented alone |
| Spontaneous recovery | A weakened CR briefly reappears after a rest period |
| Generalization | Similar stimuli also trigger the CR |
| Discrimination | Only the original CS reliably triggers the CR |

Finally, **higher-order conditioning** extends the chain: once a stimulus has become a reliable conditioned stimulus, pairing a NEW neutral stimulus with that established CS (rather than with the original US) can make the new stimulus a conditioned stimulus too — conditioning built on conditioning, though typically weaker and harder to sustain than the original link.

> **Common pitfall:** treating extinction as if the learned association is permanently erased. Spontaneous recovery demonstrates that extinction mainly suppresses the response rather than deleting the underlying association — which is also why relapse after successfully "extinguishing" a fear or craving remains a real clinical concern.

## Operant Conditioning

Operant conditioning explains behavior through its consequences. Two dimensions define every consequence: whether something is ADDED or REMOVED, and whether the behavior becomes MORE or LESS likely. **Positive reinforcement** adds something pleasant to increase a behavior (a treat for a trick); **negative reinforcement** removes something unpleasant to increase a behavior (a seatbelt chime stopping once you buckle up) — note that "negative" here means removal, not "bad," a frequent point of confusion. **Positive punishment** adds something unpleasant to decrease a behavior (a scolding); **negative punishment** removes something pleasant to decrease a behavior (taking away a phone).

| | Adds something | Removes something |
|---|---|---|
| Increases behavior | Positive reinforcement | Negative reinforcement |
| Decreases behavior | Positive punishment | Negative punishment |

Complex behaviors are built gradually through **shaping**: reinforcing successive approximations that move closer and closer to a target behavior, rather than waiting for the full behavior to occur spontaneously. Reinforcers themselves come in two kinds: **primary reinforcers** are innately rewarding (food, water), while **secondary (conditioned) reinforcers** acquire their value through learned association (money, praise, tokens).

How OFTEN a behavior is reinforced also shapes the resulting response pattern, described by four **schedules of reinforcement**:

| Schedule | Pattern | Example | Response pattern |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fixed ratio | After a set number of responses | Piecework pay | High rate, brief pause after each reinforcement |
| Variable ratio | After an average, unpredictable number of responses | Slot machines | Highest, steadiest rate; very resistant to extinction |
| Fixed interval | After a set amount of time | Weekly paycheck | Slow right after reinforcement, speeding up near the next one |
| Variable interval | After an average, unpredictable amount of time | Checking a messaging app | Slow, steady rate throughout |

> **Common pitfall:** treating "negative reinforcement" as a synonym for punishment. It is the OPPOSITE: reinforcement always makes a behavior MORE likely, whether by adding something pleasant (positive) or removing something unpleasant (negative) — punishment is what decreases behavior.

Four small cumulative-response graphs: fixed ratio shows a stair-step with flat pauses, variable ratio shows a steep smooth climb, fixed interval shows a scalloped wave.

$$\text{Variable ratio} \to \text{highest, most persistent response rate}$$

## Cognitive Factors in Learning

Not all learning shows up immediately in behavior — a challenge to strict behaviorism's insistence on observable behavior alone. **Edward Tolman**'s studies of rats in mazes demonstrated **latent learning**: rats allowed to explore a maze freely, with no reward at the end, still learned its layout — they had formed an internal **cognitive map** of the maze — even though nothing in their behavior showed it at the time. When food was later placed at the maze's end, these rats suddenly navigated it as efficiently as rats that had been rewarded all along, proving the learning had been there all along, simply unexpressed until there was a reason to use it.

A very different, and troubling, discovery came from **Martin Seligman**'s early research. Dogs exposed to inescapable electric shocks eventually stopped trying to escape entirely — and, strikingly, continued to passively endure the shocks even later, when escape became easily possible. Seligman called this **learned helplessness**: after learning that their actions had no effect on an outcome, the dogs generalized that lesson even to new situations where action WOULD have worked. This finding became foundational to later cognitive models of depression, where a person's belief that their actions cannot change a bad situation can itself perpetuate passivity, even once circumstances change.

| Finding | What it showed |
|---|---|
| Latent learning | Learning can occur without reinforcement or visible behavioral change |
| Cognitive map | An internal mental representation of a spatial layout |
| Learned helplessness | Passivity generalizes from a truly inescapable situation to new, escapable ones |

Both findings pushed psychology to acknowledge that INTERNAL cognitive representations and expectations — not just external reinforcement history — shape behavior, a key bridge between strict behaviorism and the cognitive revolution discussed elsewhere in this course.

> **Common pitfall:** assuming learned helplessness means an animal or person has simply "given up" out of laziness or low motivation. The mechanism is a learned EXPECTATION that outcomes are uncontrollable, generalized inappropriately to a new situation — not a general lack of effort or motivation as such.

## Social Learning Theory

**Albert Bandura**'s **Social Learning Theory** (later broadened into social-cognitive theory) argued that much human learning happens through **observation**, without any direct reinforcement of the observer at all. His landmark **Bobo doll experiment** (1961) had children watch an adult model either aggressively attack an inflatable Bobo doll or play calmly near it; children who observed the aggressive model went on to imitate specific aggressive acts toward the doll far more than children in the calm-model condition — even though the children themselves had received no reinforcement for aggression.

Bandura proposed that observational learning requires four sequential processes: **attention** (the observer must actually notice the model's behavior), **retention** (the observer must remember what was observed), **reproduction** (the observer must be physically/behaviorally capable of reproducing the action), and **motivation** (the observer must have a reason to actually perform the behavior, often shaped by **vicarious reinforcement** — seeing the model rewarded or punished for the behavior, which shapes the observer's own likelihood of performing it, even without any direct consequence to the observer themselves).

| Process | What it requires |
|---|---|
| Attention | Noticing the model's behavior |
| Retention | Remembering what was observed |
| Reproduction | Being capable of performing the action |
| Motivation | Having a reason to actually perform it |

Bandura's broader concept of **reciprocal determinism** holds that behavior, personal/cognitive factors, and the environment all continuously influence one another — a person's behavior shapes their environment, the environment shapes their cognitions, and their cognitions shape their behavior, in an ongoing loop rather than any single one-way cause.

> **Common pitfall:** assuming observational learning requires the observer to be reinforced directly before any imitation can occur. Bandura's whole point was the opposite — imitation of the aggressive model occurred with NO direct reinforcement to the child at all; only vicarious (observed) consequences, or none, were needed to shape the behavior.

## Theories of Emotion

Building on the earlier James-Lange (arousal precedes emotion) and Cannon-Bard (arousal and emotion are simultaneous) debate, **Stanley Schachter** and **Jerome Singer** proposed a **Two-Factor Theory** of emotion in 1962: emotion requires BOTH physiological arousal AND a cognitive LABEL for that arousal, drawn from the surrounding context. In their classic study, participants were injected with adrenaline and told either accurate information about the resulting arousal, inaccurate information, or nothing at all, then placed alongside a confederate acting either euphoric or irritated. Participants left uninformed about their arousal's true cause were the most likely to "catch" the confederate's emotional state, effectively borrowing a label from the situation to explain their own unexplained arousal — a phenomenon called **misattribution of arousal**.

A related, widely cited demonstration is the **Capilano Suspension Bridge study** (Dutton and Aron, 1974): men who had just crossed a high, wobbly suspension bridge (physiologically arousing) rated a female interviewer as more attractive, and were more likely to contact her afterward, than men who crossed a low, stable bridge — plausibly misattributing lingering physiological arousal from the bridge to attraction toward the interviewer.

| Theory | Core claim |
|---|---|
| James-Lange | Arousal, then emotion follows as interpretation |
| Cannon-Bard | Arousal and emotion occur simultaneously |
| Schachter-Singer (Two-Factor) | Emotion requires arousal PLUS a cognitive label |

**Richard Lazarus**'s **cognitive appraisal theory** goes a step further, arguing that some form of cognitive appraisal — even a very rapid, largely automatic one — must occur BEFORE emotion arises at all, since the same physiological event (a sudden bump) can produce very different emotions (fear versus amusement) depending entirely on how it is appraised in context.

> **Common pitfall:** assuming the two-factor theory says the LABEL alone creates emotion, with arousal being unnecessary. Schachter and Singer's theory requires BOTH ingredients together — genuine physiological arousal plus a context-driven cognitive label — neither one is sufficient on its own.

## Biological Basis of Emotion

The **amygdala** sits at the center of the brain's fear circuitry, but it does not work alone or on a single timescale. Neuroscientist **Joseph LeDoux** described two parallel pathways carrying sensory information toward an emotional response. The **"low road"** runs directly from the thalamus to the amygdala — fast (just milliseconds), but crude, based only on rough sensory features, allowing an almost-instant defensive reaction to a possible threat (jumping back from a stick-shaped shadow) before conscious recognition even occurs. The **"high road"** instead routes through the cortex first, for slower, more detailed, deliberate processing — allowing a follow-up correction once the "stick" is recognized as harmless. This dual-pathway design trades occasional false alarms for speed when speed could be lifesaving.

Emotion is not purely top-down, either: the **facial feedback hypothesis** proposes that facial expressions can influence, and not merely express, subjective emotional experience — in one classic (though since debated and only partially replicated) demonstration, participants who held a pen in their teeth (activating smiling-like muscles) rated cartoons as funnier than participants who held a pen with their lips (activating muscles incompatible with smiling), suggesting the physical act of "smiling" muscles can itself nudge the felt emotion, not just display it.

| Concept | Key idea |
|---|---|
| Low road | Fast, crude thalamus-to-amygdala pathway |
| High road | Slower, detailed pathway via the cortex |
| Facial feedback hypothesis | Facial expression can influence, not just express, emotion |

Across cultures, **Paul Ekman**'s research identified a set of **universal facial expressions** — commonly cited as happiness, sadness, fear, anger, disgust, and surprise — recognized at above-chance rates even in visually isolated cultures with minimal exposure to global media, supporting a biological, evolved basis for at least some basic emotional expressions rather than one purely learned through culture.

> **Common pitfall:** assuming the "low road" is simply a less important, backup pathway. It is actually the FASTER, first-responding pathway precisely because speed matters more than accuracy in a genuine emergency — the "high road" then provides the more accurate correction just slightly afterward.

A branching pathway diagram: a shadow stimulus at the thalamus; a fast accent pulse shoots directly to the amygdala (low road) while a slower ink pulse detours through the cortex (high road).

$$\text{Low road} \to \text{amygdala} \text{ (fast)}, \text{High road} \to \text{cortex} \to \text{amygdala} \text{ (slow)}$$

## Motivation and Drive-Reduction Theory

**Motivation** refers to the processes that initiate, direct, and sustain behavior toward a goal. Early explanations leaned heavily on **instinct theory**, treating much behavior as driven by fixed, innate patterns — but this approach struggled to explain the sheer flexibility and variety of human behavior, and largely fell out of favor as a general account.

**Drive-reduction theory** (Clark Hull) offered a more dynamic alternative: a physiological need (such as low blood sugar) creates an aroused psychological state called a **drive** (hunger), which motivates behavior aimed at reducing that drive and restoring the body's baseline balance, or **homeostasis**. Under this model, satisfying a drive is inherently rewarding because it relieves an uncomfortable state of arousal — motivation is fundamentally a PUSH from within.

| Concept | Role |
|---|---|
| Physiological need | The underlying biological deficit (e.g. low blood sugar) |
| Drive | The resulting aroused psychological state (e.g. hunger) |
| Homeostasis | The stable baseline the organism is motivated to restore |

Drive-reduction theory explains a great deal of behavior focused on genuine physiological needs, but it struggles with behaviors that seem to seek OUT stimulation rather than reduce it — trying a new food when not hungry, seeking a thrill, or pursuing a hobby with no biological deficit behind it. **Incentive theory** addresses this gap by emphasizing external stimuli that PULL behavior toward them because of their anticipated rewards, rather than purely internal states pushing behavior away from discomfort. Most contemporary accounts of motivation treat drive-reduction (internal push) and incentive (external pull) as complementary, not competing, explanations — different behaviors, and often the same behavior at different moments, draw on both.

> **Common pitfall:** treating drive-reduction theory as a complete account of all motivated behavior. It explains behaviors clearly tied to restoring a physiological deficit reasonably well, but struggles with sensation-seeking, curiosity, and reward-pursuit behaviors that are not obviously reducing any biological deficit at all — which is exactly the gap incentive theory was proposed to fill.

## Human Needs and Arousal Theories

Beyond need-based accounts of motivation, **arousal theories** focus on how physiological and psychological AROUSAL LEVEL itself shapes behavior and performance. The classic finding here is the **Yerkes-Dodson Law** (1908): the relationship between arousal and performance follows an **inverted U** shape — performance is poor at very low arousal (under-aroused, unmotivated, sluggish), improves as arousal rises to a moderate, optimal level, and then DECLINES again if arousal becomes too high (overwhelmed, anxious, unable to focus).

Crucially, the Yerkes-Dodson Law also holds that the OPTIMAL level of arousal depends on task difficulty. For simple, well-practiced, or physically dominant tasks, higher arousal can continue to help performance further before it starts to hurt. For complex, unfamiliar, or cognitively demanding tasks, the optimal arousal level is LOWER, and performance starts declining sooner as arousal continues to rise — which is why a moderately nervous athlete might perform a well-drilled physical routine even better than usual, while the same level of nervous arousal can derail someone trying to solve a novel, tricky problem.

| Task type | Optimal arousal level |
|---|---|
| Simple / well-practiced | Relatively high |
| Complex / unfamiliar | Relatively low to moderate |

This inverted-U framework sits alongside other accounts of human needs and motivation discussed elsewhere in this course, like Maslow's hierarchy, but focuses specifically on the AROUSAL dimension: not just what a person needs, but how activated or "keyed up" they are while pursuing it, and how that activation level interacts with what the task actually demands.

> **Common pitfall:** assuming "more arousal is always better for performance," or the reverse, "less arousal is always better." Both extremes are wrong under the Yerkes-Dodson Law — the relationship is curved, not linear, and the OPTIMAL point itself shifts depending on how difficult or complex the task is.

An inverted-U curve: a bare arousal-versus-performance axis; the curve for a simple task draws, peaking far to the right (high arousal); a second curve for a complex task draws in a contrasting shade.

$$\text{Performance peaks at moderate arousal, shifted by task difficulty}$$

## Emotional Intelligence and Regulation

**Emotional intelligence (EI)**, formalized by **Peter Salovey** and **John Mayer** and later popularized by **Daniel Goleman**, describes a set of abilities for perceiving, understanding, and managing emotions — both one's own and other people's. The Salovey-Mayer model breaks EI into four related abilities: **perceiving emotions** (accurately reading emotional cues, in faces, tone, or context), **understanding emotions** (grasping causes, consequences, and how emotions change or combine over time), **using emotions to facilitate thought** (harnessing emotional states to support reasoning, creativity, or decision-making), and **managing emotions** (regulating one's own emotional responses, and influencing others' emotions appropriately).

That final ability, emotion **regulation**, has been studied in particular depth by psychologist **James Gross**, who compared two common regulation strategies. **Reappraisal** involves cognitively reframing a situation before an emotional response fully develops (e.g. interpreting a rejection as "not a good fit" rather than "a personal failure") — research generally finds this strategy is associated with better long-term psychological and even physiological outcomes. **Suppression** instead involves masking or inhibiting the outward EXPRESSION of an emotion after it has already arisen (e.g. keeping a neutral face while inwardly upset) — this strategy tends to be LESS effective, often leaving the internal physiological arousal largely intact (or even increased) while adding the extra cognitive effort of maintaining the mask.

| Strategy | When it acts | Typical effectiveness |
|---|---|---|
| Reappraisal | Before the emotional response fully forms | Generally more effective, better outcomes |
| Suppression | After the emotion has already arisen | Generally less effective; effort without much relief |

| EI ability | What it involves |
|---|---|
| Perceiving emotions | Accurately reading emotional cues |
| Understanding emotions | Grasping causes and consequences |
| Using emotions | Harnessing emotion to support thought |
| Managing emotions | Regulating one's own and others' emotional responses |

> **Common pitfall:** assuming suppression is simply "self-control" and therefore healthy. Suppression specifically targets the outward EXPRESSION of an already-formed emotion, which tends to leave the internal arousal largely unresolved — reappraisal, by intervening EARLIER, before the emotion fully forms, tends to be the more effective regulation strategy.

## Stress and the Emotional Response

Stress and emotion are deeply intertwined: the appraisal processes discussed earlier in this course (is this a threat? do I have the resources to cope?) directly shape which emotion — fear, frustration, or a sense of challenge — accompanies a stressor, and that emotional coloring in turn shapes how a person copes. Chronic, unrelenting stress does not just produce isolated emotional episodes; it can reshape a person's baseline emotional functioning over time.

One well-studied outcome of chronic, particularly WORK-related stress is **burnout**, formalized by **Christina Maslach** as a three-component syndrome distinct from ordinary daily stress or a single stressful episode. **Emotional exhaustion** is the feeling of being emotionally overextended and drained, with nothing further to give. **Depersonalization** (sometimes called cynicism) is a detached, callous, or cynical response toward the people one works with or serves, functioning as a defensive emotional distancing. **Reduced personal accomplishment** is a diminished sense of competence and effectiveness in one's work, even when performance may not have objectively declined.

| Component | What it feels like |
|---|---|
| Emotional exhaustion | Drained, overextended, nothing left to give |
| Depersonalization (cynicism) | Detached, callous, distanced from others |
| Reduced personal accomplishment | Diminished sense of competence and effectiveness |

Burnout is distinct from Selye's General Adaptation Syndrome discussed earlier in this course: GAS describes a general PHYSIOLOGICAL stress-response pattern applicable to almost any sustained stressor, while burnout describes a more specific PSYCHOLOGICAL AND EMOTIONAL syndrome, most closely tied to chronic stress in social or occupational roles that involve sustained emotional engagement with other people, like healthcare, teaching, or caregiving professions.

> **Common pitfall:** treating burnout as simply "being very tired" or a synonym for ordinary stress. Burnout is a specific three-component syndrome — emotional exhaustion is only one piece, alongside depersonalization and a diminished sense of personal accomplishment, which do not necessarily track raw fatigue at all.
