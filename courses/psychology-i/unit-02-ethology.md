# Unit 2: Ethology

## Tinbergen's Four Questions

Niko Tinbergen proposed that a complete biological explanation of any behavior must answer **four questions**, grouped into two levels. **Proximate** causes explain how a behavior happens right now: **Mechanism** is its physiological, neural, or hormonal basis, and **Ontogeny** is how the behavior develops across an individual's lifetime. **Ultimate** causes explain why the behavior exists at all: **Function** is the survival or reproductive advantage it provides, and **Phylogeny** is its evolutionary history across related species.

Applied to bird song: the _mechanism_ involves the syrinx and seasonal hormone changes; the _ontogeny_ involves a young bird learning its song from adult tutors; the _function_ is attracting mates and defending territory; and the _phylogeny_ traces how song evolved from ancestral vocalizations. Missing any one question leaves an incomplete story.

| Level | Question | Example (bird song) |
|---|---|---|
| Proximate | Mechanism | Syrinx and hormones drive the physical act of singing |
| Proximate | Ontogeny | The song is learned from adult tutors during development |
| Ultimate | Function | Singing attracts mates and defends territory |
| Ultimate | Phylogeny | Song traces an evolutionary lineage across bird species |

A common error is treating one of the four as the "real" explanation and the others as optional detail. In fact they are complementary: a hormonal mechanism and a territorial function are both true simultaneously, at different levels of analysis, and neither one substitutes for the other.

> **Common pitfall:** treating a "how" answer (mechanism) as though it competes with a "why" answer (function). Asking how a bird's syrinx produces song and asking why singing evolved are both legitimate questions about the very same behavior — not rival explanations.

## Fixed Action Patterns

A **Fixed Action Pattern (FAP)** is an innate, stereotyped sequence of behavior that, once triggered, runs to completion regardless of further changes in the environment — it is often described as "ballistic," like a fired projectile. FAPs are triggered by a **sign stimulus** (or **releaser**): a specific feature of the environment that the animal is innately tuned to detect. Konrad Lorenz and Niko Tinbergen proposed the **Innate Releasing Mechanism (IRM)** as the hypothetical neural filter that recognizes sign stimuli and unleashes the matching FAP.

Tinbergen's classic demonstration used the male three-spined stickleback fish, which performs an aggressive display toward any object bearing a red underside — even a crude, unrealistic model — because red is the sign stimulus for "rival male," regardless of how little else about the model resembles a real fish.

A **supernormal stimulus** is an exaggerated version of a sign stimulus that triggers an even stronger response than the natural cue ever does. Herring gull chicks peck at a red spot on their parent's bill to beg for food; Tinbergen found that chicks pecked _more_ at an artificial stick bearing several red stripes than at a realistic model of a real gull's head. Birds will similarly favor incubating an oversized fake egg over their own — the exaggerated cue simply out-competes reality for the releasing mechanism's attention.

> **Common pitfall:** assuming a Fixed Action Pattern can be stopped partway once the animal "changes its mind." By definition it cannot — once the sign stimulus triggers it, the sequence runs to completion, which is exactly what makes supernormal stimuli able to hijack it so completely.

A stickleback rival-response: a neutral grey model with no red mark evokes no lunge; the same model gains a red-belly patch and the focal fish lunges to attack; an exaggerated model with an extra-bright.

$$\text{Response strength} \propto \text{sign stimulus intensity}$$

## Imprinting and Sensitive Periods

**Imprinting** is a specialized form of learning that occurs rapidly during a narrow window of early development, the **critical period** (or the more flexible **sensitive period**), and — unlike most learning — requires no reinforcement or repeated practice. Konrad Lorenz demonstrated this famously in greylag geese: hatchlings imprint on and follow the first large moving object they encounter, usually their mother, but sometimes an artificial model or even a human, and this bond then shapes their social behavior for life.

There are two primary types. **Filial imprinting** is when a young animal acquires an attachment to a parental figure, ensuring proximity and protection during a vulnerable stage. **Sexual imprinting** is when early exposure to a species' characteristics shapes an animal's mate preferences much later, in adulthood — a zebra finch raised by a foster species of a different appearance will, as an adult, often court that foster species rather than its own.

| Type | What it shapes | Timing |
|---|---|---|
| Filial imprinting | Who to follow for protection | Hours after hatching/birth |
| Sexual imprinting | Adult mate preference | Overlaps early development, expressed later |

The distinction between "critical" and "sensitive" period matters: a strict critical period has hard edges — imprinting essentially cannot occur before or after it — while a sensitive period is a softer window where learning is easiest but not strictly impossible outside it.

> **Common pitfall:** assuming imprinting needs food or some other reward to "stick," the way most learning does. It does not — a gosling will imprint on a bouncing ball with no food involved at all, purely from exposure during the sensitive window.

## Communication and Ritualization

**Animal communication** is the process by which a sender produces a signal that conveys information to a receiver, shaping the receiver's behavior. Signals travel through different **modalities**: chemical (pheromones), visual (displays, coloring), auditory (vocalizations), or tactile (touch). A signal's usefulness depends on being detectable against environmental noise and on being a reliable indicator of the sender's true state or quality.

The **Handicap Principle**, proposed by Amotz Zahavi, explains why many signals — like a peacock's extravagant tail — are so costly to produce. Because the cost is real, only a genuinely high-quality individual can afford to bear it; a low-quality individual attempting to fake the same signal would pay a cost it cannot sustain. Cost is therefore what keeps the signal honest.

**Ritualization** is the evolutionary process by which an ordinary behavior — often one originally serving an unrelated function, such as preening or a **displacement activity** performed during conflict — becomes progressively more stereotyped, exaggerated, and simplified until it functions as a specialized signal in its own right, such as a courtship display or a threat gesture.

| Modality | Example |
|---|---|
| Chemical | An ant trail pheromone |
| Visual | A peacock's tail display |
| Auditory | A wolf's howl |
| Tactile | Primate grooming |

> **Common pitfall:** assuming a cheap, easily faked signal would work just as well as a costly one. It would not — the entire point of the handicap principle is that cost is what makes a signal trustworthy; a cost-free signal could be produced by anyone, honest or not, and receivers would quickly learn to ignore it.

## Foraging and Optimal Foraging Theory

**Foraging** is the behavior of searching for, capturing, and consuming food. **Optimal Foraging Theory (OFT)** predicts that natural selection favors foragers that maximize **net energy intake** per unit of time spent foraging — not simply the most food, but the most energetic payoff per unit of effort. This means weighing the energy content of a food item against its **handling time** (the time needed to capture and process it) and the **search time** needed to find alternatives. A prey item's **profitability** is its energy content divided by its handling time; foragers are predicted to prefer prey with the highest profitability available.

The **Marginal Value Theorem (MVT)** extends this logic to patchy environments. As a forager consumes food within one patch, the local rate of energy gain declines — the easiest items are taken first, and what remains gets harder to find or process. MVT predicts the forager should leave the patch exactly when its **marginal** capture rate (the rate right now) drops to the **average** capture rate available across the whole environment, including travel time to a fresh patch.

| Concept | Definition |
|---|---|
| Profitability | Energy content / handling time |
| Search time | Time spent finding the next item or patch |
| Marginal capture rate | The current, declining rate of gain within a patch |
| Environmental average | The overall achievable rate, including travel between patches |

> **Common pitfall:** assuming a forager should stay in a patch until it is completely emptied. MVT says otherwise — leaving too late wastes time that could be spent gaining food faster elsewhere, so the optimal departure point is set by the environment's average, not by how much food is technically still left.

## Social Behavior and Altruism

**Altruism** — a behavior that increases the fitness of another individual while decreasing the fitness of the actor — is a puzzle for natural selection, which should favor genes that promote the actor's own survival and reproduction. **Kin selection**, formalized as **Hamilton's Rule**, resolves much of the puzzle: altruism is favored when $rb > c$, where $r$ is the genetic relatedness between actor and recipient, $b$ is the fitness benefit to the recipient, and $c$ is the fitness cost to the actor. Helping a relative can still spread the actor's own genes indirectly, through a shared genetic stake in that relative's success.

**Inclusive fitness** captures this fully: it is an individual's direct reproductive success _plus_ the indirect success achieved by helping relatives reproduce, weighted by relatedness. A worker bee that never reproduces herself can still have very high inclusive fitness if her efforts raise many highly related sisters.

Beyond kin, **reciprocal altruism** (Robert Trivers) explains cooperation between _unrelated_ individuals: an act of help is favored if there is a reasonable chance it will be returned later, and if the cost to the giver is lower than the benefit to the receiver. The classic example is vampire bats, which regurgitate blood to feed roost-mates who failed to feed that night — even unrelated ones — because the favor is reliably reciprocated over the long run.

> **Common pitfall:** treating "altruism" as requiring conscious kindness or intention. In behavioral biology it is defined purely by its fitness consequences — a cost to the actor and a benefit to the recipient — regardless of whether any deliberate decision-making is involved at all.

## Mating Systems and Sexual Selection

**Mating systems** describe the patterns of mating within a population, shaped largely by the distribution of resources and each sex's **parental investment** — the time and resources a parent commits to each offspring. **Monogamy** (one male, one female) is common when offspring need substantial care from both parents. **Polygyny** (one male, multiple females) arises when males can monopolize resources or mates directly. **Polyandry** (one female, multiple males) is rarer and often comes with reversed parental roles. **Promiscuity** involves multiple partners for both sexes with no lasting bonds.

Parental investment theory (Robert Trivers) predicts that the sex investing more per offspring will be more **selective (choosy)** about mates, since a poor choice costs that sex more; the less-investing sex instead competes more intensely for mating access. This asymmetry drives **sexual selection** (Darwin), which operates through two mechanisms: **intra-sexual selection** (competition within one sex, usually male-male, for mating access) and **inter-sexual selection** (mate choice exercised by the other sex, usually female choice among displaying males).

| System | Pattern |
|---|---|
| Monogamy | One male, one female |
| Polygyny | One male, multiple females |
| Polyandry | One female, multiple males |
| Promiscuity | Multiple partners, no stable bonds |

> **Common pitfall:** assuming the higher-investing sex is always female. Investment, not sex per se, is what predicts choosiness — in species like seahorses and jacanas, where males carry the eggs or the primary parenting burden, it is the males who become the choosier, more selective sex.

## Parental Care and Conflict

**Parental care** is any behavior by a parent that raises the fitness of its offspring, typically at some cost to the parent's ability to invest elsewhere. Its forms include **provisioning** (feeding), **protection** from predators or rivals, and **thermoregulation** (keeping offspring at a viable temperature). How much a parent invests is shaped in part by **paternity certainty**: males are typically less certain than females that a given offspring is genetically theirs, especially in species with internal fertilization, which historically correlates with lower average male investment.

Robert Trivers' theory of **Parent-Offspring Conflict** explains why parents and their own offspring do not always agree on how much investment is "enough." A parent is related to each of its offspring by $r = 0.5$, and equally so to all of them — so investing further in one offspring has a real, if partial, cost in terms of resources available for future offspring (each also related by $r = 0.5$). An offspring, by contrast, is related to itself by $r = 1.0$ but only $r = 0.5$ to a future sibling. Because that offspring values its own gain twice as much as it discounts the sibling's loss, it is selected to keep demanding investment for longer than the parent is selected to keep providing it — the evolutionary root of weaning conflict, tantrums, and sibling rivalry.

| Perspective | Relatedness to self | Relatedness to future sibling |
|---|---|---|
| The offspring | 1.0 | 0.5 |
| The parent | 0.5 (to this offspring) | 0.5 (to the future sibling too) |

> **Common pitfall:** framing parent-offspring conflict as one side being "selfish" and the other "generous." Both sides are simply favoring the reproductive strategy that maximizes their OWN inclusive fitness — the conflict is a mathematical consequence of relatedness asymmetry, not a moral failing on either part.

## Predator-Prey Interactions

Predator-prey interactions drive a continuous evolutionary **arms race**: any anti-predator adaptation that improves prey survival favors predators able to counter it, which in turn favors further prey adaptation. **Crypsis** is camouflage that helps an organism avoid detection altogether. **Aposematism** is the opposite strategy — bright warning coloration advertising that an organism is toxic or unpalatable, so predators learn to avoid it rather than avoid being seen.

**Mimicry** exploits the signals predators have learned. In **Batesian mimicry**, a harmless species evolves to resemble a dangerous or unpalatable one, borrowing its reputation without paying its cost — a harmless hoverfly resembling a stinging wasp, for instance. In **Müllerian mimicry**, multiple genuinely dangerous species converge on a similar warning appearance, sharing the cost of "teaching" predators to avoid that pattern, since every individual that gets eaten while teaching the lesson benefits the whole mimicry ring.

The **Red Queen Hypothesis** (Leigh Van Valen) captures why this arms race never truly ends: because predators and prey are each adapting to the other's adaptations, a species must keep evolving merely to maintain its _relative_ fitness — running, in effect, just to stay in place. Group living adds further protection: the **dilution effect** means an individual's own risk of predation falls as group size rises, and the **many-eyes effect** means a larger group detects approaching predators sooner through combined vigilance.

> **Common pitfall:** assuming Batesian and Müllerian mimicry work the same way. They do not: Batesian mimicry involves a genuinely _harmless_ species free-riding on a dangerous one's reputation, while Müllerian mimicry involves multiple genuinely _dangerous_ species converging for mutual benefit — no free-riding involved.

## Cooperation and Conflict

**Evolutionary game theory** analyzes social interactions in which the payoff of one individual's strategy depends on which strategies others in the population adopt. The **Hawk-Dove game** models conflict resolution over a shared resource: "Hawks" always escalate and fight, risking injury, while "Doves" display but retreat rather than fight. Neither pure strategy is stable on its own — an all-Dove population is easily invaded by a Hawk, and an all-Hawk population pays such heavy injury costs that Doves can do better — so the population typically settles at a mixed **Evolutionarily Stable Strategy (ESS)**: a strategy that, once common, cannot be beaten by any rare alternative. In some species, a conditional strategy called **Bourgeois** — fight if you are the resource's owner, retreat if you are the intruder — emerges as the ESS instead.

The **Prisoner's Dilemma** is the classic model for cooperation between non-relatives. Although mutual cooperation yields the best _collective_ payoff, each individual is tempted to **defect** (exploit a cooperating partner) for a higher personal payoff — and if both defect, both end up worse off than if both had cooperated. Robert Axelrod's computer tournaments, pitting many strategies against each other in repeated Prisoner's Dilemma games, found that a remarkably simple strategy — **Tit-for-Tat** (cooperate first, then simply mirror the partner's previous move) — outperformed far more complex rivals, by being nice, retaliatory, forgiving, and easy to read.

> **Common pitfall:** assuming the "winning" strategy in a repeated Prisoner's Dilemma must be a cunning, deceptive one. Axelrod's tournaments found the opposite: transparent, reciprocal strategies like Tit-for-Tat that start cooperatively tend to outperform strategies built around exploiting others.
