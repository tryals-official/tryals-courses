# Unit 4: Society and Health

## The Biopsychosocial Model

For much of the 20th century, medicine operated under the **biomedical model**: illness was explained almost entirely by biological causes — pathogens, injuries, genetic malfunctions — with psychological and social factors treated as, at best, secondary. In 1977, physician **George Engel** proposed the **biopsychosocial model** as a direct challenge to this narrow view, arguing that health and illness emerge from the interplay of three domains: **biological** factors (genetics, pathogens, physiology), **psychological** factors (beliefs, emotions, coping skills, behavior), and **social** factors (socioeconomic status, culture, relationships, access to care).

Under the biopsychosocial view, the SAME diagnosis can play out very differently in two people depending on their psychological resources and social context — a chronic pain condition, for instance, is shaped not just by tissue damage but by a patient's coping style, their beliefs about pain, and whether they have supportive relationships and stable income.

| Domain | Examples |
|---|---|
| Biological | Genetics, pathogens, physiological state |
| Psychological | Beliefs, emotions, coping skills, behavior |
| Social | Socioeconomic status, culture, relationships, access to care |

This shift also reframed the GOAL of care. The **World Health Organization (WHO)** defines health not merely as the absence of disease, but as "a state of complete physical, mental, and social **well-being**" — an explicitly positive standard that only makes sense within a biopsychosocial framework, since well-being clearly depends on more than biology alone.

> **Common pitfall:** treating the biopsychosocial model as simply "biology plus a little bit of psychology thrown in." The model insists the three domains genuinely INTERACT — a psychological state can alter biological processes (e.g. chronic stress raising cortisol), and social conditions can shape psychological states (e.g. isolation raising depression risk) — not that they merely sit side by side.

## Stress and Coping

**Stress** is the process by which people perceive and respond to environmental demands, or **stressors**, that they appraise as threatening or challenging. Physiologist **Hans Selye** described the body's physiological response to prolonged stress as the **General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS)**, unfolding in three stages: **Alarm** (an immediate burst of arousal, mobilizing the fight-or-flight response), **Resistance** (continued elevated arousal as the body adapts to an ongoing demand), and **Exhaustion** (physiological resources become depleted if the stressor persists too long, raising vulnerability to illness). Chronic activation of the **HPA axis** during the resistance stage keeps cortisol elevated, which over time can suppress immune function and raise the risk of cardiovascular disease.

Whether an event counts as "stressful" at all depends heavily on interpretation. **Richard Lazarus** and **Susan Folkman**'s transactional model describes two rounds of **appraisal**: **primary appraisal** asks "is this a threat, a challenge, or irrelevant?" and **secondary appraisal** asks "do I have the resources to cope with it?" The same event — a surprise presentation, say — can feel like a manageable challenge to one person and an overwhelming threat to another, purely based on this appraisal process.

| GAS stage | What happens |
|---|---|
| Alarm | Immediate fight-or-flight arousal |
| Resistance | Sustained arousal while adapting |
| Exhaustion | Resources depleted; illness risk rises |

**Coping** describes the cognitive and behavioral efforts used to manage stress once it is appraised. **Problem-focused coping** directly addresses the stressor to change the situation itself (e.g. making a study plan before an exam); **emotion-focused coping** instead manages the emotional response to a stressor that cannot easily be changed (e.g. seeking comfort, reframing, or distraction). Neither style is universally superior — problem-focused coping tends to work best for controllable stressors, and emotion-focused coping tends to work best for uncontrollable ones.

> **Common pitfall:** assuming stress is purely a property of the event itself. In the Lazarus-Folkman model, stress is a TRANSACTION between the person and the event — the same objective event can be appraised very differently depending on the individual's resources and interpretation.

$$\text{Alarm} \to \text{Resistance} \to \text{Exhaustion}$$

## Social Support

**Social support** refers to the comfort and assistance available from other people, particularly within a person's social network. Researchers commonly distinguish several types: **emotional support** (expressions of empathy, love, and trust), **instrumental support** (tangible aid, such as financial help or assistance with tasks), and **informational support** (advice, guidance, or useful information). A person's **social network** is the actual set of relationships they maintain, distinct from their **perceived support** — the subjective belief that help would be available if needed, which often matters more for well-being than the objective size of the network.

Two competing models describe HOW social support protects health. The **direct effects model** holds that social support benefits health regardless of stress level, by providing a steady sense of belonging and regular positive experiences. The **buffering model** (or buffering hypothesis) holds that support's benefit shows up specifically UNDER high stress, cushioning its impact — under this model, support matters far less when life is already calm.

| Support type | Example |
|---|---|
| Emotional | A friend listening and expressing empathy |
| Instrumental | A neighbor driving you to a medical appointment |
| Informational | A doctor explaining treatment options clearly |

| Model | Core claim |
|---|---|
| Direct effects | Support helps regardless of stress level |
| Buffering | Support helps specifically under high stress |

Evidence broadly supports both models operating simultaneously, for different aspects of health: perceived support seems to have a fairly steady direct benefit on well-being, while the buffering effect shows up most clearly in how support cushions the physiological impact of major stressors.

> **Common pitfall:** assuming a large social network automatically means strong social support. Network SIZE and PERCEIVED support are distinct — a person with many acquaintances but no one they trust can feel more unsupported than a person with just one or two close, reliable relationships.

## Health Belief Models

The **Health Belief Model (HBM)**, developed by Irwin Rosenstock, predicts that a person's decision to adopt a health behavior depends on their perception across several dimensions: **perceived susceptibility** (how at-risk they feel), **perceived severity** (how serious the consequences would be), **perceived benefits** (how much the action would actually help), and **perceived barriers** (the costs or obstacles involved). Two further components round out the model: **self-efficacy** (confidence in one's own ability to carry out the behavior) and **cues to action** (external triggers, like a symptom, a doctor's reminder, or a media campaign, that prompt the decision).

A related but distinct framework, Icek Ajzen's **Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB)**, centers on **intention** as the direct predictor of behavior. Intention itself is shaped by three factors: **attitude toward the behavior** (is this a good idea?), **subjective norms** (what do people I care about think I should do?), and **perceived behavioral control** (how much control do I actually have over doing this?).

| Model | Key components |
|---|---|
| Health Belief Model | Susceptibility, severity, benefits, barriers, self-efficacy, cues to action |
| Theory of Planned Behavior | Attitude, subjective norms, perceived behavioral control → intention → behavior |

Both models converge on a similar practical lesson: simply telling someone a health behavior is beneficial rarely changes behavior on its own. Effective interventions typically also need to raise perceived susceptibility or severity, lower perceived barriers, build self-efficacy, or shift subjective norms — addressing the specific belief that is actually holding someone back.

> **Common pitfall:** assuming perceived severity alone drives health behavior. A person can believe a disease is extremely severe and still not act, if they perceive themselves as not personally susceptible, or if perceived barriers (cost, time, discomfort) feel too high — the models require weighing several beliefs together, not just one.

## Social Inequality and Health

The **social gradient in health** describes a remarkably consistent finding: health outcomes and life expectancy improve in a continuous, step-wise fashion at every rung of the socioeconomic ladder, not just at the boundary between "poor" and "not poor." **Socioeconomic status (SES)** is typically measured through a combination of **income**, **education**, and **occupational prestige**. The famous **Whitehall Studies** of British civil servants (led by Michael Marmot) demonstrated this gradient starkly: even though every participant had guaranteed employment and access to Britain's National Health Service, those in lower employment grades had substantially worse health and higher mortality than those in higher grades — showing the gradient cannot be explained by access to healthcare alone.

Two broad pathways are thought to link SES to health. **Material deprivation** captures the direct lack of physical resources — worse housing, less nutritious food, more exposure to environmental hazards. **Psychosocial stress** captures a more indirect pathway: lower-SES individuals tend to face more chronic daily stressors and major life events, with fewer resources to buffer or cope with them, driving cumulative wear on the body known as **allostatic load** — a concept developed by Bruce McEwen describing the physiological cost of chronically managing stress (elevated cortisol, blood pressure, inflammation) that accumulates over time.

| Pathway | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Material deprivation | Fewer physical resources: housing, nutrition, safety |
| Psychosocial stress | More chronic stressors, less buffering capacity, higher allostatic load |

> **Common pitfall:** assuming the social gradient is really just about the very poorest versus everyone else. The Whitehall data show a continuous gradient across EVERY level of the hierarchy — even comfortably middle-tier employees have worse health than those one level above them — not a simple poor/non-poor divide.

A four-step staircase: four grey steps of equal height, unlabelled; each step gains a life-expectancy bar rising to a different height, tallest at the top step.

$$\text{Health} \propto \text{SES, step by step}$$

## Chronic Illness and Quality of Life

**Chronic illnesses** — conditions like diabetes, heart disease, or chronic pain that progress slowly and require ongoing management — differ fundamentally from acute illness in the demands they place on a person's life. Sociologist Michael Bury described the onset of a serious chronic illness as **biographical disruption**: the illness forces a person to re-evaluate their past, present, and future self, disrupting the "taken-for-granted" assumptions most people hold about their body and their life trajectory. Adapting successfully involves not just managing physical symptoms, but navigating changes to **identity** and **social roles** — a formerly independent person may need to renegotiate their role within their family, workplace, or friendships.

Because chronic illness touches so many dimensions of life, researchers assess its impact using **Health-Related Quality of Life (HRQoL)**: a multidimensional construct capturing a patient's own subjective sense of how their health status affects their physical, mental, and social functioning. HRQoL measures matter because two patients with an identical clinical diagnosis and identical lab results can have very different day-to-day quality of life, depending on symptom severity, coping resources, and social context — exactly the point the biopsychosocial model makes more broadly.

| Concept | Focus |
|---|---|
| Biographical disruption | The illness forces a re-evaluation of one's life story |
| Identity change | Renegotiating who one is, given new limitations |
| Social role change | Renegotiating one's place in family, work, relationships |
| HRQoL | A multidimensional, subjective measure of impact on functioning |

Evaluating treatments purely by clinical or survival metrics can miss what matters most to patients living with the condition day to day — which is exactly why HRQoL has become a standard companion measure alongside traditional clinical outcomes in chronic disease research.

> **Common pitfall:** assuming HRQoL is just a fancy term for "how sick someone is" clinically. It specifically captures the patient's own SUBJECTIVE experience of functioning — which can diverge substantially from an objective clinical severity score.

## Patient-Provider Interaction

The relationship between a patient and their healthcare provider shapes outcomes as much as the treatment itself. Three broad models describe how decisions get made within that relationship. In the **paternalistic model**, the provider makes decisions on the patient's behalf, based on their own clinical judgment. In the **consumerist model**, the patient takes the leading role, treating the provider more like a technical service supplier. In the **collaborative (or mutual) model**, both parties share decision-making, combining the provider's clinical expertise with the patient's own values and preferences — the model increasingly favored in contemporary practice.

Effective communication requires exchanging both technical information and emotional support, and several well-documented barriers get in the way: **medical jargon** that a patient cannot follow, **"baby talk"** or excessive simplification that feels depersonalizing, and simple **time constraints** that leave little room for questions. These barriers correlate with lower patient satisfaction and worse understanding of a treatment plan.

| Model | Who decides |
|---|---|
| Paternalistic | The provider |
| Consumerist | The patient |
| Collaborative | Both, together |

**Adherence** (sometimes called compliance, or — in its more modern, collaborative framing — **concordance**) is the extent to which a patient's behavior matches medical advice. Rates of non-adherence are strikingly high: for long-term therapies treating chronic conditions, the World Health Organization estimates that roughly **half** of patients do not take treatment as prescribed. Non-adherence is rarely simple defiance — it often reflects unaddressed barriers (cost, side effects, complexity, or a mismatch between the plan and the patient's actual life) that better communication could have surfaced.

> **Common pitfall:** treating low adherence as a purely individual failing of "difficult" or "unmotivated" patients. Most non-adherence research instead points toward SYSTEM and COMMUNICATION factors — cost, complexity, side effects, unclear instructions — as the dominant drivers, which is exactly why the collaborative model, built around understanding the patient's actual circumstances, tends to improve adherence more than paternalistic instruction alone.

## Life Cycle and Health

Health-related challenges and opportunities shift systematically across the lifespan. In **childhood**, health behaviors and beliefs are first established, largely through family modeling — patterns around diet, activity, and risk-taking laid down early often persist into adulthood. **Adolescence** brings a distinctive spike in health risk-taking (substance use, reckless driving, unprotected sex), partly explained by a developmental mismatch: the brain's reward-seeking systems mature faster than its self-regulatory control systems, encouraging bold exploration before judgment has fully caught up. **Adulthood** is when most chronic diseases (cardiovascular disease, diabetes, many cancers) first emerge or become clinically apparent, often as the cumulative result of decades of earlier behavior and exposure.

**Aging** brings its own health psychology. Rather than viewing aging purely as decline, John Rowe and Robert Kahn's **successful aging** model identifies three components that, together, characterize aging well: a **low probability of disease and disability**, **high cognitive and physical functioning**, and **active engagement with life** (social relationships, productive activity). This model deliberately shifted research attention away from a purely deficit-based view of old age.

| Life stage | Central health issue |
|---|---|
| Childhood | Health behaviors and beliefs are established |
| Adolescence | Risk-taking spikes; regulation lags reward-seeking |
| Adulthood | Chronic disease commonly first emerges |
| Older age | Successful aging: low disease risk, high function, engagement |

A recurring theme across the whole lifespan is that health behavior is cumulative: exposures and habits at one stage shape risk at later stages, which is exactly why health psychologists increasingly emphasize prevention starting early, rather than only intervening once disease has already emerged.

> **Common pitfall:** treating adolescent risk-taking as simple recklessness or poor character. The maturational mismatch between an early-maturing reward system and a later-maturing control system is a normal developmental pattern seen across populations, not a personal failing unique to a "bad" individual.

## Prevention and Health Promotion

Public health distinguishes three levels of **prevention**, defined by WHEN they intervene relative to a disease's natural course. **Primary prevention** acts before disease onset, aiming to stop it from occurring at all — vaccination, health education, and safety regulations are classic examples. **Secondary prevention** acts once a disease process may have already begun but before it becomes symptomatic or severe, through early detection — cancer screening programs are the textbook case, catching disease at a more treatable stage. **Tertiary prevention** acts after a disease is already established, aiming to manage it, limit complications, and support functioning — cardiac rehabilitation after a heart attack is a typical example.

| Level | Timing | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Primary | Before disease occurs | Vaccination |
| Secondary | Early, before symptoms worsen | Cancer screening |
| Tertiary | After disease is established | Cardiac rehabilitation |

Beyond treating prevention as an individual medical matter, the World Health Organization's 1986 **Ottawa Charter for Health Promotion** reframed health promotion as a broader societal project, emphasizing action across multiple levels: building healthy public policy, creating supportive environments, strengthening community action, developing personal skills, and reorienting health services toward promotion rather than treatment alone. This reflects the same logic as the biopsychosocial model applied at a population scale — health is produced by social and environmental conditions, not simply delivered by individual medical encounters.

> **Common pitfall:** treating "prevention" as a single undifferentiated idea. The THREE levels intervene at genuinely different points in a disease's course and call for different strategies — confusing early screening (secondary) with stopping disease before it starts (primary) leads to very different, and non-interchangeable, public health policies.

## Community Health Psychology

**Community health psychology** widens the lens beyond the individual patient to the community and societal conditions that shape health at a population level. The **social-ecological model**, adapted from Urie Bronfenbrenner's broader developmental framework, arranges influences on health as nested levels: the **individual** (knowledge, attitudes, skills), the **interpersonal** (family, peers, social networks), the **community** (organizations, norms, local institutions), and the **societal** (policy, economic conditions, culture). A health intervention aimed only at the individual level — say, a leaflet about healthy eating — often struggles against interpersonal, community, and societal forces (food deserts, advertising, cultural norms) working in the opposite direction.

**Community-based participatory research (CBPR)** responds to this by treating community members as genuine partners in designing and conducting research, not merely as subjects to be studied — recognizing that people who live with a health issue daily often hold crucial, otherwise inaccessible knowledge about what will actually work in their context. This approach is closely linked to **empowerment**-based interventions, which aim to build a community's own capacity to identify and address its health needs, rather than simply delivering a predetermined program from outside.

| Ecological level | Example influence |
|---|---|
| Individual | Personal health knowledge and skills |
| Interpersonal | Family and peer support or pressure |
| Community | Local institutions, norms, available resources |
| Societal | Policy, economic conditions, culture |

Applied to health disparities specifically, this framework helps explain why disparities are so persistent: they are produced and maintained across MULTIPLE nested levels simultaneously, so interventions focused only on individual behavior change, without addressing community and societal conditions, tend to have limited and short-lived impact.

> **Common pitfall:** treating community-level health problems as simply the sum of many individual bad choices. The social-ecological model's whole point is that community and societal conditions actively SHAPE individual choices — the same "individual choice" looks very different when it is made inside a supportive environment versus a constrained, unsupportive one.

Four concentric rings: a bare center dot (the individual); rings expand outward one at a time — interpersonal, community, societal — each labelled as it appears.

$$\text{Individual} \to \text{Interpersonal} \to \text{Community} \to \text{Societal}$$
