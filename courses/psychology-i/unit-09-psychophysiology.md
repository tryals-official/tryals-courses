# Unit 9: Psychophysiology

## The Nervous System

The **nervous system** is the body's rapid electrochemical communication network. Its first great division is anatomical. The **central nervous system (CNS)** is the brain and the spinal cord — the processing core where information is integrated and decisions are made. The **peripheral nervous system (PNS)** is every nerve outside that core, the cabling that carries signals to and from the rest of the body.

The PNS then splits by function. The **somatic nervous system** carries sensory information inward and commands to the _skeletal_ muscles outward — the system of voluntary movement and conscious sensation. The **autonomic nervous system (ANS)** governs the involuntary machinery: heart rate, digestion, breathing, glandular secretion.

The autonomic system itself has two opposing branches that keep the body in balance:

| Branch | Nickname | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| Sympathetic | "Fight or flight" | Mobilizes energy: speeds heart, dilates pupils, halts digestion |
| Parasympathetic | "Rest and digest" | Conserves energy: slows heart, constricts pupils, resumes digestion |

These two branches are _antagonistic_: the sympathetic branch throws the body into an aroused, energy-spending state during threat, and the parasympathetic branch returns it to a calm, energy-conserving baseline afterward. Health depends on the smooth back-and-forth between them.

> **Common pitfall:** assuming the sympathetic and parasympathetic branches take turns being "on" and "off." In reality both are active at all times; what changes is the _balance_ between them. Arousal is a shift in that balance toward the sympathetic side, not the flipping of a single switch.

A seesaw balance beam with "sympathetic" on one side and "parasympathetic" on the other, both always weighted (never zero). A threat event tips the beam toward sympathetic.

$$\text{arousal} = \text{sympathetic} - \text{parasympathetic tone}$$

## The Neuron and Action Potentials

The **neuron** is the basic signalling cell of the nervous system. Three parts do the work. The **dendrites** are branching fibers that _receive_ incoming signals from other neurons. The **soma** (cell body) integrates those signals and houses the nucleus. The **axon** is the long fiber that _carries_ the outgoing signal away to the next cell; many axons are wrapped in a fatty **myelin sheath** whose gaps (nodes of Ranvier) let the signal leap forward far faster.

At rest, the neuron holds a **resting potential** of about $-70$ millivolts: the inside is negatively charged relative to the outside, a battery waiting to discharge. When incoming signals push the membrane past a **threshold**, voltage-gated channels snap open and positive ions rush in — the membrane briefly reverses to positive. This spike is the **action potential**, and it sweeps down the axon.

The action potential obeys the **all-or-none principle**: it either fires completely or not at all. A stronger stimulus does _not_ produce a bigger spike — every action potential in a given neuron is the same size. What a strong stimulus changes is the _frequency_ of firing (spikes per second), not their amplitude. After firing, a brief **refractory period** must pass before the neuron can fire again.

> **Common pitfall:** believing a more intense stimulus makes a "bigger" action potential. It cannot. Because of the all-or-none principle, intensity is coded by how OFTEN the neuron fires, not by how large each spike is.

## Synaptic Transmission

Neurons rarely touch. Between the axon terminal of one neuron and the dendrite of the next lies a microscopic gap, the **synapse** (or synaptic cleft). The electrical action potential cannot jump it directly; instead it is converted into a _chemical_ message.

When the action potential reaches the axon terminal, it triggers tiny sacs called **vesicles** to release **neurotransmitters** into the synaptic cleft. These chemical messengers drift across and bind to **receptor** sites on the receiving neuron, like a key fitting a lock. Binding either _excites_ the receiving neuron (nudging it toward firing) or _inhibits_ it (nudging it away). Whether the neuron fires depends on the summed balance of all excitatory and inhibitory signals it receives.

The message must then be cleared so the synapse can reset. The main mechanism is **reuptake**: the sending neuron reabsorbs the leftover neurotransmitter for reuse. Drugs exploit exactly this machinery. An **agonist** mimics or boosts a neurotransmitter's effect; an **antagonist** blocks it. Many antidepressants, for example, block the reuptake of serotonin, leaving more of it active in the synapse.

> **Common pitfall:** picturing the electrical impulse as jumping the synapse itself. It does not. At the synapse the signal switches from electrical to _chemical_ — neurotransmitters carry it across — and only then becomes electrical again in the next neuron.

A: an impulse arrives at the terminal; vesicles fuse and release dots of neurotransmitter into the cleft; dots bind receptors on the far membrane.

$$\text{net signal} = \text{excitatory} - \text{inhibitory input}$$

## The Brainstem and Cerebellum

Sitting where the spinal cord meets the brain are its oldest, most essential structures — the machinery of survival that runs without a thought.

The **brainstem** is the stalk that keeps you alive. Its **medulla** controls the automatic vital functions: heartbeat, breathing, blood pressure. Damage here is often fatal. Just above, the **pons** helps coordinate movement and relays signals between the cerebrum and cerebellum, and helps regulate sleep and arousal. Threaded through the brainstem is the **reticular formation**, a net of neurons that governs arousal and alertness — it filters the flood of incoming stimuli and decides what wakes you up.

Behind the brainstem sits the **cerebellum** ("little brain"), a densely folded structure that coordinates **voluntary movement, balance, and posture**, and fine-tunes the timing of skilled motor sequences. It is why a practiced motion — signing your name, riding a bike — becomes smooth and automatic. Damage to the cerebellum does not paralyze, but it makes movement clumsy, jerky, and poorly timed.

> **Common pitfall:** confusing the cerebellum with the cerebrum. The **cerebrum** is the large, wrinkled outer brain of higher thought; the **cerebellum** ("little brain") is the small structure tucked _behind and below_ it that coordinates movement and balance. Similar names, very different jobs.

## The Limbic System

Ringing the top of the brainstem is the **limbic system**, a set of structures that generate emotion, drive, and the formation of memory. Four structures do the heavy lifting.

The **amygdala** is the brain's threat detector and emotional core, central to _fear_ and aggression. It flags danger before the conscious mind catches up, which is why you flinch at a shape on the path before recognizing it as a stick, not a snake. The **hippocampus** is essential for forming new **long-term memories**; damage to it leaves old memories intact but blocks the making of new ones. The **hypothalamus** is a tiny regulator that governs the basic drives — hunger, thirst, body temperature, and sex — and controls the pituitary gland, linking the nervous and hormonal systems. The **thalamus** is the brain's sensory relay station: nearly all sensory information (except smell) passes through it on the way to the cortex.

| Structure | Core role |
|---|---|
| Amygdala | Fear, threat detection, emotion |
| Hippocampus | Forming new long-term memories |
| Hypothalamus | Basic drives; controls the pituitary |
| Thalamus | Sensory relay station to the cortex |

> **Common pitfall:** confusing the hippocampus and the hypothalamus because their names sound alike. The **hippo_campus_** makes new _memories_; the **hypo_thalamus_** regulates _drives_ (hunger, thirst, temperature) and hormones. Very different jobs.

## The Cerebral Cortex

The **cerebral cortex** is the deeply folded outer layer of the cerebrum — the wrinkled grey surface where perception, thought, language, and voluntary action come together. Its folds pack an enormous surface area into the skull. The cortex is divided into four **lobes**, each with characteristic jobs.

| Lobe | Location | Core functions |
|---|---|---|
| Frontal | Front | Planning, judgment, voluntary movement, personality |
| Parietal | Top-rear | Touch, body sense, spatial processing |
| Temporal | Sides | Hearing, language comprehension, memory |
| Occipital | Rear | Vision |

Two strips straddle the boundary between the frontal and parietal lobes. The **motor cortex** (rear of the frontal lobe) sends commands for voluntary movement; the **somatosensory cortex** (front of the parietal lobe) receives touch and body-position signals. Both are _mapped_: adjacent body parts are represented by adjacent cortical regions, and body parts with fine control or high sensitivity (hands, lips) get disproportionately large areas.

Vast stretches of cortex are **association areas** — not tied to any single sense or muscle, but integrating information across them to produce planning, language, and abstract thought.

> **Common pitfall:** assuming each lobe works in isolation. Complex behavior almost always draws on _multiple_ lobes and the association areas that link them — reading, for instance, engages occipital (vision), temporal (language), and frontal (attention) regions at once.

## Hemispheric Lateralization

The cerebrum is split into two **hemispheres**, left and right, joined by a thick band of some 200 million nerve fibers called the **corpus callosum**. Each hemisphere controls the _opposite_ side of the body — the left hemisphere moves the right hand, and vice versa. This crossing is called **contralateral** control.

The hemispheres are also somewhat **lateralized** — specialized for different functions. In most people, the **left hemisphere** dominates _language_: two classic language areas, **Broca's area** (speech production) and **Wernicke's area** (language comprehension), sit in the left hemisphere, and damage to them produces distinct aphasias. The **right hemisphere** tends to specialize in spatial processing, face recognition, and aspects of emotional and musical perception.

The most dramatic evidence comes from **split-brain** patients, whose corpus callosum was surgically cut (historically to control severe epilepsy). With the bridge severed, the two hemispheres can no longer share information directly. An object shown only to the right hemisphere (via the left visual field) can be picked out by the left hand but _cannot be named_, because the language-dominant left hemisphere never received it.

> **Common pitfall:** the pop-psychology myth of "left-brained" (logical) versus "right-brained" (creative) _people_. Lateralization means certain _functions_ lean to one side — it does not mean individuals are dominated by one hemisphere. Both hemispheres are active in virtually everything a healthy brain does.

A top-down head: left and right visual fields cross to the opposite hemispheres; the corpus callosum bridge is drawn, then a scissors cut animates severing it.

$$\text{naming} \Rightarrow \text{signal reaches the left hemisphere}$$

## Brain Imaging Techniques

For most of history the working brain was a black box. Modern **imaging techniques** opened it, and they divide into two families: those that reveal _structure_ (anatomy) and those that reveal _function_ (activity).

| Technique | Measures | Reveals |
|---|---|---|
| EEG | Electrical activity via scalp electrodes | Function; excellent time resolution |
| CT | X-rays combined into a cross-section | Structure |
| MRI | Magnetic fields and radio waves | Structure; high detail |
| fMRI | Blood-oxygen changes over time | Function; good spatial resolution |
| PET | A radioactive tracer's uptake | Function (e.g. glucose use) |

The key trade-off is between **temporal** and **spatial** resolution. **EEG** records electrical activity through scalp electrodes with millisecond timing — it tells you _when_ neurons fire with great precision, but is poor at pinpointing _where_. **fMRI** tracks blood-oxygen changes (the BOLD signal) and locates activity to within millimeters — it tells you _where_ activity is with great precision, but lags by a second or more because it measures blood flow, not the firing itself. **CT** and **MRI** show structure (MRI in far finer detail than CT), while **PET** uses a radioactive tracer to reveal metabolic activity such as glucose consumption.

> **Common pitfall:** treating fMRI as a direct measure of neural firing. It measures **blood-oxygen changes** that _follow_ neural activity by a second or more — a proxy, not the electrical signal itself. That lag is exactly why fMRI has excellent spatial but poor temporal resolution.

## The Endocrine System

Alongside the fast electrical nervous system runs a slower chemical one: the **endocrine system**, a network of **glands** that secrete **hormones** directly into the bloodstream. Where a neuron's message is fast, targeted, and brief, a hormone's is slow, widespread, and long-lasting — carried by the blood to any cell with the right receptor.

The **pituitary gland**, dangling from the hypothalamus, is the "master gland": its hormones direct many other glands, and it is itself controlled by the hypothalamus — the point where the nervous and endocrine systems meet. Other key players include the **thyroid** (metabolism), the **adrenal glands** (which release adrenaline and cortisol during stress), and the **gonads** (sex hormones).

The stress response runs along the **HPA axis**: the **h**ypothalamus signals the **p**ituitary, which signals the **a**drenal glands to release **cortisol**, the body's main stress hormone. Cortisol mobilizes energy and, in turn, feeds back to shut the axis down — a self-regulating loop. Where the sympathetic nervous system produces a fast adrenaline surge in seconds, the HPA axis produces a slower, sustained cortisol response over minutes to hours.

> **Common pitfall:** thinking the endocrine system is just a slower copy of the nervous system. Its messages are not only slower but _broader and longer-lasting_ — a single hormone released into the blood can affect many organs at once, for minutes, hours, or even years, in ways a brief targeted neural signal cannot.

## Neuroplasticity

For decades the adult brain was thought to be fixed. It is not. **Neuroplasticity** is the brain's lifelong ability to change its structure and function in response to experience, learning, and injury. Several mechanisms drive it.

At the level of connections, learning strengthens the synapses that fire together — a process called **long-term potentiation (LTP)**, often summarized as "cells that fire together, wire together." Conversely, unused connections are removed through **synaptic pruning**, which is especially dramatic in childhood and adolescence: the brain sculpts an efficient network by discarding what it does not use.

At a larger scale, the cortex can **remap**: when one region is deprived of input (say, after the loss of a limb or of sight), neighboring regions can take over its territory, reassigning cortical real estate. And contrary to old dogma, the adult brain retains some **neurogenesis** — the birth of new neurons — notably in the hippocampus, supporting new learning and memory.

Plasticity is greatest in early development (the **critical** and **sensitive periods**), when experience most powerfully shapes the wiring, but it never fully stops. It is the biological basis of learning itself, and of recovery after brain injury.

> **Common pitfall:** believing neuroplasticity ends in childhood. Plasticity is _greatest_ early in life, but it continues throughout adulthood — the adult brain keeps forming, strengthening, and pruning connections (and even growing some new neurons) as long as it keeps learning.
