# Unit 7: Individual Differences

## Foundations of Intelligence

**Francis Galton**, a cousin of Charles Darwin, was among the first to attempt a scientific study of intelligence, in the late 19th century. Galton hypothesized that intelligence could be measured through basic sensory discrimination (reaction time, sensory acuity), reasoning that "smarter" nervous systems should process sensory information more efficiently — but these measures turned out to correlate poorly with more meaningful measures of mental ability, and his approach was largely abandoned.

The first genuinely practical intelligence test came from **Alfred Binet** and **Théodore Simon** in France in 1905, commissioned by the French government to identify schoolchildren who needed additional academic support. Binet introduced the concept of **mental age**: a child's test performance was compared to the average performance of children at different ages, yielding an age-equivalent score. A child who performed like a typical 8-year-old, regardless of their own actual age, had a mental age of 8.

**Lewis Terman** at Stanford University adapted and extended Binet's test for American use, producing the **Stanford-Binet** test, and introduced the now-familiar **intelligence quotient (IQ)** — originally a simple ratio:

$$IQ = \frac{\text{Mental age}}{\text{Chronological age}} \times 100$$

Under this ratio formula, a 10-year-old with a mental age of 12 would score $12/10 \times 100 = 120$. This ratio IQ formula worked reasonably well for children but broke down for adults, since mental age growth naturally plateaus while chronological age keeps climbing — a problem that later, more modern tests solved with a different approach (deviation IQ, covered later in this course).

| Figure | Contribution |
|---|---|
| Galton | First attempt at scientific measurement (sensory discrimination); largely unsuccessful |
| Binet and Simon | First practical test (1905); introduced mental age |
| Terman | Stanford-Binet; introduced the ratio IQ formula |

> **Common pitfall:** assuming Binet's original test was designed to rank children from smartest to least smart for its own sake. It was explicitly practical and applied — commissioned to IDENTIFY students needing extra academic support, not to create a general-purpose ranking of intelligence.

## Psychometric Theories of Intelligence

**Charles Spearman** used a statistical technique called **factor analysis** to examine why people who score well on one type of cognitive test tend to also score well on very different types of cognitive tests. He concluded that all cognitive tests are influenced by a single underlying **general intelligence factor**, or **"g"**, alongside narrower, test-specific factors ("s") unique to each particular task — his **two-factor theory**.

**Louis Thurstone** disagreed with the primacy of a single g factor, instead proposing **seven Primary Mental Abilities** he considered relatively independent of one another (including verbal comprehension, numerical ability, spatial visualization, and memory) — though later re-analyses found these seven abilities still correlated with each other more than chance alone would predict, suggesting SOME broad general factor persists even underneath Thurstone's multiple abilities.

**Raymond Cattell** (building on work with John Horn) proposed splitting general intelligence into two distinct components. **Fluid intelligence (Gf)** is the capacity to reason and solve novel problems, independent of any previously learned knowledge — it tends to peak in early adulthood and gradually decline with age. **Crystallized intelligence (Gc)** is accumulated knowledge and vocabulary built up through experience and education — it tends to remain stable, or even continue increasing, well into later life.

| Theory | Core claim |
|---|---|
| Spearman | A single general factor (g) underlies all cognitive tests |
| Thurstone | Seven relatively independent primary mental abilities |
| Cattell-Horn | Fluid intelligence (novel problem-solving) versus crystallized intelligence (accumulated knowledge) |

Modern psychometric models often synthesize these views hierarchically: a general factor (g) sits at the top, broad abilities like fluid and crystallized intelligence sit beneath it, and more specific abilities sit at the narrowest level — treating Spearman, Thurstone, and Cattell's ideas as complementary layers rather than flatly competing claims.

> **Common pitfall:** assuming fluid and crystallized intelligence age in the same direction. They diverge sharply: fluid intelligence (novel problem-solving) tends to DECLINE with age after early adulthood, while crystallized intelligence (accumulated knowledge) tends to REMAIN STABLE or even increase — which is why older adults often outperform younger adults on vocabulary and general-knowledge tasks while underperforming on novel, abstract reasoning puzzles.

## Cognitive and Multiple Intelligences

**Howard Gardner**'s theory of **Multiple Intelligences** challenges the idea that intelligence is a single, unified capacity captured by one IQ score. Gardner proposed roughly eight distinct, relatively independent intelligences, each tied to different underlying cognitive and even neurological systems: **linguistic** (language), **logical-mathematical** (numbers and logic), **spatial** (visualizing and manipulating space), **musical** (rhythm, pitch, and sound), **bodily-kinesthetic** (physical movement and coordination), **interpersonal** (understanding others), **intrapersonal** (understanding oneself), and **naturalistic** (recognizing and classifying patterns in the natural world). Gardner argued each is a genuinely distinct "intelligence," not merely a talent — though critics point out the theory has limited direct empirical support from controlled testing, and some of these categories overlap heavily with established personality or aptitude constructs rather than intelligence specifically.

**Robert Sternberg**'s **Triarchic Theory of Successful Intelligence** takes a different tack, proposing three distinct components rather than many separate intelligences: **analytical (componential) intelligence** (the traditional academic, problem-solving ability measured by conventional IQ tests), **creative (experiential) intelligence** (the ability to deal effectively with novel situations and generate original ideas), and **practical (contextual) intelligence** (often called "street smarts" — the ability to adapt to, shape, or select environments to achieve real-world goals). Sternberg argued that "successful intelligence" requires balancing all three, and that traditional IQ tests capture mainly the analytical component, systematically underselling creative and practical ability.

| Theory | Structure |
|---|---|
| Gardner (Multiple Intelligences) | ~8 distinct, independent intelligences |
| Sternberg (Triarchic Theory) | 3 components: analytical, creative, practical |

> **Common pitfall:** assuming these theories claim traditional IQ tests are worthless. Both Gardner and Sternberg instead argue traditional tests are too NARROW — capturing only some facets of a broader mental landscape (Gardner's linguistic/logical-mathematical intelligences, or Sternberg's analytical component) while missing others entirely.

## Measuring Intelligence

Modern intelligence testing largely uses the **Wechsler scales** — the **WAIS** (Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale) for adults and the **WISC** (Wechsler Intelligence Scale for Children) for children — which replaced Terman's old ratio IQ formula with **deviation IQ**: instead of comparing mental age to chronological age, a person's raw score is compared to the scores of a large, representative sample of SAME-AGE peers, converted onto a standardized scale with a **mean of 100** and a **standard deviation of 15**. This solves the ratio formula's breakdown for adults, since it no longer depends on mental age continuing to climb.

Deviation IQ scores form a **normal distribution** (a symmetric bell curve): about 68% of people score within one standard deviation of the mean (between 85 and 115), and about 95% score within two standard deviations (between 70 and 130). This standardized, peer-relative approach also makes IQ scores directly comparable across different age groups, since everyone is compared only to others their own age.

| IQ range | Approximate population share |
|---|---|
| 85-115 (±1 SD) | About 68% |
| 70-130 (±2 SD) | About 95% |

A striking pattern discovered by researcher **James Flynn**, now called the **Flynn effect**, is that raw intelligence test scores have risen substantially across successive generations throughout the 20th century — commonly cited at roughly 3 points per decade in many countries — even though genes change far too slowly to explain a shift this fast. Because IQ tests are periodically RE-NORMED to keep the population mean at 100, this generational rise is easy to miss unless you compare raw scores directly across test versions; likely explanations include improved nutrition, more years of schooling, and increased familiarity with abstract test-taking and reasoning tasks in modern life.

> **Common pitfall:** assuming a person's current deviation IQ score is meaningful in isolation, without reference to which NORM GROUP it was calculated against. Because tests are periodically re-normed to account for the Flynn effect, a raw test score from decades ago cannot be directly compared to a score from today without adjustment.

A bell curve: a bare symmetric curve peaking at 100; the ±1 SD band (85-115) shades in with a '68%' label; the ±2 SD band (70-130) shades in wider with a '95%' label, both bands visible together at the end.

$$\mu = 100, \sigma = 15$$

## Introduction to Personality and Traits

**Personality** refers to an individual's characteristic, relatively stable patterns of thinking, feeling, and behaving across time and situations. The **trait approach** to personality assumes these patterns can be described using a manageable set of underlying dimensions, or **traits** — relatively stable predispositions that influence behavior across many different situations, rather than one-off reactions to a specific moment.

**Gordon Allport**, an early and influential trait theorist, proposed a three-level hierarchy. **Cardinal traits** are rare, unusually dominant traits that define a person's entire life and identity (Allport suggested most people never develop one). **Central traits** are the 5 to 10 major traits that form the core building blocks of most people's personality (traits like "honest" or "anxious" that a close friend might use to describe you). **Secondary traits** are more situational, surface-level preferences and tendencies that appear only in particular contexts (a preference for a certain type of music, for instance).

| Trait level | Scope | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Cardinal | Defines an entire life (rare) | An all-consuming devotion to a single cause |
| Central | Core, general traits (5-10) | Honest, anxious, sociable |
| Secondary | Situational, surface-level | A preference for spicy food |

**Raymond Cattell** took a more data-driven approach, using factor analysis (the same statistical technique Spearman applied to intelligence) to reduce Allport's enormous initial list of trait-related words down to a more compact, empirically-derived set: his **16 Personality Factors (16PF)**, a foundational step toward later, even more compact trait models like the Big Five, covered next in this course.

> **Common pitfall:** treating "trait" as meaning a rigid, all-or-nothing category a person either has or lacks entirely. Modern trait theory treats traits as CONTINUOUS DIMENSIONS — a person is more or less high on a trait like sociability, not simply "sociable" or "not sociable" as a fixed label.

## The Big Five Trait Model

The **Big Five** (also known by the acronyms **OCEAN** or **CANOE**) is the most widely accepted and empirically supported trait model in contemporary personality psychology, having emerged from decades of factor-analytic research converging, across many independent research teams and languages, on the same five broad dimensions.

| Trait | High scorers tend to be... | Low scorers tend to be... |
|---|---|---|
| **O**penness | Curious, imaginative, open to new experiences | Conventional, practical, prefer routine |
| **C**onscientiousness | Organized, disciplined, dependable | Spontaneous, careless, flexible |
| **E**xtraversion | Outgoing, sociable, energized by others | Reserved, solitary, energized by quiet |
| **A**greeableness | Cooperative, trusting, compassionate | Competitive, skeptical, blunt |
| **N**euroticism | Anxious, emotionally reactive, moody | Calm, emotionally stable, resilient |

Each of the five is a **continuous spectrum**, not a category — everyone scores somewhere along each dimension, and a person's overall personality profile is the combination of where they fall on all five simultaneously, not a single label. Unlike many earlier trait models, the Big Five has been repeatedly replicated across very different cultures and languages, suggesting it captures something close to a genuinely fundamental structure of personality variation rather than an artifact of English-language trait words.

The Big Five also shows meaningful **predictive validity**: conscientiousness reliably predicts job performance and academic achievement across many studies; extraversion predicts social and leadership behaviors; neuroticism predicts vulnerability to anxiety and mood disorders; and the traits show substantial, though not perfect, stability across adulthood — people's relative standing on each trait tends to persist over years and even decades, even as everyone's absolute scores shift somewhat with age (conscientiousness and agreeableness tend to rise modestly, for instance, while neuroticism tends to decline modestly).

> **Common pitfall:** treating "extraversion" and "introversion" as a strict either/or category. In the Big Five model, extraversion is a CONTINUOUS dimension — most people fall somewhere in the middle, and "introvert" versus "extravert" is really shorthand for relatively lower versus higher scores on a single spectrum, not two distinct types of people.

A five-spoke radar chart: a bare pentagon outline with five unlabeled spokes; each spoke labels itself in turn (O-C-E-A-N) as a bar climbs partway up it; the five bar-tips connect into a single closed.

$$\text{Personality} = f(O, C, E, A, N)$$

## Psychoanalytic Perspectives

Applying psychoanalytic theory specifically to personality, Freud's structural model (id, ego, superego, covered earlier in this course) suggests that a person's characteristic personality reflects the particular, relatively stable balance struck among these three systems — someone with a chronically dominant superego might show a rigid, moralistic personality, while someone with a poorly restrained id might show impulsive, self-indulgent patterns.

Several of Freud's followers extended this psychoanalytic tradition in influential directions specifically relevant to personality trait theory. **Carl Jung** proposed the dimension of **introversion versus extraversion** — where introverts direct their psychic energy inward, toward their own thoughts and inner world, and extraverts direct it outward, toward other people and external activity. This distinction, first articulated by Jung, was later absorbed into mainstream trait psychology, appearing directly as one of the Big Five's five dimensions.

**Karen Horney**, a prominent critic of some of Freud's more culturally-bound assumptions (particularly regarding female psychology), proposed that early childhood insecurity produces a pervasive **basic anxiety**, which a person manages through one of three characteristic **neurotic trends**: moving TOWARD people (an excessive need for approval and affection), moving AGAINST people (an excessive need for control and dominance), or moving AWAY from people (an excessive need for independence and self-sufficiency, avoiding closeness altogether).

| Theorist | Contribution to personality theory |
|---|---|
| Jung | Introversion versus extraversion |
| Horney | Basic anxiety and three neurotic trends (toward, against, away) |

> **Common pitfall:** assuming the introversion-extraversion dimension originated with the modern Big Five model. It actually traces back to Jung's earlier psychoanalytic work — a clear example of how an idea from one theoretical tradition (psychoanalysis) can be absorbed and repurposed within a very different one (trait psychology).

## Humanistic and Social-Cognitive Views

The **humanistic perspective**, building on Rogers and Maslow discussed earlier in this course, treats **self-concept** — a person's organized set of beliefs about who they are — as central to personality. Closely related is **self-esteem**: the overall evaluative judgment a person makes about their own worth. Humanistic theorists argue that a mismatch between one's self-concept and one's actual experience (incongruence) is a major source of psychological distress, while a growth-supportive environment (offering unconditional positive regard) helps a person's self-concept develop in a healthy, integrated way.

The **social-cognitive perspective**, most closely associated with **Albert Bandura**, emphasizes how COGNITIVE beliefs about oneself and one's environment shape personality and behavior. **Self-efficacy** is a person's belief in their own capability to succeed at a SPECIFIC task or domain — distinct from global self-esteem, since a person can have high self-efficacy for public speaking but low self-efficacy for cooking, for instance. Self-efficacy strongly predicts persistence, effort, and resilience in the face of setbacks within that specific domain.

**Julian Rotter**'s closely related concept of **locus of control** describes a person's general belief about what determines life outcomes: an **internal locus of control** is the belief that outcomes result mainly from one's own actions and choices, while an **external locus of control** is the belief that outcomes result mainly from outside forces — luck, fate, or powerful others. An internal locus of control is generally, though not universally, associated with better mental health, higher achievement, and more proactive coping.

| Concept | Scope | Key theorist |
|---|---|---|
| Self-concept | Global beliefs about who one is | Rogers |
| Self-efficacy | Belief in capability for a SPECIFIC task | Bandura |
| Locus of control | General belief about what determines outcomes | Rotter |

> **Common pitfall:** treating self-efficacy and self-esteem as the same concept. Self-esteem is a GLOBAL evaluation of one's overall worth, while self-efficacy is SPECIFIC to a particular task or domain — a person can feel confident (high self-efficacy) about one skill while still holding low overall self-esteem, or vice versa.

## Personality Assessment

Personality psychologists rely on two broadly distinct families of assessment tools. **Objective tests** are standardized self-report questionnaires with fixed response options, scored consistently and compared against normative data from large samples. The **Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory (MMPI)** is the most widely used and researched example, originally developed to help diagnose psychological disorders; it notably includes built-in **validity scales**, designed to detect whether a respondent is faking good, faking bad, or answering inconsistently, which helps flag unreliable response patterns before they are used to guide any clinical conclusion.

**Projective tests**, by contrast, present ambiguous, unstructured stimuli and ask the respondent to interpret or respond to them freely, on the theoretical assumption that unconscious thoughts, conflicts, and motives will be "projected" onto the ambiguous material, revealing content a direct question might never surface. The **Rorschach Inkblot Test** asks respondents to describe what they see in a series of symmetric inkblots; the **Thematic Apperception Test (TAT)** asks respondents to construct a story about a series of ambiguous scenes.

| Test type | Example | Key strength | Key weakness |
|---|---|---|---|
| Objective | MMPI | Standardized scoring, strong norms, includes validity scales | Vulnerable to intentional distortion (though scales help detect it) |
| Projective | Rorschach, TAT | May reveal content beyond direct self-report | Notably lower reliability and validity than objective tests |

While projective tests remain influential and still see some clinical use, they have been extensively criticized for lower **reliability** (different scorers or occasions producing inconsistent results) and lower **validity** (weaker evidence that scores actually predict what they claim to) compared to objective tests — a scientific concern that has pushed contemporary clinical and research practice toward objective measures as the primary assessment tool, with projective tests used more cautiously, if at all, as a supplementary source of clinical impressions.

> **Common pitfall:** assuming projective tests are simply an older, outdated curiosity with no modern relevance at all. They remain in some clinical use and retain some theoretical appeal, but the scientific consensus is that their reliability and validity are considerably weaker than objective self-report inventories, which is why objective tests are far more heavily used in contemporary research and much clinical assessment.

## Nature vs. Nurture

The **nature versus nurture** debate asks how much of individual variation in traits like intelligence and personality comes from genetic inheritance ("nature") versus environmental experience ("nurture"). Modern behavioral genetics has largely moved past treating this as an either/or question, instead trying to estimate the RELATIVE contribution of each using **twin studies**: comparing **identical (monozygotic) twins**, who share essentially 100% of their genes, against **fraternal (dizygotic) twins**, who share on average 50% of their genes, like ordinary siblings. If identical twins resemble each other on a trait substantially MORE than fraternal twins do, that gap suggests a meaningful genetic contribution to the trait. Studies of identical twins raised APART in different environments add further evidence, since any similarity between them cannot be explained by a shared upbringing.

These studies yield an estimate called **heritability**: the proportion of variance in a trait, WITHIN A GIVEN POPULATION, that can be statistically attributed to genetic differences. For adult IQ, heritability is commonly estimated in the range of roughly 50-80%, with the estimate tending to RISE with age (sometimes called the Wilson effect) — plausibly because as people age, they increasingly select and shape environments that fit their genetic predispositions.

| Concept | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Monozygotic (identical) twins | Share ~100% of genes |
| Dizygotic (fraternal) twins | Share ~50% of genes, like ordinary siblings |
| Heritability | Proportion of trait VARIANCE, within a population, attributable to genetic differences |

Heritability is a widely misunderstood statistic, and two clarifications matter enormously. First, heritability describes variance WITHIN a specific population under specific conditions — it says nothing about any single INDIVIDUAL (a heritability of 70% does NOT mean 70% of one person's IQ comes from genes and 30% from environment). Second, heritability WITHIN a population says nothing about the CAUSE of average DIFFERENCES BETWEEN populations or groups, since those groups may differ systematically in environmental factors not captured in the original heritability estimate.

> **Common pitfall:** interpreting a heritability estimate (like 70% for IQ) as meaning "70% of my personal intelligence came from my genes." Heritability is a population-level statistic about the SOURCE OF VARIANCE across many people in a specific context, not a percentage breakdown that applies to any single individual's own intelligence.

Two twin silhouette pairs: an identical-twin pair connected by a thick accent bond line labelled with a high similarity score.

$$\text{Heritability} \approx 2 \times (r_{identical} - r_{fraternal})$$
