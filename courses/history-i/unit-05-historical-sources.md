# Unit 5: Historical Sources

## Primary vs Secondary Sources

Students learn "primary = from the time, secondary = written later" and then discover the definition leaks everywhere. The fix is to stop treating it as a property of the object.

**A source is primary or secondary _relative to a question_.**

Gibbon's _Decline and Fall_ (1776) is a secondary source on the Roman Empire — and a **primary source** on eighteenth-century English attitudes to Rome, on Enlightenment historiography, and on Gibbon. A 1950s textbook is secondary on the Napoleonic Wars and primary on what 1950s schoolchildren were taught to believe. Nothing about the book changed. The question changed.

| Source | Question | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Gibbon (1776) | What happened in Rome? | Secondary |
| Gibbon (1776) | How did Enlightenment England view Rome? | **Primary** |
| A modern textbook | What caused the war? | Secondary |
| A modern textbook | What is taught about the war today? | **Primary** |

So the useful first question is never "is this primary?" but "**primary to what?**"

## The Chain from Event to Evidence

Every source sits at the end of a chain, and each link is a chance for information to be lost, shaped, or invented:

**Event → perception → recording → copying → preservation → your desk.**

- **Perception.** Only some people were present, seeing part of it, understanding some of that.
- **Recording.** Someone chose to write, chose what to include, wrote for a purpose and an audience.
- **Copying.** Manuscripts were copied by hand for centuries; scribes skipped lines, corrected what looked wrong, and improved what seemed inadequate.
- **Preservation.** An institution decided this was worth keeping.
- **Your desk.** Catalogued, perhaps translated, perhaps edited — every one an interpretive act.

**Provenance** is the documented history of that chain. Its power is mostly negative and completely decisive: a source with no provenance cannot be authenticated, and an object without provenance has lost the archaeological context that would have told you most of what it could say. This is why the antiquities market destroys evidence even when it preserves objects — a looted vase is a beautiful thing that has been stripped of its testimony.

> **The distinction that actually does the work:** _witting_ versus _unwitting_ testimony. Witting testimony is what the source is trying to tell you — and it is where all the motives to distort are concentrated. Unwitting testimony is what it reveals without meaning to: the language it takes for granted, the categories it uses, the things it does not bother to explain because everyone knew them. A medieval charter's witting testimony is who owns the mill; its unwitting testimony is the entire social order it assumes and never mentions. **The witting testimony is what the author wanted; the unwitting is what they could not help. The second is often more reliable and almost always more interesting.**

## Archives and Paleography

A library is organized by _subject_, for readers. An archive is organized by **origin**, for accountability — and this single difference governs everything about how you use one.

The governing principle is ***respect des fonds***: records are kept grouped by the body that created them, in the order that body kept them (_original order_). An archivist who reorganized a ministry's papers by topic — helpfully gathering everything about, say, tariffs into one place — would have destroyed evidence, because _how an institution filed something is itself a historical fact_. Which department handled this? What was it filed next to? What did they think it was an instance of? Reorganize by subject and those questions become permanently unanswerable.

The practical consequence: **you cannot search an archive by topic, because it is not indexed by topic.** You must reason about _who would have generated a record of the thing you want_ and go to that body's fonds. Finding aids describe fonds and series, not subjects. Beginners look for "documents about X" and find nothing; the skill is asking which office would have had to write something down.

| Library | Archive |
|---|---|
| Organized by subject | Organized by creator (_fonds_) |
| Many copies exist | Usually unique |
| Catalogued item by item | Described at series level, often not item by item |
| Search by topic | Reason to the creating body |

## Paleography: The Script Is a Dating Tool

**Paleography** is the study of historical handwriting, and it earns its place because scripts change systematically over time and space — which makes a hand a **dating and localizing instrument** even before you read a word.

The broad European sequence: Roman capitals and cursive; then regional hands after Rome's fall; then **Carolingian minuscule** (c. 800) — clear, separated, lowercase, a deliberate legibility reform under Charlemagne; then **Gothic/textura** (12th c. onward), compressed and angular to save expensive parchment, and much harder to read; then the humanist hands of the Renaissance, which revived Carolingian minuscule in the belief that it was ancient Roman — and which, printed, became the roman type you are reading now.

> **A fine irony:** the letterforms of essentially every book in the Western world descend from a ninth-century Frankish legibility reform, adopted by Renaissance humanists who had misidentified it as classical. You are reading Charlemagne's font because Petrarch's circle got the date wrong.

## The Obstacles That Actually Stop You

Reading old documents fails on four things, and only one of them is letterforms:

1. **Abbreviation.** Parchment was expensive and scribes were fast: words are contracted, with a _macron_ or a suspension mark standing in for whole syllables. A single line can hide a dozen truncations, and you must expand them correctly to read anything.
2. **Ligatures and letterforms.** Joined characters, plus forms that no longer exist or that mean something else — long _s_ looks like _f_; _u_ and _v_, _i_ and _j_ are not yet distinct letters.
3. **Language.** Not modern Latin, French, or English but the _administrative_ dialect of a specific place and century, thick with formulae.
4. **Formulae.** Legal and diplomatic documents are largely boilerplate — which is a gift once you know it, because the boilerplate is predictable, so you can read the standard clauses at speed and concentrate on the few words that are _not_ standard. Those are the ones carrying the information.

**And the deviation from formula is itself evidence.** A charter that departs from the standard clause is telling you something happened here that the template could not handle.

## Numismatics and Epigraphy

Most sources survive by accident. Coins and inscriptions survive **by design** — they were made durable on purpose, which changes what they can tell you and how much you should trust them.

## Coins: The Densest Object in the Archive

A single Roman coin can carry, in a few grams of metal:

- **A date**, often to the year, from the emperor's titulature — his consulship and _tribunicia potestas_ were renewed annually, so a legend reading TR P XII COS V narrows the strike to a twelvemonth.
- **A message**: the reverse advertises a victory, a new emperor's legitimacy, a road built, a god's favour, an heir presented. Coins were the empire's mass medium — the only image of the emperor most subjects ever saw, in everyone's hand, everywhere.
- **An economy**: metal fineness is measurable. The Roman silver denarius runs from ~97% silver under Augustus down to about 5% by the mid-third century — an inflation curve you can weigh.
- **A geography**: mint marks say where; **hoard distributions** map circulation, trade, and — since people bury treasure when frightened and recover it when not — the unrecovered hoards cluster in the decades of crisis.

The most powerful trick is **stratigraphic**: a coin in an archaeological layer gives a _terminus post quem_ — the layer cannot be earlier than the coin's strike date. But coins circulate for decades, so a worn coin dates the layer to _some time after_ its issue, not to its issue. The gap is the whole art. **Wear is a clock: a nearly-new coin narrows the interval; a coin worn smooth may be fifty years into its working life.**

## Inscriptions: The Official Voice, Chiselled

Epigraphy studies inscriptions on stone, bronze, and other hard materials, and its virtues are structural:

| Virtue | Why it holds |
|---|---|
| **Uncopied** | No scribes intervened — you read what was cut |
| **Located** | It stands (or stood) where it was meant to stand |
| **Public** | Meant to be read by contemporaries, so it must be plausible |
| **Formulaic** | Standard formulae make fragments restorable |

That last one does real work. Because funerary and honorific inscriptions follow tight conventions, a broken stone can often be restored with confidence — epigraphers write the missing letters in brackets, and the formula tells them how many are gone. The great corpora (the _Corpus Inscriptionum Latinarum_, some 180,000 texts) turn this into a search problem.

And the _ordinary_ inscriptions are the treasure. Tombstones name soldiers, freedmen, midwives, and children — people who never appear in a single literary source. Roman literature is written by and about perhaps the top fraction of one percent; epigraphy is where a plumber's wife gets a sentence in the historical record.

> **The limitation, stated plainly:** both media are official, expensive, and public — a coin is a state's advertisement of itself and a monument is what someone could afford to have said permanently. Their reliability is exactly inverted from what their durability suggests. **A coin proclaiming CONCORDIA MILITUM ("harmony of the soldiers") is near-proof that the army was mutinous** — nobody advertises the thing they already have. Read the message as evidence of anxiety, not of fact; and read the metal, the mint, the wear, and the findspot as evidence of everything the message was trying to distract you from.

The animation takes a single third-century denarius and reads it four times.

**The date.** The obverse legend resolves letter by letter, and the titulature does the work: consulships and renewals of _tribunicia potestas_ were annual, so the abbreviations narrow the strike to a single year. The coin dates itself.

**The message.** The reverse turns: CONCORDIA MILITVM, the harmony of the soldiers, clasped hands, a legend of unity. The camera holds on it — the empire's mass medium, in every hand, the only image of the emperor most subjects would ever see.

**The metal.** Then the silver-fineness curve draws across the frame, from roughly 97% under Augustus down toward 5% by the mid-third century, and a marker drops onto this coin's position. It is billon, silver-washed, worth a fraction of what it claims.

$$\text{proclaimed value} \;\gg\; \text{metal value}$$

**The lie.** The last beat sets the message against the record: the years around this strike run through mutiny, usurpation, and murdered emperors. The animation lets the two readings sit on screen together — the coin insists on harmony precisely while the armies are making and killing emperors, and its debased metal is the empire paying them with a promise it cannot keep.

Nobody advertises what they already have. The message is evidence of the anxiety; the metal is evidence of the crisis; the date tells you exactly when both were true.

## Diplomatics and Forgery Detection

The **Donation of Constantine** claimed that the Emperor Constantine, cured of leprosy and baptized by Pope Sylvester, had in gratitude handed the Western Empire to the papacy — Rome, Italy, and the western provinces, together with imperial primacy over the other patriarchates.

It underwrote papal territorial claims for seven centuries. It was cited in canon law. It was quoted at emperors. And it was fabricated, probably in the eighth-century papal chancery.

## Valla Kills It (1440)

Lorenzo Valla's demolition is the founding text of historical source criticism, and its method is entirely internal — he never had to leave his desk or find a rival document. He simply read the thing _as a fourth-century object_ and asked whether it could be one:

1. **The Latin is wrong.** It is not fourth-century Latin. It is eighth-century Latin, full of medieval usages and barbarisms Constantine's chancery could not have produced. A language changes, and a forger writes in his own.
2. **The vocabulary is anachronistic.** The text uses terms — for offices, garments, honours — that did not exist in the fourth century. It calls Constantinople a patriarchate before it was one.
3. **The realia are wrong.** It describes regalia, ceremonies, and administrative arrangements of a later era.
4. **The silence is deafening.** No contemporary source mentions the most consequential land transfer in the history of the West. Not one.
5. **The behaviour makes no sense.** No emperor gives away half an empire, and the popes did not act, for centuries afterward, like people who owned Italy.

> **Why Valla matters more than the result:** he proved a document false without a single external witness — by reading it against itself and against what a fourth-century object _must_ look like. That is the method: **a forgery is anchored to the moment of forging, not the moment it claims.** The forger cannot help writing in his own Latin, thinking in his own categories, and knowing his own world. Every forgery carries the fingerprint of its actual date, and the historian's job is to find it.

## Diplomatics: The Anatomy of a Document

_Diplomatics_ — founded as a discipline by Jean Mabillon in 1681, out of a furious argument between monasteries over which charters were genuine — dissects documents by their formal parts, because authentic documents from a given chancery follow tight conventions and forgers get the conventions subtly wrong.

| Part | What it contains | How it betrays a forger |
|---|---|---|
| **Protocol** | Invocation, address, greeting | Wrong formula for the office or reign |
| **Text** | Preamble, statement, disposition, clauses | Anachronistic terms; over-explicitness |
| **Eschatocol** | Date, witnesses, subscription, seal | Impossible dates; wrong witness lists |

**External criticism** examines the physical object: parchment, ink, script, seal, dimensions, folds. **Internal criticism** examines the content: language, formulae, consistency, plausibility.

## The Tell of the Forger

Real forgeries share a signature, and it is psychological rather than technical:

- **Over-explicitness.** Genuine documents assume what everyone knew; forgeries spell out exactly the point in dispute, because that is why they exist. A charter that anxiously specifies the very right being litigated in the forger's own decade is telling on itself.
- **Too good.** The document answers the present question perfectly, which no genuine document does — real ones are cluttered with irrelevance.
- **The forger's present leaks.** His Latin, his handwriting habits, his assumptions about what an office does.

**And the crucial complication:** most medieval forgeries were made by people who believed the claim was _true_. A monastery whose genuine charter had burned would forge a replacement documenting rights it really held. So "forged" and "false" are not synonyms, and the historian's finding is about the _document_, not the claim. The forgery is still excellent evidence — of the moment of forging, of what was disputed then, and of what a claim needed to look like to be believed.

## Demographic and Statistical History

Before the nineteenth century almost nobody counted populations for the sake of knowing them. States counted for **taxing** and **conscripting**, which means every pre-modern number was produced by a process the counted had excellent reasons to defeat. Yet we now have reasonably confident English population figures back to 1541 — and the technique that produced them is one of the discipline's genuine triumphs.

## Family Reconstitution

Parish registers recorded baptisms, marriages, and burials for religious reasons, with no demographic intent whatsoever. **Family reconstitution** (Louis Henry's method, industrialized by the Cambridge Group) links those three registers back together person by person: this baptism and that marriage and this burial are one woman, and these baptisms are her children.

Do it across a parish and you recover what no source ever recorded: age at first marriage, birth intervals, completed family size, infant mortality, remarriage speed after widowhood. The labour is brutal — thousands of hand-linked records per parish — and the payoff reversed a central assumption of European history.

**The finding:** north-west Europe had a distinctive marriage pattern — late marriage (women at 24–26) and a large proportion never marrying at all. Since fertility within marriage was largely uncontrolled, _the age of marriage was the population's brake_. Push marriage later or leave more women unmarried, and births fall — no contraception required. Population responded to the economy through the wedding, because couples waited for a viable household. That is a control loop nobody designed and nobody noticed, discovered entirely by re-linking records kept for a different purpose.

## Every Pre-Modern Number Is a Negotiation

| Source | Made for | Systematic distortion |
|---|---|---|
| Tax rolls | Extraction | **Under**-count: people hide |
| Muster rolls | Conscription | **Under**-count: sons vanish |
| Grain requisition lists | Supply | Under-report the harvest |
| Parish registers | Religion | Miss the unbaptized, dissenters, the mobile |
| Bills of mortality | Plague policy | Cause of death is a searcher's guess |

The rule: **ask what the count was for, and the direction of the bias follows.** A tax roll undercounts because the purpose of a tax roll, from below, is to be defeated. Nobody was confused about this at the time; only later readers mistake the roll for a census.

## The Multiplier Problem

You have 400 hearths in 1377 and want people. Multiply by a household size — but which? Estimates run from 4.0 to 5.5, and that spread alone swings your answer from 1,600 to 2,200: **a 38% range, before any other error.** Then compound it with unknown exemption rates and evasion, and confident-looking population figures for pre-modern Europe rest on a chain of assumptions any link of which can move the total by a third.

> **The honest practice:** report the assumption with the number, and give the range rather than the point. "About 1,600–2,200, assuming 4.0–5.5 per hearth and no allowance for evasion" is a better sentence than "1,900" — because it tells the reader what would have to change for the figure to move, which is the only thing that makes a number arguable rather than decorative. **A spurious decimal place is not precision; it is a claim about evidence you do not have.**

The animation opens on three columns of raw entries — baptisms, marriages, burials — kept by a parish priest with no interest in demography whatsoever. Nothing about them looks like data.

Then the links begin drawing. A baptism entry reaches across to a marriage; the marriage reaches to a cluster of baptisms; a burial closes a line. One family assembles out of scattered records, then ten, then the parish. What was three lists becomes a population with structure.

Now the measurements appear, none of which any register ever recorded: age at first marriage, birth intervals, completed family size, infant mortality.

$$\text{baptisms} + \text{marriages} + \text{burials} \;\xrightarrow{\;\text{linkage}\;}\; \text{age structure, fertility, mortality}$$

The final movement makes the discovery visible. A marriage-age dial appears beside a population curve. Drag marriage age from 20 to 26 and the births thin out and the curve bends downward — no contraception anywhere in the model. The animation holds on it: in north-west Europe the wedding _was_ the brake, because couples waited for a viable household, so population answered the economy through the age of marriage.

Nobody designed that control loop and nobody at the time noticed it. It was recovered four centuries later by re-linking records that had been kept for the salvation of souls.

## Visual and Iconographic Sources

The temptation with images is to treat them as transparent — as though a painting shows you a room and a photograph shows you an event. Every image is **made**, by someone, for someone, with choices at every step: what to include, where to stand, what to leave outside the edge.

The frame is the first argument. **What is outside it was decided by someone**, and you cannot see the decision — which makes framing the most effective form of distortion ever invented, because it leaves no trace inside the image.

## Photographs Lie Without Faking Anything

The nineteenth century believed the camera could not lie, and the belief survives in the phrase "photographic evidence". But a photograph involves:

| Choice | Effect |
|---|---|
| **Framing** | What is cropped out is invisible and unrecoverable |
| **Moment** | A face mid-blink reads as drunk; mid-word, as hysterical |
| **Angle and lens** | Low angle ennobles; long lens compresses a crowd into a mob |
| **Caption** | Names the event and often supplies the meaning entirely |
| **Selection** | Which frame of the roll was published, and by whom |

Not one of these requires manipulating the negative. And the historical record is worse than that: early photographs required long exposures, so many "documentary" images of war are staged — Roger Fenton's Crimean cannonballs were arranged, and Civil War photographers moved corpses to improve the composition. The Falling Soldier, Iwo Jima, the Reichstag flag: the most famous "instants" of the twentieth century are contested precisely because the camera's authority made staging worth the trouble.

> **The rule:** treat a photograph as testimony from a witness with a position, not as a fact. Ask who held the camera, why they were there, what they were paid for, and — always — what is just outside the frame.

## Maps Are Arguments With Coastlines

A map is a **projection**: the earth is a sphere, the page is flat, and no flattening preserves everything. You choose which distortion to accept.

- **Mercator** preserves angles — which is why it is a superb navigational chart — and inflates area with latitude. Greenland appears roughly the size of Africa. Africa is about **14 times larger**.
- **Peters** preserves area and mangles shape, and was pushed explicitly as a political corrective.
- **Every** projection distorts. There is no neutral map, and the choice of which lie to tell is the map's first argument.

The rest of the cartographic arguments are less subtle: what is at the centre; what is named and in whose language; which borders are drawn firm and which dotted; what is left blank. "Blank" spaces on colonial maps were full of people — the blankness was a claim that nothing was there worth recording, and a rather effective one.

**The historian's payoff:** an image's _unwitting_ testimony is usually its best. A propaganda poster is unreliable about conditions and authoritative about anxieties — it shows exactly what the regime feared people believed. A painting's background — the tools on the wall, the food on the table, the clothes of the person nobody was looking at — was not what the painter was arguing about, and is therefore the part least distorted by the argument.

## Oral History and Testimony

Oral history is the one source that answers back. Every other source is fixed — you ask a charter a question and it says what it always said. An interview can be pressed, redirected, asked _why_, asked what it felt like, asked about the thing no document ever recorded because everyone present already knew it.

And it reaches people the archive structurally excludes. The three filters — recorded, kept, survived — tilt toward the literate and powerful; oral history goes to the people the paper never described. For the history of the twentieth-century working class, of women's work, of migration, of colonial rule from below, it is not a supplement. It is often the only source there is.

## Memory Is Reconstructive, Not Retrieval

The obvious objection is that memory is unreliable. The obvious objection is correct, and it is worse than most people think — because memory does not degrade like a photocopy. It **reconstructs**.

| Effect | What it does |
|---|---|
| **Reconstruction** | Each recall rebuilds the memory — and re-stores the rebuilt version |
| **Retrospective coherence** | The past is edited to make sense of how things turned out |
| **Source confusion** | What you were told, read, or saw on television becomes what you remember |
| **Social conformation** | Memory reshapes toward what the group agrees happened |
| **Present concerns** | Today's questions determine what surfaces and how it is framed |

Note what this does to the interview itself: a witness recalling 1943 in 2025 is not opening a file from 1943. They are reconstructing it now, through eighty years of subsequent telling, reading, film, family narrative, and the knowledge of how it all came out — and through the fact that _you_ are sitting there asking. The interviewer is part of the source.

## Portelli's Reversal

Alessandro Portelli investigated a worker killed in Terni in 1949 and found that many locals dated the death to 1953, moved it into the middle of mass layoffs, and attached it to a different set of events. Factually, the memories were wrong.

His argument — and it is the founding insight of the field — is that **the error is the finding**. The community had relocated the death to where it _meant_ the most: into the story of the layoffs, where it became a symbol of what was done to Terni. That is not a defective recollection of 1949; it is accurate testimony about what the killing came to mean, how the community made sense of its own defeat, and what it needed the death to be about.

> **The formulation:** "Oral sources tell us not just what people did, but what they wanted to do, what they believed they were doing, and what they now think they did." A factual error in testimony is a datum, not a disqualification — as long as you are clear which question you are answering with it.

## What This Means in Practice

- **Never use testimony alone for verifiable facts** — dates, numbers, sequences. Cross-check against documents; that is what documents are good at.
- **Do use it, essentially alone, for meaning** — experience, motivation, atmosphere, what it was like, what people thought they were doing. No document is good at that.
- **Record the interview conditions.** Who asked, what was asked, when, in whose presence. A widow's account differs with her son in the room.
- **Preserve the recording, not just a transcript.** Hesitation, laughter, the refusal to answer, the long pause before a name — the transcript discards exactly the parts where the interview is most informative.

**The symmetry worth holding on to:** documents are strong on the verifiable and weak on the meant; testimony is weak on the verifiable and strong on the meant. Neither is a degraded version of the other, and a historian who dismisses oral history for unreliability has simply misunderstood which question it answers.

## Material Culture as a Source

A letter was written to persuade you. A chronicle was composed to be read. An object was made to be **used**, and that difference is the whole argument for material culture as evidence.

A cooking pot is not trying to convince anyone of anything. Its wall thickness, its soot pattern, its repairs, and the wear on its rim are the residue of thousands of ordinary acts nobody thought worth recording. This is unwitting testimony in its purest form: the object cannot lie about what was done with it, because it was never making a claim in the first place.

And things reach where writing does not. The people who left no documents — the illiterate, the poor, the enslaved, the colonized — left **pots, houses, tools, rubbish, and bones**. For most of humanity across most of the past, material culture is not a supplement to the written record. It is the record.

## What an Object Testifies To

| Property | What it reports |
|---|---|
| **Material** | Where it came from; what trade reached this place |
| **Technique** | The skill available, and how labour was organized |
| **Wear** | What was actually done with it, and how often |
| **Repair** | What it was worth to keep — the clearest proxy for scarcity |
| **Deposition** | How it was discarded, hidden, or buried, and by whom |
| **Distribution** | Who had one and who did not |

**Repair deserves the emphasis.** A society that mends is a society where the object costs more than the labour to fix it; a society that discards has reversed that ratio. Nobody in either society ever wrote the ratio down — and you can read it off a rubbish heap in an afternoon.

## Buildings and Landscapes Are Objects Too

A house plan is a diagram of social relations rendered in stone. Where are the doors? Who can see whom? Is there a room a servant cannot enter, or a passage that lets them move without being seen? Georgian houses added corridors — and corridors are a machine for privacy, replacing rooms that opened into one another with rooms you can be _shut out of_. Nobody published a manifesto about wanting privacy. The floor plan announces it anyway.

Landscapes work the same way, at another scale: field boundaries, terraces, hedges, and ridge-and-furrow record centuries of decisions about labour, tenure, and inheritance. Enclosure is legible from the air as straight lines cutting across curved ones — a legal revolution you can photograph.

> **The trap: objects survive selectively, and the bias is physical.** Stone, ceramic, and metal survive; wood, textile, leather, and food do not. So the record over-represents the durable, and therefore the ceremonial, the elite, and the buried — and under-represents almost everything people actually touched all day. A society that built in timber and dressed in cloth can look impoverished next to one that built in stone, when the only real difference is what the ground kept.

**The historian's move is to read the silence as physics, not as absence.** "No textiles" means "the soil was acidic", not "they had no clothes". And the moment a waterlogged site turns up — a bog, a well, a shipwreck — the missing 90% reappears at once, and the picture built from the durable 10% usually turns out to have been wrong in exactly the direction the survival bias predicted.

## Digital Humanities and Big Data

Franco Moretti's provocation: nobody has read the nineteenth-century novel. People have read _a canon_ — a few hundred books out of tens of thousands published, selected by a filtering process that ran for a century and had opinions. Any claim about "the Victorian novel" is really a claim about the survivors of that filter.

**Distant reading** is the response: don't read the books, count them. Chart genre lifespans, title lengths, character networks, word frequencies across thousands of texts at once. You lose everything that makes a novel worth reading and gain the only thing close reading structurally cannot see — **the shape of the whole**.

The two are not rivals. Close reading answers what a text means; distant reading answers what was typical, when a pattern began, and what the canon left out. Asking either to do the other's job produces nonsense.

## What Computation Is Good At

| Task | Why the machine wins |
|---|---|
| **Counting** | Word frequencies across 50,000 texts; no human can |
| **Linking** | Record linkage across censuses, registers, shipping lists |
| **Mapping** | Plotting thousands of events; patterns emerge spatially |
| **Networks** | Who corresponded with whom, at a scale no reader can hold |
| **Finding the odd** | Anomalies in a mass no one could ever eyeball |

Real results follow: correspondence networks of the Republic of Letters, trans-Atlantic slave-voyage databases that turned scattered manifests into a countable system, name-linked censuses that made nineteenth-century mobility measurable for the first time.

## What Computation Cannot See

**Digitization is a new filter stacked on the old ones.** The archive already filtered by recorded/kept/survived; digitization now adds _scanned_, and scanning follows money, copyright, institutional prestige, and OCR-friendliness. So the digital corpus is not the archive — it is the well-funded, out-of-copyright, cleanly-printed corner of it, and searching it feels exactly like searching everything.

The specific traps:

- **The OCR floor.** Optical character recognition fails on damaged print, gothic type, and handwriting. Your "complete search" of a newspaper run silently skips the smudged pages — and smudged is not random.
- **Searchability bias.** You find what you can search for. Concepts without a keyword — and phenomena people had no word for — are invisible to full-text search, which quietly reshapes research toward whatever happens to be named.
- **Metadata is an argument.** Every category in a database was a decision. Fields for gender, race, occupation, and status impose a classification the sources may not share, and once entered it hardens into a fact and gets counted.
- **False precision.** "1,847 mentions" looks like a measurement. It is a measurement _of a corpus_, and the corpus is a sample nobody designed.

> **The rule that keeps this honest:** a digital method changes the scale, not the epistemology. Every question you would ask a document — who made it, why, what is missing, what is it for — you must ask the corpus, the OCR, and the schema. A number produced from an unexamined corpus is not evidence; it is a rumour with a decimal point.

**The productive loop** is neither pure: distant reading finds the anomaly, close reading explains it, and the explanation suggests the next thing to count. The machine finds where to look. It does not know what it found.

## Source Criticism in Practice

The fantasy is a document that answers the question. The reality is four documents that partly contradict each other, all written by people with reasons, none of them addressing what you actually want to know. **Source criticism is not the stage before the argument. It is the argument.**

## The Questions, Every Time

1. **What is it?** Genre first — a chronicle, a charter, a letter, a report. Genre sets the conventions and therefore the expected distortions.
2. **Who made it, when, and how far from the event?** An eyewitness the same evening is not a compiler three centuries later working from a lost source.
3. **Why?** Every source was made _for_ something. Find the purpose and you have found the direction of the bias.
4. **For whom?** Audience shapes content — a private letter, a public despatch, and a report to a superior say different things about the same afternoon.
5. **What does it take for granted?** The unwitting testimony, and usually the best part.
6. **What is missing, and why?** Silence is evidence, but you must decide whether it is a silence of absence, of filtering, or of physics.

## Corroboration Is About Independence

Three sources agreeing sounds decisive. It is worth nothing if all three copied the fourth.

**Ask whether the agreement is independent.** Independent sources have separate causal paths to the event; dependent ones share an ancestor and multiply nothing but the copy count. Most spectacular ancient "confirmations" are one lost source with three descendants — and the error is not that the historian trusted the sources, but that they counted them.

> **The one rule of thumb worth memorizing: weigh testimony against interest.** When a source reports something _against_ its own interest, that report is unusually strong — a tax record admitting an exemption, a general's despatch conceding a setback, a chronicle recording its patron's defeat. Nobody invents inconvenient facts about themselves. Conversely, a source reporting exactly what it wants to be true has told you about its wants, not the world.

## Contradiction Is a Finding

When sources conflict, the amateur picks the one they like. The historian asks **why they differ**, because the pattern of disagreement is itself evidence:

- Do they diverge exactly where their interests diverge? Then the divergence _is_ the finding — you have located the dispute.
- Do they agree on the mundane and differ on the significant? That is the normal shape of politically live events, and the mundane agreement is your firmest ground.
- Does one contain a detail no purpose explains? Purposeless detail is expensive to invent, and often the most trustworthy thing in the document.

## Writing It Honestly

State the evidence, its provenance, and the inference. Distinguish "the record shows" from "it is likely" from "I suspect" — and give the reader enough to disagree with you. **The measure of an argument is not confidence but whether a reader can see exactly where it would break.**

An argument that survives because its foundations were hidden is worth less than one that fails visibly. And the honest historian's final duty is the unglamorous one: say what the evidence cannot settle, rather than filling the gap with fluency. Most of the past is not recoverable, and a discipline that admits this is more trustworthy — not less — than one that always has an answer.
