## Stratigraphy and Relative Dating

Every occupied place accumulates: floors are laid, walls collapse, pits are dug and refilled, dust settles. Archaeology's founding insight, borrowed from geology, is that this accumulation has **rules** — and the rules turn dirt into a dated document.

| Principle | Statement | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| **Superposition** | In undisturbed deposits, lower layers are older | Depth is a relative clock |
| **Cutting** | A cut (pit, ditch, grave) is younger than everything it cuts | Intrusions date after their targets |
| **Filling** | A fill is younger than the cut that contains it | Pit contents postdate the pit |
| **Association** | Objects sealed in one context entered the ground together | The context, not the object, is the dating unit |

The recording unit is the **context**: a single depositional event — one layer, one cut, one fill. A site is thousands of contexts, and the **Harris matrix** (Edward Harris, 1973) diagrams their complete older/younger relationships as a flowchart of time — the site's stratigraphy with the soil removed.

## The Two Termini

A dated object inside a layer brackets the layer's age from one side only:

- **Terminus post quem (TPQ)** — "limit after which": a coin struck in 120 CE inside a fill means the fill closed _no earlier than_ 120. It could be centuries later — coins circulate and linger.
- **Terminus ante quem (TAQ)** — "limit before which": a structure sealed _beneath_ the datable layer must be older than it.

> **The classic trap — residuality:** old things end up in young layers constantly (a Roman coin in a medieval pit is unremarkable). The reverse — a young thing in a truly sealed old layer — is impossible. That asymmetry is why one anachronistic sherd can redate an entire deposit _later_, but a thousand old sherds cannot push it earlier. The **latest** object in a context sets its TPQ.

Relative dating orders events without calendar years; the next lesson's laboratory methods pin the order to numbers. The order comes first — a radiocarbon date from a misread context dates the wrong event precisely.

The animation builds a site the way time did: floor, occupation debris, collapse, windblown soil — layer on layer. Then a pit knifes down through three of them and refills. Watch the section drawing acquire its logic: the pit's **cut line truncates** every layer it passes through, so the pit must be younger than all of them, no matter how deep its bottom reaches.

$$\text{cut} \Rightarrow \text{younger than everything it cuts}$$

Finally the layers lift out of the ground one by one and rearrange into the **Harris matrix** — boxes and lines, oldest at the bottom, the pit branching in at its true position. The section shows _where_ things are; the matrix shows _when_. Depth was never the clock — **sequence** is, and the pit that reaches deepest while dating youngest is the proof.

## Absolute Dating Techniques

Stratigraphy orders events; **absolute dating** hangs calendar numbers on the order. Each method is a natural clock with a specific tick, range, and material — choosing the wrong one is like timing a sprint with a sundial.

| Method | Clock | Dates | Range |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Radiocarbon (C-14)** | Decay of carbon-14 after death | Anything once alive | to ~50,000 yr |
| **Dendrochronology** | Annual tree-ring widths | Preserved timber | to ~12,000 yr, precise to the year |
| **Potassium-argon (K-Ar)** | Argon accumulating in cooled lava | Volcanic rock around fossils | 100,000 yr to billions |
| **Thermoluminescence (TL)** | Radiation dose since last heating | Pottery, burnt flint, hearths | to ~500,000 yr |

## How the Radiocarbon Clock Runs

Living things constantly exchange carbon with the atmosphere, holding a steady trace of radioactive **carbon-14**. Death stops the intake; the C-14 then decays with a **half-life of 5,730 years**. Measure the surviving fraction and the elapsed time follows:

$$t = 5730 \times \log_2(N_0 / N)$$

Half the original C-14 means one half-life (~5,730 years); a quarter means two (~11,460); an eighth, three (~17,190). Past ten half-lives almost nothing survives — hence the ~50,000-year ceiling, and hence why dinosaurs (66+ million years) are radiocarbon's most popular impossible request.

## The Fine Print

Willard Libby's method (1949, Nobel 1960) has a subtlety: atmospheric C-14 was never perfectly constant, so raw radiocarbon years drift from calendar years. The fix is **calibration** — tree-ring sequences of known calendar age carry their own measured C-14, providing a correction curve back through the millennia. Dendrochronology thus does double duty: the year-exact dating of timbers _and_ the ruler that keeps radiocarbon honest.

> **Date the death, not the use:** C-14 dates when the organism _died_. A hearth burning 300-year-old driftwood dates the tree, not the fire ("old wood problem"); a reused oak beam dates its felling, not the house's third renovation. The laboratory delivers a number — the _archaeologist_ must supply what the number means.

The animation follows one organism's carbon. In life, C-14 holds steady — intake balances decay. At death the intake line is severed and the decay curve takes over, halving the stock every 5,730 years: one half, one quarter, one eighth, the bars shrinking down the exponential staircase.

$$N(t) = N_0 \cdot (1/2)^{t / 5730}$$

Watch the far end of the curve: by ten half-lives the remaining C-14 sinks into the measurement noise floor — the ~50,000-year ceiling appears on screen not as a rule but as a _consequence_. Then the calibration beat: raw radiocarbon years bend against the tree-ring curve, and the date steps from "radiocarbon age" to calendar age. The clock is elegant; reading it honestly is the craft.

## Excavation Methodology

Excavation is the only experiment that can never be rerun: to read a site you must take it apart, and the layers, once lifted, are gone. Archaeology's answer to this brutal fact is **total recording** — the site is destroyed, but it is destroyed _into an archive_ of drawings, photographs, coordinates, and context sheets from which the sequence can be rebuilt on paper forever.

## Before the First Trowel

Digging is the last resort, not the first move. A project begins with **survey**:

- **Desk-based assessment** — maps, archives, previous finds, aerial photos.
- **Field walking** — systematic surface collection across plowed fields; artifact scatters betray buried sites.
- **Test pits and sampling** — because no one can dig everything, statistics decides where: random sampling avoids bias, stratified sampling covers every terrain zone, and judgment targets the anomalies.

The site is then gridded — a permanent coordinate system so that every find has an address: square, layer, and three-dimensional position. **Provenience is the find's meaning**; an object without context is merely an _objet d'art_.

## Taking It Apart in Order

Modern practice excavates in **single contexts**, peeling depositional events in reverse order — youngest first, each context drawn, photographed, levelled, and sheet-recorded before removal, then its finds bagged under the context number. The alternative of digging in arbitrary fixed spits is faster and blinder: a 20-centimeter spit can chew through three floors and mix their contents permanently.

> **Why not dig it all?** Even with perfect recording, excavation spends the site. Best practice leaves part of every site untouched — future archaeologists will bring questions and instruments we cannot imagine, and the unexcavated half is our gift to them. (Radiocarbon, DNA, and isotope analysis all arrived _after_ most of the world's great sites were already dug.)

## Artifact Typology and Seriation

A thousand potsherds are noise until you classify them. **Typology** sorts artifacts into types by shared attributes — form, material, decoration, technique — and a good type is one that _clusters in time or space_, so that its presence carries information. Types are tools, not truths: the "type" of a pot is a hypothesis that these fragments were made to one shared idea.

Archaeologists distinguish:

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Attribute** | A single observable feature (rim shape, temper, glaze color) |
| **Type** | A recurring bundle of attributes |
| **Assemblage** | All the material from one context — the type mix at one moment |
| **Industry / tradition** | Types sharing a technological lineage over time |

## Seriation: Chronology Without a Lab

Before radiocarbon, how did anyone order sites in time with no stratigraphy linking them? **Seriation**, the elegant answer, rests on one fact of fashion: a style is rare when new, becomes common, peaks, then fades — its popularity over time traces a lens shape. Plot each type's frequency as a horizontal bar per time-slice and stack the slices in the order that makes every type swell and shrink smoothly, and you get the famous **"battleship curves."** The single arrangement that makes _all_ the curves rise-and-fall cleanly is the chronological order.

Flinders Petrie invented the method (**sequence dating**, 1890s) to order thousands of Egyptian graves that no stratigraphy connected — sorting them purely by the changing frequencies of their pottery types.

> **Direction is the catch:** seriation delivers the _sequence_ but not its _polarity_ — the battleship curves look identical run forwards or backwards. An external anchor (one C-14 date, one stratified find, one historical link) tells you which end is early. Relative method, absolute anchor: the recurring partnership of the whole chapter.

## Bioarchaeology

A skeleton is a written record in bone. Bioarchaeology reads it — reconstructing how a person aged, ate, worked, sickened, and moved — and aggregated across a cemetery, it recovers the health and demography of whole populations that left no census.

## What the Bones Say

| Question | Where the answer lives | Reliability |
|---|---|---|
| **Age at death** | Tooth eruption/wear; bone fusion; pubic-symphysis wear | High in young, coarser in old age |
| **Biological sex** | Pelvis shape (definitive), skull robusticity | High for adults; poor for children |
| **Stature** | Long-bone length via regression | Good |
| **Disease and injury** | Lesions, healed fractures, joint wear | Only bone-marking conditions leave traces |

Age reads most sharply in the young — teeth erupt and bones fuse on a tight schedule — and blurs in adults, where methods estimate a range, not a birthday. Sex determination leans on the pelvis, reshaped by childbirth's demands, and is unreliable before puberty because those differences have not yet developed.

## Chemistry: Diet and Origins

Bone chemistry preserves biography the bones' shape cannot:

- **Carbon isotopes (δ¹³C)** distinguish diets — notably whether staple crops were "C3" plants (wheat, barley) or "C4" plants (millet, maize) — letting archaeologists watch maize spread across the ancient Americas plate by plate.
- **Nitrogen isotopes (δ¹⁵N)** rise with trophic level, revealing how much _meat and fish_ versus plants a person ate, and even flagging the nitrogen spike of breastfeeding infants.
- **Strontium and oxygen isotopes**, locked into tooth enamel in childhood from local water and rock, reveal **where a person grew up** — so a skeleton buried in one region but bearing another region's isotopic signature is a **migrant**, caught in the act millennia later.

> **The survivorship trap — the "osteological paradox":** a skeleton with _healed_ disease lesions belonged to someone whose body fought the illness and lived long enough to remodel bone; a victim who died fast may show _no_ lesions at all. So a cemetery full of lesions can mean a _healthier_, more resilient population, not a sicker one. As always, the evidence needs an interpreter — the bone states a fact; the meaning is an argument.

## Environmental Archaeology

Monuments survive; the world around them mostly rots. Environmental archaeology recovers that vanished setting — forests, fields, floods, herds — from the microscopic and the discarded, reconstructing the landscape people actually inhabited.

## The Proxies

Nature keeps records in materials that outlast the things they record:

| Proxy | What it is | What it reveals |
|---|---|---|
| **Pollen (palynology)** | Near-indestructible grains in bogs, lakebeds | Vegetation history; forest clearance |
| **Phytoliths** | Silica bodies from plant cells | Crops even where pollen fails; grasses |
| **Charcoal / seeds (macros)** | Charred plant remains in deposits | Diet, fuel, cultivated species |
| **Faunal bones (zooarchaeology)** | Animal remains | Herding, hunting, butchery, seasons |
| **Insects, molluscs** | Species with narrow habitat needs | Fine-grained local climate and moisture |

## What They Reconstruct

Pollen cores are the workhorse: drill a bog, and each centimeter down is an older snapshot of the region's vegetation. The unmistakable signature of the first farmers is the **elm decline and rise of cereal and weed pollen** — the pollen record _watching Neolithic clearance happen_, tree by tree, thousands of years before any text.

Zooarchaeology reads the bones of eaten animals: species ratios separate hunters from herders; **kill-off patterns** (the ages at which animals were slaughtered) distinguish herding for meat (kill young males) from herding for milk or wool (keep females alive for years); cut-marks record butchery; and tooth-eruption stages even fix the **season** of slaughter.

> **Proxies are indirect — mind the filters.** Wind-pollinated trees (pine, oak) flood the pollen rain; insect-pollinated plants and many crops are badly under-represented, so raw pollen percentages are not a headcount of the vegetation. Bones face taphonomy — dogs, decay, and recovery bias reshape any assemblage before the archaeologist ever counts it. Reading proxies means reading through the filter that made them, not around it.

## Settlement Patterns and Landscapes

A single site is a dot; the real subject is the **pattern of dots** — how settlements of different sizes distribute across a region, and what that distribution says about economy, power, and movement. **Landscape archaeology** widens the lens from the trench to the territory, treating the whole terrain as the artifact.

## Site Hierarchy

Rank a region's sites by size and function and a **hierarchy** usually appears: many hamlets, fewer villages, a handful of towns, one dominant center. The number of _tiers_ is a rough gauge of political complexity — a two-tier landscape (villages plus one center) suggests a chiefdom; a four-tier landscape (hamlet–village–town–capital) signals a state administering through nested levels. The pattern of dots is a fossil of the organization that made it.

## Why Settlements Sit Where They Do

**Central place theory** (Walter Christaller, 1933) models the ideal: if people minimize travel to obtain goods and services, central places space themselves _evenly_, each surrounded by a hexagonal service area — hexagons alone tile a plane with no gaps or overlaps. Real landscapes never match the clean lattice, but the _deviations_ are the data: rivers, coasts, passes, and defensive hills pull settlements off the ideal grid, and reading the pull reconstructs what mattered — trade, safety, water, or a lord's whim.

Archaeologists test spacing with the **nearest-neighbour statistic**, which distinguishes three regimes:

- **Clustered** — sites huddle (around a resource, a market, a spring).
- **Random** — no organizing principle detectable.
- **Regular / dispersed** — sites hold each other at arm's length (competition for land, central-place spacing).

> **The visibility trap:** what survives to be mapped is not what once existed. Stone towns outlast timber hamlets; deep-plowed valleys erase sites that hilltops preserve; a Roman villa is easy to find and a laborer's hut is not. A settlement "pattern" is always partly a **preservation and discovery pattern** — the map of the found, not the map of the past.

## Remote Sensing and Geophysics

Excavation destroys; remote sensing does not. In fifty years archaeology has acquired a suite of instruments that read buried sites through soil, canopy, and pasture — turning survey from a preliminary chore into the discipline's most productive method.

## From the Air

**Aerial photography** exploits three signatures:

- **Cropmarks** — crops over a buried ditch root deeper, staying green in drought; crops over a buried wall parch early. The field prints the plan of a vanished settlement in living wheat, visible for a few weeks in a dry summer and invisible for years otherwise.
- **Soilmarks** — plowing scatters differently-colored buried material across the surface.
- **Shadow marks** — low evening sun rakes across earthworks too subtle to see at noon.

**LiDAR** (laser pulses from an aircraft) goes further: because a fraction of the pulses slip between leaves and return from the ground, software can _strip the vegetation away_ and render a bare-earth model. LiDAR has rewritten entire regions — Maya causeways and field systems under Guatemalan jungle, Angkor's urban sprawl under Cambodian forest — landscapes centuries of ground survey had missed.

## From the Ground

| Method | Reads | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **Magnetometry** | Tiny magnetic contrasts | Fast, wide coverage: ditches, pits, kilns, hearths |
| **Resistivity** | Resistance to electrical current | Stone walls (dry, high resistance) vs ditches (damp, low) |
| **Ground-penetrating radar (GPR)** | Reflected radar pulses, timed | Depth _and_ plan — 3D slices of buried structures |

Magnetometry is the workhorse: it can sweep a field in a morning, and burnt features (kilns, hearths) shine because fire re-aligns the magnetism of the soil. GPR is slower but uniquely gives _depth_, cutting horizontal slices through a buried building floor by floor.

> **A picture is not an interpretation.** Geophysics shows anomalies — differences. A rectangular high-resistance anomaly _might_ be a Roman building, or a modern sheepfold, or a geological quirk. The instrument tells you where to put the trench; only the trench tells you what it is, and only stratigraphy tells you when.

## Experimental and Cognitive Archaeology

**Experimental archaeology** turns speculation into hypothesis-testing: replicate the technology, run the process, compare the results with the archaeological record. If your reconstruction cannot actually work, it is not what happened.

The results are humbling and precise. Flint knapping shows that an Acheulean handaxe demands hours of skilled, forward-planned reduction — the maker held the finished shape in mind before the first strike. Replica firings reveal what kiln temperatures the pottery required. Experimental earthworks, deliberately built and then re-excavated after decades, show exactly how ditches silt and mounds slump — calibrating how archaeologists read every ditch section. And the _Kon-Tiki_ and Polynesian voyaging replicas established that ancient watercraft could genuinely cross open ocean.

## Use-Wear and Residues

Experiment underwrites two forensic methods. Cut a replica blade through hide, then wood, then bone, and each material polishes the edge in a distinct, microscopically recognizable way — so an excavated blade's polish can be read back to its **job**. And traces of blood, starch, or plant residues surviving on real tools can be identified against experimental references, sometimes to the species.

## Reading Ancient Minds

**Cognitive archaeology** asks what the material record implies about the thinking behind it. Its evidence is behavioural, and its arguments run from _capability_ to _concept_:

| Evidence | Inference |
|---|---|
| Standardized tool forms across a region | A shared mental template |
| Ochre, beads, deliberate burial | Symbolic behaviour |
| Raw material transported 300 km | Planning, exchange networks |
| Weight standards, tallies, seals | Measurement and accounting |
| Astronomical alignments in monuments | Systematic sky observation |

> **The limit — and the discipline:** archaeology recovers _behaviour_, and behaviour under-determines belief. A burial with grave goods proves that people treated the dead with care and expense; it cannot, by itself, prove a specific belief in an afterlife of a particular kind. Cognitive archaeology's honest claims are about **capabilities** — planning, symbolizing, measuring, abstracting — not about doctrines. The best practitioners state the inference and its ceiling in the same breath.

## Ethics and Heritage Management

Archaeology's evidence is finite, and everyone has a claim on it: descendant communities, nation-states, museums, scholars, collectors, and the market. Heritage management is the field where those claims collide — and where the discipline's ethics stop being abstract.

## Looting Destroys Knowledge, Not Just Objects

An looted object survives; its **context** does not. The pot ripped from a tomb loses its date, its associations, its sequence — everything except its market price. Roughly speaking, looting converts _evidence_ into _decoration_. And the trade is demand-driven: every purchase of an unprovenienced antiquity finances the next pit dug into the next cemetery. This is why professional ethics forbid archaeologists from appraising or authenticating unprovenienced material — the expertise itself would become a service to the market.

## The Legal Architecture

| Instrument | Year | Core provision |
|---|---|---|
| **Hague Convention** | 1954 | Protects cultural property in armed conflict |
| **UNESCO Convention** | 1970 | Bars illicit import/export; the watershed date for acquisitions |
| **World Heritage Convention** | 1972 | Sites of outstanding universal value |
| **NAGPRA** (US) | 1990 | Returns Native American remains and sacred objects to descendants |

The 1970 UNESCO Convention became the profession's bright line: reputable museums will not acquire material that left its country of origin after 1970 without documentation. **NAGPRA** did something more radical — it recognized that descendant communities, not museums, hold the primary claim to ancestral remains, transforming archaeologists' relationship with Indigenous nations from extraction toward consultation and, at its best, collaboration.

## The Hard Cases

The Parthenon Marbles (removed by Lord Elgin, 1801–12, contested ever since), Benin Bronzes (looted in the 1897 punitive expedition, now being returned by museum after museum), and countless human remains in European collections all pose the same question in different keys: does legality at the time of taking settle the matter, or does the descendant community's claim outrank the possessor's title?

> **The working consensus:** the past is not a scholarly commons free for the taking. Descendant communities have standing to speak for their ancestors, and consultation is not a courtesy extended to them — it is a condition of doing the work at all. Where the discipline once asked "what may we study?", it increasingly asks "with whom?"
