# Unit 6: Geography

## Physical Geography and Geomorphology

Geography does not determine history — we will spend the last lesson of this unit dismantling that idea properly. But the stage is not neutral either. It makes some things cheap and other things ruinously expensive, and over centuries the difference in cost shows up as a difference in what happened.

The honest formulation: **geography sets the price list, not the outcome.** People choose; the landscape decides what the choices cost.

## Water Is the Cheapest Thing in Pre-Modern History

Before railways, moving goods overland was catastrophically expensive. Rough figures for the Roman world: a cargo could cross the **entire Mediterranean** for roughly what it cost to cart it **100 km inland**. That single ratio explains more about ancient settlement than any battle.

The consequences cascade:

- **Cities cling to water.** Coasts, navigable rivers, confluences. A city 200 km from water in a pre-industrial economy is a city that has to be _very_ good at something else.
- **Empires are shaped like their water.** Rome is a ring around a sea — the Mediterranean was its highway, not its edge. Its landward frontiers were where the cheap transport stopped.
- **Rivers unify; mountains fragment.** A navigable river basin tends toward a single political unit, because the river makes the basin cheap to hold together. Broken terrain does the reverse, which is why fragmented Greece produced _poleis_ and the Nile produced a state that lasted three thousand years.

> **The Nile is the cleanest case in history.** It floods predictably, deposits fertile silt annually, flows north while the prevailing wind blows south — so you sail upstream and drift down. A single river valley, navigable both ways, agriculturally self-renewing, and walled by desert on both sides. Egypt's political continuity is not a mystery; it is a river with unusually good terms.

## Soil Is Slow and Decisive

| Soil / substrate | Consequence |
|---|---|
| **Alluvial floodplain** | Renewed annually; supports dense settled populations |
| **Loess** | Deep, soft, fertile; easily worked with primitive ploughs |
| **Volcanic** | Extremely fertile — people live on volcanoes knowing exactly why |
| **Podzol / leached tropical** | Nutrients wash out fast; slash-and-burn, then move |
| **Karst limestone** | Water disappears underground; surface is arid despite rainfall |

Soil constrains on a timescale nobody can perceive from inside a lifetime, which is exactly why it took the Annales school and their _longue durée_ to make it visible as history at all. A tropical soil that will not hold nutrients for more than three seasons imposes a settlement pattern, which imposes a political ceiling — and no one involved ever experiences this as a decision.

## The Landscape Also Moves

Geomorphology is the reminder that the stage is itself in motion, just slowly:

- **Rivers change course**, and cities die of it. Harbours silt up: Ephesus, Bruges, and Ostia are all inland now, and each was a great port that ended not through conquest but through sediment.
- **Coastlines advance and retreat.** Ancient shorelines sit kilometres from the modern sea, so "coastal" sites can be found in fields.
- **Erosion is often our own doing.** Deforestation strips slopes, sediment fills the valley, malaria arrives with the marsh. The classical Mediterranean did a great deal of this to itself.

**The methodological point:** you cannot read modern terrain back onto the past. The historian who looks at today's map and reasons about an ancient decision is analysing a stage that has since been rebuilt — and the actors were standing somewhere else.

## Climate and Biomes

Weather is what happened on Tuesday. **Climate is the distribution weather is drawn from** — the averages, and just as importantly the _variance_. Confusing the two is the oldest error in the field, and it runs in both directions: a cold winter is not evidence against a warming climate, and a warm decade did not by itself end a dynasty.

For history, the variance often matters more than the mean. A region with reliable rainfall and one with the same average rainfall arriving unpredictably are not the same place at all. The second cannot support the same population, because populations live at the level the _bad_ years permit, not the average ones.

## The Köppen System

Wladimir Köppen's 1884 classification is still the standard, and its elegance is that it classifies climate by **vegetation** — the plants integrate temperature and rainfall over the year better than any index a person could design.

| Group | Name | Character |
|---|---|---|
| **A** | Tropical | Hot, wet; no real winter |
| **B** | Arid | Evaporation exceeds precipitation |
| **C** | Temperate | Mild winters — the historic heartland of dense agrarian states |
| **D** | Continental | Cold winters, warm summers; big seasonal swing |
| **E** | Polar | No warm season |

Group **B** is defined by a _relation_, not a temperature: arid means evaporation exceeds precipitation. That is why a cold desert is a desert. Aridity is a budget, not a thermometer reading.

## Climate's Fingerprints on the Record

Climate leaves signatures a historian can read, and the last century of paleoclimatology has made this genuinely quantitative:

- **Tree rings** (dendrochronology): annual, exactly dated, sensitive to growing-season conditions.
- **Ice cores**: trapped air gives past atmospheric composition; layers give volcanic ash and dating.
- **Pollen cores**: which plants grew, and therefore the biome, century by century.
- **Speleothems**: cave deposits recording rainfall regimes.

The correlations are real and dramatic. The **Medieval Climate Anomaly** (c. 950–1250) coincides with Norse settlement of Greenland and European agricultural expansion. The **Little Ice Age** (c. 1300–1850) coincides with famine, the abandonment of Greenland, and the seventeenth-century general crisis. The 536 CE dust veil — a volcanic event that dimmed the sun for over a year — coincides with famine and plague across Eurasia.

> **And here is the discipline's hard-won rule about all of that: climate is a stressor, not a script.** The same Little Ice Age that helped wreck one polity found another that adapted, traded, or stored its way through. Climate changes the difficulty setting; institutions, reserves, and choices determine the outcome. The Norse abandoned Greenland while the Inuit, in the identical climate, did not — which is the single most useful fact in this lesson, because it kills determinism with a controlled experiment nature happened to run for us.

**The methodological trap is correlation.** Two dates lining up is a hypothesis, not a finding — and paleoclimate series are now precise enough that you can _always_ find a climatic wobble near any event you care about. The demand is the same as ever: show the mechanism. Harvest yields, grain prices, mortality records, migration. A cold decade next to a revolt is a coincidence until you can trace how the cold reached the crowd.

The animation opens on the Little Ice Age arriving over Greenland as a falling temperature curve, and two settlements sitting underneath it — Norse and Inuit — in the same place, in the same century, under the identical cold.

The Norse column builds first: cattle and sheep, hay meadows, turf byres, a European farming package carried north and held onto. As the curve descends, the growing season shortens and the hay yield bar drops. The animation traces the mechanism rather than asserting it — shorter season, less hay, fewer cattle overwintered, less milk, more hunger, and the trade link to Norway thinning at the same time. The settlement empties.

The Inuit column runs beside it under the same curve. Ringed seal, sea ice, kayaks, dogs, a technology aimed at the sea rather than the meadow. Cold _lengthens_ the ice season. The same descending curve that starves the hay meadow extends the hunting platform, and the second bar rises while the first falls.

$$\text{same climate} + \text{different subsistence} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{opposite outcomes}$$

The final beat holds both columns side by side under one temperature line, and states the thing plainly: the cold did not decide anything. It changed the difficulty setting. What the Norse could not do was stop being Norse farmers — and that was a choice about identity, not a fact about the weather.

Climate is a stressor, not a script. Nature ran the controlled experiment and the answer survived.

## Maps and Projections

This is not a complaint about cartographers. It is a theorem: **a sphere cannot be flattened onto a plane without distortion.** Gauss proved the general form of it — curved and flat surfaces have different intrinsic geometry, and no amount of cleverness gets around it.

So every projection chooses which property to save and which to sacrifice:

| Preserved | Name | Sacrificed |
|---|---|---|
| Angles / local shape | **Conformal** (Mercator) | Area, badly, toward the poles |
| Area | **Equal-area** (Peters, Mollweide) | Shape |
| Distance from a point | **Equidistant** | Nearly everything else |
| Nothing exactly | **Compromise** (Robinson, Winkel) | A little of each, on purpose |

**There is no neutral map.** Not "we haven't found one yet" — there cannot be one. Which means the choice of projection is always an argument, and the only question is whether it was made deliberately.

## Mercator Was Right (For His Job)

Mercator's 1569 projection is routinely denounced, usually by people who have not asked what it was for. It has a genuinely beautiful property: **a line of constant compass bearing is a straight line on the map.** For a sixteenth-century navigator that is not a convenience, it is the entire problem solved — you draw a straight line, read the bearing, and hold it.

The cost is that area inflates with latitude, and near the poles it inflates without bound. Greenland ends up looking the size of Africa when Africa is about **fourteen times larger**.

> **So Mercator is not a bad map. It is a superb navigational chart that spent four centuries hanging on classroom walls, doing a job it was never designed for.** The scandal is not the projection; it is the promotion of a specialized tool to the status of "what the world looks like" — and then the fact that the tool systematically flatters high latitudes, which is where the people printing the maps happened to live.

Peters (1973) preserves area and mangles shape, and was marketed explicitly as a political corrective. Cartographers largely disliked it — its shapes are genuinely poor, and equal-area projections had existed for centuries without the sales pitch. But the argument it started was the right one, and it worked: nobody puts Mercator on a classroom wall innocently any more.

## The Rest of the Arguments

Projection is only the mathematical layer. The rhetorical ones are simpler and often louder:

- **Centring.** Nothing is at the centre of a sphere's surface. Whatever you put there is a choice, and it reads as the middle of the world.
- **Orientation.** North-up is a convention, not a fact. Medieval T-O maps put east at the top (hence "orientation" — toward the _orient_); Islamic maps often put south up.
- **Naming.** Whose name, in whose language. A place with two names on a map has a border running through it.
- **Line weight.** A firm border and a dotted one are two claims about the same disputed line.
- **Blankness.** The famous one. Colonial maps' empty interiors were full of people — the blank was an assertion that nothing there merited recording, and an invitation to whoever arrived with the map.

## Maps as Instruments of Power

Mapping is not describing; it is often the act by which a claim becomes administrable. The cadastral survey exists to tax land, and it must first make land legible: fixed boundaries, unique owners, measured areas — replacing tangles of customary, overlapping, seasonal rights that no column could hold. Once the survey is done, the customary rights are not merely unrecorded. They are **unrecognized**, because the register has become the reality the state can see.

The colonial version is starker. Borders drawn through territories the mapmakers had never visited became the borders of states, and remain so. The map preceded the reality, and then produced it.

**The historian's discipline is therefore to read a map as a source, not a backdrop:** who made it, for whom, what does it save and sacrifice, what is at the centre, what is named, what is blank — and above all, what did it _make possible_ that was not possible before it was drawn.

The animation begins with the theorem, shown rather than stated: a globe's graticule is peeled and pressed toward a flat page, and it tears. It tears again, differently, at every attempt. The caption is short — a sphere has no flat copy, so every map is a bargain.

Then Mercator's bargain gets built. The cylinder wraps the globe; the parallels stretch as they climb; and crucially the animation shows _why_ the stretch is not arbitrary — to keep angles true, the vertical stretch must match the horizontal one at every latitude, so the map buys conformality by paying in area, and the bill grows with latitude.

The payoff is demonstrated where it deserves to be. A navigator's rhumb line — constant compass bearing — curves absurdly across the globe, then snaps into a perfectly straight line on the Mercator sheet. This is the sixteenth century's hardest problem, solved. The animation lets that land before it collects.

Then the bill arrives. A Greenland-shaped tile lifts off the map and slides down to the equator, shrinking as it descends, until it settles beside Africa at its true size:

$$\text{Africa} \approx 14 \times \text{Greenland}$$

The final movement is the argument. The Mercator sheet is re-framed as what it is — a navigational chart, superb at its job — and then a classroom wall assembles around it, four centuries of it, flattering exactly the latitudes where the maps were printed. The closing caption does not blame the projection: the scandal was promoting a specialized instrument to "what the world looks like", and forgetting there was ever a bargain at all.

## Demography and Migration

For most of human history, births were high and deaths were high, and the population barely moved. Then, society by society and starting in eighteenth-century Europe, something extraordinary happened — and it happened in a specific order that is the whole point.

| Stage | Births | Deaths | Population |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1 · Pre-transition** | High | High | Flat, with brutal fluctuations |
| **2 · Death rates fall** | High | Falling | **Explodes** |
| **3 · Birth rates fall** | Falling | Low | Grows, decelerating |
| **4 · Post-transition** | Low | Low | Flat again, at a vastly higher level |

**The lag is everything.** Deaths fall first — better food, clean water, sanitation, later medicine — and births keep going at the old rate for a generation or more, because fertility is governed by habits, norms, and economics that adjust far more slowly than mortality responds to a sewer. That gap between the curves _is_ the population explosion. It is not a failure of anyone's self-control; it is the arithmetic of two curves moving at different speeds.

> **Every population boom in modern history is stage 2, and every stage 2 ends.** This is worth stating plainly because the panic is so reliable: Europe's own explosion in the nineteenth century produced the same alarms about unstoppable multiplication now aimed elsewhere — and Europe finished its transition, as did Japan, then Korea, then Iran, whose fertility fell faster than any Western country's ever had. The pattern is a transition, not a trait.

## Why Births Fall

Not mainly because of contraceptive technology — the technology usually arrives after the decline is under way, which is a fact fatal to a lot of confident theories. The real drivers are about **the value of a child**:

- **Child mortality falls**, so you no longer need six children to keep three.
- **Children become expensive**: schooling costs money and takes years, and child labour disappears from a modern economy.
- **The economic direction reverses.** On a farm a child adds hands; in a city a child consumes for eighteen years.
- **Women's education and options**, the single strongest correlate anywhere in the data.
- **Old-age security** stops depending on surviving sons.

## Migration: Push, Pull, and the Two Things Everyone Forgets

The push–pull frame is standard — famine, war, and persecution push; wages, land, and safety pull. But it is incomplete in two ways that matter:

- **Networks.** People do not migrate to the best destination; they migrate to where someone they know already is. A single successful pioneer generates a chain, which is why migration flows are so lumpy — one Italian village to one Massachusetts town, over decades. The destination was not optimal, it was _known_.
- **The middle move.** The poorest rarely migrate the furthest, because leaving is expensive. Migration usually rises with development at first — as people can finally afford to go — which means development in a sending region tends to _increase_ emigration before reducing it. Nearly every policy built on the opposite assumption has failed.

**Migration is also overwhelmingly normal.** The great named migrations get the attention, but most movement is short-range, circular, and repeated — seasonal labour, marriage, apprenticeship, return. The sedentary peasant who never left the village is largely a nineteenth-century fiction, and parish registers demolished it the moment anyone bothered to link the names.

## Cultural Geography and Diffusion

A **cultural hearth** is where something originates — agriculture in the Fertile Crescent, in China, in Mesoamerica, in the Andes, independently. Writing in Mesopotamia, in China, in Mesoamerica. Independent invention is real and it is common, which matters, because the alternative assumption — that every good idea was invented once and carried outward by a superior people — was the load-bearing beam of a century of diffusionist racism.

From a hearth, things spread. **How** they spread is the interesting part.

| Type | Mechanism | Signature in the record |
|---|---|---|
| **Relocation** | People physically carry it | Appears at the destination, gap in between |
| **Expansion — contagious** | Spreads to whatever is adjacent | Ink-blot; distance-decay from the origin |
| **Expansion — hierarchical** | Jumps down a settlement hierarchy | Big cities first, countryside last — _regardless of distance_ |
| **Expansion — stimulus** | The idea travels, the form is rebuilt | Same concept, unrelated execution |

**Hierarchical diffusion is the one that catches people out**, because it defeats the intuition that near things arrive first. Fashion, plague along trade routes, new technology, and slang all land in the largest node first and reach an adjacent village last. If your map shows a phenomenon in the four biggest cities and nowhere between them, you are not looking at a gap in the evidence — you are looking at the mechanism.

**Stimulus diffusion is the subtlest and the most flattering to the receiver.** Sequoyah saw that English marks on paper carried speech, and built a Cherokee syllabary — he could not read English, so he took the _concept_ and invented an entirely independent system. That is not imitation; it is invention triggered by knowing a thing is possible. Half the "influences" in art history are this, and calling them copies gets the causation exactly backwards.

## Reception Is Not Passive

Nothing diffuses unchanged. Adoption is a negotiation, and the receiver has terms:

- **What arrives is reworked** to fit existing structures — the syncretism of religion is the standard case, where the local saint is older than the church.
- **Selective adoption is normal.** Societies take the crossbow and refuse the theology, take the script and reject the god.
- **Rejection is a decision, not a failure.** Tokugawa Japan had firearms, made excellent ones, and then substantially abandoned them for two centuries. That is not backwardness; that is a functioning polity deciding a weapon that lets a peasant kill a samurai in an afternoon is politically intolerable.

> **The cultural landscape is the residue of all this.** Field shapes, house forms, street grids, boundary hedges, the placement of the church — a landscape is a text written by everyone who ever made a decision on it and never erased by any of them. Carl Sauer's formulation still holds: culture is the agent, the natural area is the medium, the cultural landscape is the result. And that landscape is legible — a street plan can tell you about a colonial administration that fell two centuries ago, because street plans outlive the states that draw them.

## Political Geography and Borders

A **frontier** is a zone. A **border** is a line. Almost every political space in history was the first thing, and the modern world is built on the assumption it was always the second.

Pre-modern authority faded with distance. Power was strong at the centre, weaker further out, and somewhere in the mountains it became a matter of negotiation, tribute, and periodic reminder. Two empires could both plausibly claim the same valley, and the valley could pay both, or neither, depending on the year. There was no line because a line answers a question — _exactly where does one sovereignty stop?_ — that nobody needed answered.

The line arrives with the state that needs to tax, conscript, and administer uniformly. Then it arrives everywhere, fast, and the world is partitioned:

| Type | Definition | Trouble |
|---|---|---|
| **Antecedent** | Drawn before dense settlement | Often the calmest |
| **Subsequent** | Evolved with settlement, following existing divisions | Fits the people; fits the terrain poorly |
| **Superimposed** | Imposed over an existing cultural landscape | Cuts through nations — the colonial signature |
| **Relict** | Abandoned, but still visible | Shows up in voting maps for centuries |

**Relict boundaries are the eeriest thing in this field.** The border between Prussian and Russian Poland disappeared in 1918. It is still visible in Polish electoral maps, in road density, in rail gauge legacies — a line that has not existed for over a century still organizing behaviour, because a border builds institutions and institutions outlive the border by generations.

## The Straight Line Problem

Look at a map of Africa and the straight lines are unmistakable. About 30% of African land borders are straight lines drawn on projections by people who had not been there, and they partition ethnic groups roughly 40% of the time.

But the honest version is more interesting than "colonial borders caused Africa's conflicts":

- The lines are real damage, and they were drawn at conferences by men naming rivers they could not have found.
- **Yet post-independence African states almost universally kept them** — the OAU adopted the inherited borders as a founding principle, on the entirely sane grounds that opening the question would be worse than the answer.
- And partitioned groups are not reliably more conflict-prone than unpartitioned ones. The effect is real, contingent, and much smaller than the rhetorical use made of it.

> **The general lesson: a line drawn in ignorance becomes real by being administered.** Two sides of an arbitrary border accumulate different currencies, schools, languages of administration, legal systems, and road networks — and after sixty years the arbitrary line is describing a genuine difference it created. The map preceded the reality, and then produced it.

## Geopolitics: A Warning

Mackinder's Heartland thesis (1904) — that whoever controls the Eurasian interior controls the world — is the founding text of geopolitics, and it is a superb illustration of the genre's problem. It was elegant, influential, and read as a law of nature. German _Geopolitik_ took it up and welded it to _Lebensraum_, and the result was an academic theory functioning as a justification for conquest.

The trouble with geopolitical laws is that they present a strategic argument as geography. Distance and terrain are real constraints; "he who controls X controls the world" is a policy dressed as a fact — and a theory that tells a state what it must do, in the language of inevitability, is a theory doing political work while claiming to be a map.

## Urban Geography and City Systems

A city is never alone. It sits in a system of cities, and the system has a shape regular enough to be startling.

**The rank-size rule:** in a mature urban system, the $n$th largest city has about $1/n$ the population of the largest. Second city, half. Fourth city, a quarter. Tenth, a tenth. It is a rough empirical regularity, not a law — but it holds across wildly different countries and centuries, which is the sort of thing that demands an explanation nobody has fully supplied.

$$P_n \approx \frac{P_1}{n}$$

**The interesting cases are the violations.** When a system does not fit, it is telling you something:

- **Primate city:** the largest is far bigger than rank-size predicts — often several times the second city. Paris, London, Buenos Aires, Seoul, Bangkok. This is the signature of a system organized around a single centre: a centralized state, an imperial capital, or a colonial economy built to funnel exports through one port. A primate city is usually a political fact wearing a demographic costume.
- **Missing middle:** the classic colonial pattern. One huge port and lots of villages, with no mid-sized cities — because the economy was built to extract to the coast, and mid-sized cities are what an _internal_ economy grows. What is absent from the distribution testifies as loudly as what is present.

## Why Cities Sit Where They Sit

**Central place theory** (Christaller, 1933) is the classic account, and its logic is simple and powerful. Goods and services have a **range** (how far someone will travel for one) and a **threshold** (the minimum population needed to sustain one). A bakery has a small range and small threshold, so bakeries are everywhere. A cardiac surgery unit has a huge range and huge threshold, so there are few, in big cities, far apart.

Stack those and you get a **hierarchy**: many small centres offering low-order goods, fewer large centres offering everything the small ones do _plus_ high-order goods. On a uniform plain the market areas tile into hexagons, which is where the theory becomes both beautiful and obviously false — no plain is uniform, no transport is isotropic, and rivers exist. But the hierarchy logic survives the caricature, and it explains why the number of cities offering a service falls so predictably as the service gets rarer.

| Model | Claim | Fits |
|---|---|---|
| **Concentric zone** (Burgess) | Rings outward from the CBD | Industrial Chicago, roughly, in 1925 |
| **Sector** (Hoyt) | Wedges along transport routes | Cities where a rail line or river dominates |
| **Multiple nuclei** (Harris–Ullman) | Several centres, not one | Modern car-based cities |

All three are Chicago-shaped, and generalize badly — a European city with a medieval core, or a city whose rich live in the centre rather than the suburbs, breaks Burgess immediately. Use them as vocabulary, not as laws.

> **The deepest point about cities:** they are the densest artefact humans make, and the density is the point. Cities exist because proximity is productive — it lowers the cost of exchange, matching, and imitation, and it always has. This is also why cities have historically been demographic sinks: until sanitation, cities killed more people than they bore and grew _only_ by continuous in-migration from the countryside. A pre-modern city is a place that consumes people, and they came anyway, because it was still the best offer available.

## Economic Geography and Trade Routes

Before cheap transport, only certain things could cross a continent, and the filter was ruthless: **value per unit weight**. If a cargo cannot pay for a thousand kilometres of camel, it does not go a thousand kilometres.

| Cargo | Value density | Range |
|---|---|---|
| **Silk, spices, gems, gold** | Enormous | Anywhere |
| **Slaves** | High, and self-transporting | Anywhere |
| **Manufactured metalwork** | Moderate | Regional |
| **Wine, oil** | Modest — but amphorae go by sea | Coastal, far; inland, short |
| **Grain** | Low | Sea only; overland it eats itself |

**Grain is the instructive one.** Move grain far enough overland and the oxen consume the cargo — the transport eats the freight. Which is why every pre-modern city large enough to need imported bread had to be on water, without exception, and why Rome fed itself from Egypt and North Africa rather than from Italy fifty kilometres inland.

So pre-modern long-distance trade is systematically biased toward luxuries. Not because merchants were frivolous, but because luxuries were the only things the arithmetic permitted to travel. **A trade route is a value-density filter, and the goods you find on it are the ones that passed.**

## The Silk Road Was Not a Road

Two corrections, and the second is the interesting one.

First, it was not a road but a shifting web of routes across oases, and the name was coined in **1877** by Ferdinand von Richthofen — nobody who ever travelled it called it that.

Second, and more importantly: **almost nobody travelled its length.** Goods did; people mostly did not. Silk moved from hand to hand through dozens of intermediaries, each moving it a few hundred kilometres and taking a margin. A bolt of silk reaching Rome had been traded twenty times, and no single merchant knew both ends. This is why Rome's knowledge of China was so spectacularly wrong: the goods arrived, the information did not, because information does not survive twenty relays even when the silk does.

> **The relay structure explains the whole system.** It explains the markups (each hand takes a cut), the ignorance at both ends, the resilience (no single point of failure, so a war closes one segment and the web reroutes), and the fact that the network's most consequential cargo was never intended: ideas, religions, and disease. Buddhism travelled the routes to China. So did the plague. **A trade network is a disease network with better public relations** — the same relays that pass a bolt of silk pass a flea, and the flea does not need a merchant who knows both ends either.

## The Container Broke the Filter

For most of history, transport costs shaped the map. Then, in 1956, Malcom McLean put cargo in standardized steel boxes, and the cost of moving goods collapsed toward irrelevance.

The effects were structural, not incremental:

- **Loading time collapsed** — the port, not the ocean, had always been the bottleneck. Break-bulk cargo was handled piece by piece by armies of dockers; a container is one crane movement.
- **Value density stopped mattering.** When shipping is nearly free per unit, you can ship anything anywhere. Grain crosses oceans. So does bottled water, which is a genuinely absurd thing for the arithmetic to permit.
- **Location de-anchored.** Manufacturing could go anywhere with labour and a port, because proximity to the market stopped being a cost worth paying for.
- **Old ports died and new ones appeared** wherever the water was deep enough for the ships and the land flat enough for the stacks. The great labour-intensive dock districts emptied — a container terminal is a car park with cranes.

**The historical point is what this reveals:** almost everything that looked like a permanent law of economic geography — luxuries travel, bulk does not; industry clusters near markets; cities cling to natural harbours — was a statement about the price of moving things. Change the price and the "laws" evaporate within a generation. Geography did not change in 1956. The price list did, and it turned out the price list had been doing all the work.

## GIS and Spatial Analysis

The founding story: in 1854, John Snow mapped cholera deaths in Soho and found them clustered around the Broad Street pump. He had the handle removed. The outbreak ended.

The story is told as data visualization defeating superstition, and nearly every part of that telling is wrong in an instructive way. **The outbreak was already declining** when the handle came off. And the map did not generate Snow's hypothesis — he already believed cholera was waterborne, and drew the map to _argue_ it against a medical establishment committed to miasma.

What the map genuinely did is more interesting than the legend: it made a spatial argument **checkable**. Its power was in the anomalies — the brewery workers who drank beer and did not die, the widow who had Broad Street water delivered across town and died of it. Those are the cases that no miasma theory could accommodate, and you can only _see_ them once the deaths are on a map with the pumps.

> **That is exactly what GIS does. It does not answer questions; it makes spatial arguments visible, checkable, and falsifiable** — and it is worth being clear that Snow's map was a rhetorical instrument, deployed in an argument he was already having.

## Layers, and the Question They Enable

GIS is layers over shared coordinates: terrain, rivers, roads, settlements, soils, parishes, deaths, prices. **The value is in the overlay** — each layer is ordinary, and the relationships between them are where the history is.

| Operation | The question it answers |
|---|---|
| **Overlay** | What coincides with what? |
| **Buffer** | What is within X of this? |
| **Least-cost path** | Where would movement actually go, given terrain? |
| **Viewshed** | What can be seen from here? |
| **Density / clustering** | Is this pattern more concentrated than chance? |

**Least-cost path deserves emphasis** because it is the one that changes historical practice. Straight-line distance is nearly meaningless in real terrain: what matters is the cost surface — slope, rivers, marsh, season. Compute the cheapest route across it and Roman roads frequently fall out of the model, which is both a validation and a tool. Where the model and the road disagree, you have found something: a reason that was not about cost. Politics, ritual, an estate someone owned, a decision.

**Viewshed** answers questions that were previously pure assertion. Could that signal tower see the next one? Was this castle sited to be seen, or to see? Put the observer at the parapet and the model tells you, and a good deal of confident writing about "commanding positions" has not survived the test.

## Georeferencing, and the Trap It Sets

Historical maps must be stretched onto modern coordinates to join the layers — which requires control points, and imports every one of the old map's errors into a system that displays them with perfect crispness.

**This is the central danger of the whole enterprise.** GIS output looks authoritative in a way that spreadsheets do not. A map renders a guess and a survey identically, and puts a boundary on a screen at sub-metre precision when the source was a sketch. The parish boundary you digitized was approximate, seasonal, and disputed; on your screen it is a crisp line, and three papers later it is a fact.

The related trap is the **ecological fallacy**: an aggregate pattern does not license a claim about individuals. Districts with more X have more Y does not mean the people with X have Y. Spatial data invites this error constantly, because the unit you can map is almost never the unit you want to talk about.

**The rule is the one from the digital-humanities lesson, restated:** the method changes the scale, not the epistemology. Ask the layer what you would ask a document — who made it, why, what is missing, at what precision. A crisp line is a claim about evidence, and GIS will render your assumptions beautifully.

## Geography and History

**Environmental determinism** says the physical environment determines culture, character, and historical outcome. It is very old — Hippocrates had a version, Montesquieu had a version — and it has been discredited, revived, discredited, and revived again, which is worth noticing. Ideas that keep coming back are answering a demand.

The demand is obvious once you say it out loud. Determinism explains inequality without implicating anybody. If the tropics are poor because of the tropics, then nobody did anything. It is an alibi with a map.

Its high-water mark was academically respectable and morally grotesque: Ellsworth Huntington's climatic energy, Ratzel's _Lebensraum_ — a state as an organism requiring space to grow, which fed directly into German _Geopolitik_ and from there into a program of conquest. This is not guilt by association. The theory was doing exactly the work it was built to do.

## Possibilism, and Its Limit

**Possibilism** (Vidal de la Blache) is the corrective: the environment offers possibilities and constraints; culture chooses among them. Same island, two peoples, two outcomes — Norse and Inuit under one Little Ice Age, which is the entire argument in one controlled experiment.

But possibilism, pushed hard, becomes uninformative. "The environment offers possibilities and humans choose" is true and explains nothing — a theory that can never be wrong is not doing work. If geography permits everything, why do we keep finding patterns?

## Where This Has Actually Landed

Not in the middle for the sake of the middle, but on a specific and defensible claim: **geography acts on institutions and costs, not on people.**

- **It sets a price list.** Terrain, water, and distance make things cheap or expensive, and cost differences compound over centuries into different outcomes.
- **It acts through institutions.** The persuasive modern work — on the disease environment, on colonial settlement patterns — argues that geography shaped what institutions got built, and the institutions did the rest. The mechanism runs through human arrangements, always.
- **It is contingent on technology.** Containerization erased laws of economic geography in a generation. A river is an obstacle or a highway depending on whether you have boats — and geography's effects are not fixed because the price list is not fixed.
- **It never acts on character.** No climate makes a people industrious. This is where determinism always went, and it is where determinism was always empty.

> **The test that settles it:** find two societies in the same environment with different outcomes. If you can, determinism is dead for that case. Greenland hands us this test on a plate — Norse and Inuit, same island, same century, same cold, opposite fates — and the outcome turned on subsistence strategy and cultural commitment, not on temperature. Nature ran the controlled experiment. The determinists lost it.

## The Working Formulation

Jared Diamond's continental-axis argument is the honourable modern version, and the right way to hold it is instructive. Its **question** is excellent: why did the conquest run in that direction rather than the other? Its **mechanism** is real: an east–west axis lets a domesticate package ride one climate band for thousands of kilometres, which compounds into density, herd animals, and zoonoses. And its **problem** is that it is a geographic explanation for a chain long enough that human decisions had ten thousand years to intervene — and they did.

So: **geography sets the price list. People choose. The choices compound.** All three clauses are load-bearing. Drop the first and you cannot explain the patterns; drop the second and you have an alibi with a map; drop the third and you cannot explain why small differences in cost became civilizational differences in outcome. That is not a compromise. It is the answer.
