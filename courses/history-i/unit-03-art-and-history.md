## Methodologies of Art History

For a historian, a painting is not decoration but a **document** — one that records what a society could imagine, what it paid for, and what it wanted believed. Reading it takes method, because images lie differently from texts: they persuade without arguing, and they omit without admitting it.

## Panofsky's Three Levels

Erwin Panofsky's scheme remains the discipline's backbone. Each level asks a harder question and needs more outside knowledge:

| Level | Question | Requires |
|---|---|---|
| **Pre-iconographic** | What do I literally see? | Ordinary experience |
| **Iconographic** | What story or figure is this? | Knowledge of texts and conventions |
| **Iconological** | Why this, here, now? | The whole cultural moment |

A worked example: _a woman holding a wheel_ (pre-iconographic) is _Saint Catherine of Alexandria_, identified by her attribute (iconographic), painted for a guild whose patron she was, in a city asserting its piety against a rival's (iconological). Level one anyone can do; level three is history.

## Four Complementary Lenses

- **Formal analysis** — line, color, composition, light, space. The disciplined description of how the object works on the eye, before any meaning is claimed.
- **Style and connoisseurship** — the fingerprint of a period, region, or hand. Powerful for attribution and dating; treacherous when it slides into ranking.
- **Patronage** — who paid, and what did they want? Almost no pre-modern art was made speculatively; the contract often survives, specifying pigment, size, and deadline.
- **Social art history** — art as a product of its conditions: workshops, guilds, markets, politics. T. J. Clark's question: not what the painting means, but what work it _did_.

> **The historian's warning:** art shows the world its patrons wanted seen. Tomb paintings show abundant harvests and never famine; portraits show sitters at their most composed. Surviving art is also a **survival bias** — stone temples outlast wooden ones, and the art of the rich outlasts the art of everyone else. Read images as arguments, not as photographs.

## Prehistoric Art

The oldest art poses the hardest interpretive problem: superb images, and not one word of explanation. Prehistoric art is where method matters most, because the temptation to project is strongest.

## The Record

| Site / form | Date (approx.) | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **Blombos Cave ochre** | 75,000 BP | Engraved cross-hatching: abstraction, not depiction |
| **Sulawesi & Borneo cave art** | 45,000+ BP | Figurative painting outside Europe — the "European revolution" was never only European |
| **Chauvet** | ~36,000 BP | Masterful lions, rhinos; the oldest great painted cave |
| **Lascaux** | ~17,000 BP | The famous bulls; Magdalenian peak |
| **Altamira** | ~15,000 BP | Polychrome bison; dismissed as forgery for 20 years |
| **Venus figurines** | 30,000–20,000 BP | Portable art from the Pyrenees to Siberia |

Two lessons jump from the table. First, this art is not "primitive": Chauvet's lions use shading, perspective, and even sequential images suggesting motion — and Chauvet is _twice as old_ as Lascaux, so the story is not one of steady improvement. Second, the art is not European: Sulawesi's animals are as old as anything in France. What changed around 45,000 years ago changed across the human world.

## What Was It For?

Every generation reads its own preoccupations into the caves:

- **Art for art's sake** (19th c.) — idle beauty. Fails: the images are in dark, dangerous, deliberately inaccessible chambers, not in the living areas.
- **Hunting magic** (Breuil) — paint it to catch it. Fails on the data: the painted species often do not match the bones in the middens. They ate reindeer and painted horses.
- **Shamanism** (Lewis-Williams) — images from altered states at the membrane between worlds. Fits the deep-cave placement and the geometric motifs; but is very hard to falsify.
- **Information storage / totemism** — clan emblems, seasonal knowledge, myth. Plausible, partially testable.

> **The honest position:** the caves are places where people went to great effort in the dark, repeatedly, for tens of thousands of years, and what they made there was serious. That much the evidence proves. Which story it served, no cave will ever say — and any interpretation that fits _all_ the art from Spain to Siberia across 30,000 years is almost certainly too loose to be wrong.

## Art of the Ancient Near East and Egypt

In Mesopotamia and Egypt, art and the state arrive together. These are the first societies with kings, taxes, and writing — and their art exists largely to make that arrangement look eternal. The images are not illustrations of power; they are instruments of it.

## Rules Before Realism

Egyptian figures look "wrong" to modern eyes and were meant to. The **canon of proportions** ruled a grid — 18 squares from sole to hairline in the classic system — so that any trained artist anywhere in Egypt made the same figure, for three thousand years. Combined with **composite view**, each body part appears from its most identifiable angle: head in profile, eye frontal, shoulders frontal, legs in profile. The goal is not what a body _looks like_ from one spot, but what a body _is_ — a complete, permanent statement, fit for eternity.

**Hierarchical scale** completes the grammar: size means status, not distance. The pharaoh towers, the wife reaches his knee, the servants are ankle-high — a diagram of the social order printed as a picture. When you see a giant figure in ancient art, you are reading rank, not perspective.

## The Monuments Argue

| Work | Date | The argument it makes |
|---|---|---|
| **Warka Vase** | ~3200 BCE | Tiered registers: gods above, ruler, then offerings — the cosmos as a hierarchy |
| **Standard of Ur** | ~2600 BCE | War on one side, peace on the other: the two faces of kingship |
| **Stele of Naram-Sin** | ~2250 BCE | The king climbs above his army, horned as a god — a breach of the old rules |
| **Code of Hammurabi** | ~1750 BCE | The king receives law from Shamash: legislation as revelation |
| **Ziggurat of Ur** | ~2100 BCE | A mountain built in a flat land — the temple visible from the horizon |

> **Reading against the grain:** these works record the _claims_ of power, never its limits. Naram-Sin's stele shows an invincible god-king; his empire collapsed within a generation, and a later Elamite raid carried the stele off as loot — where archaeologists found it, in Susa. The historian's task is to notice both the claim and its fate, and never to confuse a monument's confidence with the world's compliance.

## Classical Greek Art

Around 480 BCE Greek sculpture does something no tradition had done: it lets a figure **shift its weight**. The archaic _kouros_ had stood rigid and frontal, both feet planted, a cousin of the Egyptian figures that inspired it. Then the _Kritios Boy_ rests his weight on one leg — and the whole body responds. The hip rises over the engaged leg, the shoulders counter-tilt, the spine curves gently, the head turns.

This is **contrapposto**, and it is not a trick of anatomy but a change of subject: the figure now exists in a _moment_, capable of moving, conscious of standing. Polykleitos codified the achievement in his **Canon** — a treatise, now lost, demonstrated by his _Doryphoros_ — arguing that beauty lies in _symmetria_, the commensurable ratio of every part to every other and to the whole.

## Idealism, Not Portraiture

Classical figures are nobody. The faces are calm at the moment of maximum exertion, the bodies are perfected composites, the individual is refined away. The subject is not this athlete but **the athlete** — humanity as it might be. (Portraiture of actual, wrinkled individuals waits for the Hellenistic age and arrives in force with Rome.)

## The Orders and the Parthenon

| Order | Capital | Character |
|---|---|---|
| **Doric** | Plain cushion | Sturdy, unornamented, no base |
| **Ionic** | Scrolled volutes | Slender, elegant, with base |
| **Corinthian** | Acanthus leaves | Ornate, late-blooming, adored by Rome |

The Parthenon (447–432 BCE) is the argument's summit — and its most famous feature is a deception. Its **refinements** correct optical illusions: the columns swell slightly at mid-height (_entasis_, so they don't look pinched), they lean inward, the corner columns are thicker (seen against bright sky, they'd otherwise look thin), and the platform _curves upward_ in the middle by about 11 cm across 70 meters, so it will not appear to sag.

> **The point of the refinements:** hardly a straight line exists in the building. Every one of those adjustments exists so the temple will _look_ perfectly regular — the Greeks corrected reality to satisfy perception. That priority, appearance over measurement, is the philosophical core of the whole classical project: the ideal is truer than the actual.

The animation sets an archaic _kouros_ beside the _Kritios Boy_ and moves the weight. Watch the chain reaction: as the body shifts onto one leg, the pelvis tilts up on the engaged side, the shoulders tilt the _opposite_ way, and the spine draws its gentle S. Nothing else has changed — one decision propagates through the whole figure.

$$\text{weight on one leg} \Rightarrow \text{hip} \uparrow \;,\; \text{shoulder} \downarrow \;,\; \text{spine} \sim$$

Then the axis lines overlay: the horizontal of the hips and the horizontal of the shoulders, once parallel in the rigid kouros, now cross. That crossing _is_ contrapposto — the visual proof that this body has a moment, a choice, and an interior. Sculpture stopped depicting a shape and started depicting a person standing.

## Roman Art and Engineering

Greek architecture is **post-and-lintel**: columns hold up a stone beam. The beam is the problem — stone is strong in compression but weak in tension, so a lintel cracks under its own weight past a few meters. That is why Greek temples are forests of columns: they _had_ to be.

The **arch** dissolves the limit. Wedge-shaped voussoirs transfer load sideways and down as pure compression, with the **keystone** locking the ring; the thrust travels to the ground through the piers. Extend an arch and you get a **barrel vault**; cross two and you get a **groin vault**; spin one around and you get a **dome**. Rome now had spans no lintel could dream of, and interior space became the subject of architecture.

## Concrete: The Force Multiplier

Roman **concrete** (_opus caementicium_) — lime, volcanic ash (_pozzolana_), aggregate — could be poured into any form, cured underwater, and was laid by unskilled labor over a skilled framework. The Pantheon's dome (~126 CE) spans **43 metres**, unreinforced, and remains the largest of its kind ever built; the builders graded the aggregate from heavy basalt at the base to light pumice at the crown, coffered the ceiling to shed weight, and left the oculus open. Nineteen centuries later it still stands.

## Portraits That Admit Age

Roman portraiture inverts the Greek priority. Republican **verism** shows warts, jowls, receding hair, sagging necks — a face that has _served_, whose wrinkles are a resume of gravitas. Augustus then engineers a synthesis: the _Prima Porta_ statue gives him classical Greek idealized proportions and a face that never ages across forty years of images — perpetual youth as a policy of state.

| Work | Argument |
|---|---|
| **Republican veristic bust** | Age equals experience equals authority |
| **Augustus of Prima Porta** | Divine descent, eternal youth, victory |
| **Column of Trajan** | The whole Dacian war, narrated in a spiral |
| **Arch of Titus** | The Jerusalem menorah paraded — conquest as fact |

> **The historian's use:** Roman art is state media of extraordinary quality. The Column of Trajan's 155-scene spiral is the most detailed visual account of a Roman campaign in existence — and it is also a monument by the victor, in his forum, showing his war as he wished it remembered. Read it for the equipment, the engineering, the ritual; distrust it for the reasons and the numbers.

## Medieval Art and Architecture

Every stone-vaulted church faces the same enemy: **thrust**. A vault does not press straight down; it pushes _outward_ at its base, and unresisted, it will shove the walls apart and collapse. Romanesque and Gothic are two answers to that single force.

**Romanesque** (c. 1000–1150) answers with **mass**. Round arches, thick walls, small windows, heavy piers, barrel vaults. The wall itself resists the thrust, so the wall must stay solid — which means the interiors are dim, powerful, and cave-like. Every window is a hole in the structure, so windows stay small.

**Gothic** (from c. 1140) answers with **skeleton**. Three inventions work as a system:

| Element | Job |
|---|---|
| **Pointed arch** | Steeper thrust line — pushes more downward, less outward |
| **Rib vault** | Concentrates load onto ribs, then onto piers, not the whole wall |
| **Flying buttress** | Catches the remaining outward thrust and carries it over the aisle to an external pier |

Together they take the load _off the wall_. And the moment the wall is no longer structural, it can be dissolved into glass. Gothic height and Gothic light are not two achievements but one: the engineering exists to make the window possible.

## The Cathedral as Argument

Abbot Suger, rebuilding Saint-Denis around 1140, gave the movement its theology: light is the medium through which the divine becomes perceptible, so a church of light is a church of God. The stained glass that resulted was also the **encyclopedia of the illiterate** — Chartres alone carries some 176 windows and thousands of sculpted figures narrating scripture, saints, months, labors, and the liberal arts. The building is a total system of knowledge, readable by people who could not read.

> **A caution about "Gothic":** the name is an insult. Renaissance Italians coined it to mean _barbarian_ — the style of the Goths who destroyed Rome — and it stuck, long after the contempt faded. Half of art history's vocabulary is fossilized abuse (_Baroque_: misshapen pearl; _Impressionism_: a sneering review; _Fauves_: wild beasts). Style names record the winners of old arguments, not the makers' intentions.

## The Italian Renaissance

Around 1413 Brunelleschi ran a demonstration in front of the Florence Baptistery: a painted panel with a peephole, a mirror, and an illusion so exact that viewers could not tell the picture from the building. He had made **linear perspective** — not a style, but a geometry.

The system is deceptively simple. All lines running away from the viewer (**orthogonals**) converge on a single **vanishing point** on the **horizon line**, which sits at the viewer's eye level. Objects shrink with distance by a fixed ratio, so the painting becomes a **window**: a slice through the cone of vision from one eye, at one instant, standing at one spot. Alberti wrote it down in _Della pittura_ (1435) and turned a trick into a curriculum.

The philosophical payload is enormous. Perspective builds the picture _around a viewer_ — a single, located, human observer whose position organizes everything visible. Medieval images had ordered space by importance (bigger = holier); Renaissance images order it by _geometry_, from a human standpoint. The technique and the humanism are the same idea in two registers.

## The System That Made the Art

| Institution | Function |
|---|---|
| **Workshop (_bottega_)** | Master plus apprentices; a firm, not a garret |
| **Guild** | Regulated quality, training, and prices |
| **Contract** | Fixed size, deadline, pigments — even ultramarine's grade |
| **Patron** | Church, guild, commune, or dynasty; specified the program |

Nobody painted for self-expression: they filled orders. Ultramarine, ground from Afghan lapis lazuli, cost more than gold and appears in contracts by weight — which is why the Virgin's robe is blue. Theology helped; the invoice decided.

## The Arc

Giotto (c. 1300) reintroduces weight, grief, and legible human emotion — figures that occupy space and feel. Masaccio (1420s) fuses that solidity with the new perspective. Brunelleschi domes Florence's cathedral (1420–36) without centring, using a self-supporting herringbone brick pattern and a double shell. Leonardo dissolves the outlines Alberti's geometry made possible (_sfumato_), and the "Renaissance man" ideal — anatomy, engineering, painting, hydraulics in one notebook — arrives as its own kind of argument about human capacity.

> **The label is a claim, not a fact.** "Renaissance" (Vasari's _rinascita_, and Burckhardt's 1860 elaboration) asserts that antiquity was reborn after a "dark" middle — a story invented by the people who profited by it. Medieval Europe had its own renaissances (Carolingian, 12th-century), classical texts were preserved and advanced by Byzantine and Islamic scholars throughout, and the 1400s continued as much as they revived. The period name is a piece of Renaissance propaganda that historians still repeat daily.

The animation builds a perspective from nothing. First the **horizon line** appears at the viewer's eye level; then the **vanishing point** on it. Orthogonals rake out from that point across the picture plane, and a tiled floor materializes along them — each row of tiles compressing toward the horizon by exact ratio, never by eye.

$$\text{apparent size} \propto \frac{1}{\text{distance}}$$

Then the camera pulls back to reveal what the geometry really is: a cone of vision from a single eye, sliced by the picture plane. Move the eye — the animation does — and the whole construction shifts, because the picture was built _around_ that observer.

The final beat sets a medieval panel beside it: there, size means holiness and space serves meaning. Here, size means distance and space serves the viewer. The same wall, two centuries apart, and the entire relocation of authority from heaven to the human standpoint, drawn in ruled lines.

## Baroque Art and the Counter-Reformation

In 1563 the Council of Trent, closing its long answer to the Reformation, issued a decree on images. Protestants had whitewashed church walls and smashed statues, arguing that images were idols standing between the believer and God. Trent's reply was the opposite bet: images stay, and they must **teach, move, and persuade**. Art was assigned a job — reach the illiterate, fire the lukewarm, make doctrine _felt_.

**Baroque** is that brief executed. Where the High Renaissance sought balance, poise, and clarity, the Baroque wants **impact**: diagonal compositions instead of symmetrical ones, the peak instant of an action instead of the calm before it, and a viewer pulled into the picture rather than left to admire it from outside.

| Renaissance | Baroque |
|---|---|
| Balanced, pyramidal, frontal | Diagonal, spiralling, off-axis |
| The moment before | The instant of maximum drama |
| Even, rational light | **Tenebrism** — darkness with a shaft |
| Viewer observes | Viewer is implicated |

## Tenebrism and the Dirty Feet

Caravaggio's method is a light switch. Most of the canvas is near-black; a single raking beam picks out a hand, a face, a startled gesture. There is no gentle modelling — the transition from lit to unlit is a cliff. This is **tenebrism** (from _tenebrae_, darkness), and it does two things at once: it makes the picture legible from across a dim chapel, and it makes the illumination look like grace arriving.

His second weapon was scandal. Caravaggio painted saints as laborers — Matthew with a peasant's face, pilgrims with filthy soles turned toward the viewer, a dead Virgin modelled (rumor insisted) on a drowned prostitute. Patrons rejected canvases for indecorum. But the theology was ruthless: if the incarnation means God entered an actual body in an actual poor country, then dirty feet are not an insult to the sacred, they _are_ the sacred claim.

## Bernini and the Total Work

Bernini pushed further — dissolving the boundary between sculpture, architecture, painting, and the room. The _Ecstasy of Saint Teresa_ is not a statue in a chapel; it is a staged event. Concealed windows drop real daylight down gilded bronze rays. Marble is carved into cloth, cloud, and flesh at once. Sculpted members of the Cornaro family lean from theatre boxes on either wall, watching — so the viewer arrives to find the audience already in place, and joins it.

> **The historian's read:** Baroque splendour is a religious argument _and_ a budget. Every diagonal, every shaft, every gilded ray was paid for by a Church that had just lost half of Europe and was buying the other half back with spectacle. Protestant Amsterdam, meanwhile, produced small, sober canvases for merchants' parlors — same century, opposite theology, opposite market. Style follows the client.

## Realism and Impressionism

By 1800 the French Académie ran painting like a civil service, and it ranked subjects:

| Rank | Genre | Status |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **History painting** | Myth, scripture, antiquity — the only serious art |
| 2 | Portraiture | Respectable |
| 3 | Genre scenes | Everyday life; minor |
| 4 | Landscape | Decorative |
| 5 | **Still life** | Fruit. Barely art. |

The Salon was the only real venue, its jury the only real gatekeeper, and history painting the only route to a career. To be a serious painter meant painting gods.

**Courbet detonated the hierarchy in 1850.** _A Burial at Ornans_ shows a village funeral — no saint, no hero, no moral — painted across seven metres of canvas at the scale reserved for the death of kings. Nothing is idealized: the mourners are provincial, jowly, bored. The scandal was not the subject but the _size_. Courbet had said, in the only grammar the Académie understood, that a peasant's burial deserves the space of a coronation. "Show me an angel," he replied to his critics, "and I will paint one."

## Manet and the Optics of Modern Life

Manet's _Déjeuner sur l'herbe_ (1863) was refused and shown at the Salon des Refusés, where crowds came to laugh. A nude woman picnics with two clothed men in modern dress — and _she looks straight out at you_. Nude goddesses were entirely acceptable; a naked Parisian who has noticed you looking was not. Manet also flattened his modelling, killing the smooth academic gradations and leaving the picture assertively a _painted surface_.

## Impressionism: Perception Over Object

The Impressionists (first independent exhibition 1874; the name lifted from a hostile review of Monet's _Impression, Sunrise_) took the next step. Their subject is not the object but the **light striking it at one moment**.

The method follows: paint outdoors (_en plein air_, enabled by the collapsible metal paint tube from 1841 — a hardware invention with an art-historical consequence), work fast because the light is moving, drop black from the palette and build shadows from complementary colors, and leave the brushstroke visible rather than blending it away.

Monet's serial paintings make the thesis explicit: thirty views of Rouen Cathedral, same façade, different hours. The cathedral is not the subject. The **light** is the subject; the cathedral is just what it happened to fall on.

> **The technology behind the revolt:** photography (1839) took over the jobs of documentation and likeness within a generation. Painting could compete on accuracy and lose, or ask what only paint can do — perception, sensation, the visible act of painting. Every modernist move for the next century runs through that fork.

## Modernism and the Avant-Gardes

For five centuries the Western picture had been Alberti's **window**: one eye, one instant, one place. In 1907 Picasso's _Les Demoiselles d'Avignon_ smashed the pane. Faces turn to masks (drawn from African sculpture Picasso saw at the Trocadéro — an encounter Western histories long buried under the word "primitive"), bodies fracture into planes, and the space behind them refuses to recede.

Analytic Cubism (Picasso and Braque, 1909–12) then works the logic through. If a painting shows one viewpoint at one instant, it is lying about how we actually know an object — we walk around a jug; we remember its handle while looking at its lip. So show the viewpoints _simultaneously_: the guitar's face, its side, its soundhole, all at once, fractured into facets and reassembled on a surface that is honestly flat.

Then **collage** (1912) delivers the coup: Braque and Picasso glue in actual newspaper, oilcloth, chair caning. Real material enters the picture. The painting stops pretending to be a view through a wall and admits to being an object on one.

## The Logic Runs to Zero

| Move | Consequence |
|---|---|
| **Cubism** (1907–12) | One viewpoint abandoned; the surface admits it is flat |
| **Futurism** (1909) | Speed, machines, and the worship of the new |
| **Abstraction** (1910s) | Kandinsky, Malevich, Mondrian: no subject at all |
| **Dada** (1916) | The war made sense absurd; art attacks itself |
| **Surrealism** (1924) | Freud's unconscious as the picture's true source |

Malevich's _Black Square_ (1915) is the endpoint stated flatly: a black square on white, hung in the corner where a Russian home hangs its icon. Not a picture of nothing — a picture that is _only itself_, and the pointed replacement of one object of contemplation with another.

Duchamp's move is stranger and, in the long run, larger. In 1917 he submits a factory urinal, signed "R. Mutt", titled _Fountain_. He made nothing; he **chose** something, and the choice was the art. Every question the following century asks — what counts, who decides, does skill matter, is the institution part of the work — is already in that one object, which the exhibition committee promptly hid.

> **Why it happened when it did:** the avant-gardes are not a fashion cycle. Photography had taken likeness; industrial modernity had shattered the pace and scale of experience; Freud had claimed the mind was not transparent to itself; and the First World War killed the Enlightenment story that reason and progress were carrying Europe somewhere good. Dada is not childishness — it is what art looks like when a generation concludes that reason produced Verdun. **Art history is not a parade of styles. It is a record of what people believed was real, and what happened when they stopped believing it.**
