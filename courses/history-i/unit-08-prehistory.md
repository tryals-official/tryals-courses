# Unit 8: Prehistory

## Human Evolution and Hominization

Everyone assumes the brain led. It did not. The single most reliable finding in palaeoanthropology is that **bipedalism precedes encephalization by millions of years** — we walked upright for roughly three million years while carrying an ape-sized brain, and only then did the skull start inflating.

The evidence is unarguable. **Lucy** (_Australopithecus afarensis_, ~3.2 million years ago) walked fully upright on a brain of about 400 cc — chimpanzee range. And at **Laetoli**, three individuals crossed wet volcanic ash 3.6 million years ago and left footprints: a human stride, a human arch, no knuckle-marks. Ash hardened; feet became fossils.

This wrecks the story people expect — big brain first, then tools, then walking to carry them. The actual sequence:

| Stage | Roughly | Brain |
|---|---|---|
| Bipedalism established | 4+ Ma | ~400 cc — ape-sized |
| First flaked stone tools | 3.3–2.6 Ma | ~500–600 cc |
| Brain expansion accelerates | ~2 Ma onward | 600 → 1,350 cc |

> **Why walk first?** Nobody is certain, which is itself the honest lesson. Candidates: freeing the hands to carry food; efficiency over long distances as forests broke into patches; a smaller sunlit surface at midday; better sightlines over grass. Bipedalism is not obviously "better" — it is a compromise that wrecked our backs, our knees, and made childbirth dangerous. Evolution does not optimize. It tinkers with whatever variant survives long enough to breed.

## Brains Are Expensive

A modern human brain is about **2% of body weight and burns roughly 20% of resting energy.** No animal gets a bargain that steep for free. Something had to pay:

- **Meat and marrow** — dense calories, unlocked by tools, from carcasses other animals could not open.
- **Fire and cooking** (Richard Wrangham's argument) — cooking predigests food outside the body, so the gut can shrink and hand its energy budget to the skull. Note our guts are small for a primate our size.
- **Groups** — the "social brain" hypothesis: tracking who owes whom, who lies, who allies with whom is computationally brutal, and being outsmarted by your own species is a permanent arms race.

The bill also came due at birth. A wide pelvis carries a big head; a narrow pelvis walks efficiently. You cannot have both, so human babies are born **early and helpless** relative to other primates — which produces long childhood, extended learning, and the necessity of cooperative care. The awkwardness of human infancy is a direct structural consequence of walking upright with a large head.

## Not a Ladder — a Bush

The march-of-progress image is the most persistent lie in science illustration. There was never a single file of ancestors improving toward us. There was a **bush**: many hominin species, most of them dead ends, and **for almost all of the last few million years, several human species were alive at once.**

We are the strange case. _Homo sapiens_ appears in Africa by roughly **300,000 years ago** (Jebel Irhoud in Morocco pushed the date back and, importantly, showed a modern face on a still-elongated braincase — evolution in mosaic, not in lockstep). Neanderthals, Denisovans, _Homo floresiensis_ — all real, all human, all gone within the last 50,000 years. And they are not entirely gone: most people outside Africa carry a **1–4% Neanderthal** genetic inheritance.

Being the only human species on Earth is not the normal condition. It is a recent, temporary anomaly — and we are living in it.

## The Lower and Middle Paleolithic

The **Acheulean handaxe** is a teardrop of stone, worked on both faces, symmetrical. It is also the longest-running product in human history: it stays essentially **the same for over a million years**, from Africa to India to Britain, across at least two hominin species.

Sit with that. A million years. Every technology you own would be unrecognizable after a century. This design outlived species. And the puzzle is not that early humans were stupid — knapping a handaxe demands planning, a mental template of the finished object, and skill you cannot acquire without teaching. The puzzle is the opposite: **why so much competence with so little change?**

The honest answer is that we do not know, and the candidates are all interesting:

- Cultural transmission was high-fidelity but low-innovation — copying without deviating.
- The design was simply _sufficient_, and nothing in the environment demanded better.
- Some argue the symmetry exceeds any functional requirement — a possible signal of the maker's skill, which would make the handaxe an advertisement as much as a tool.

## Fire: the Contested Threshold

Fire changes everything — it cooks, it heats, it protects, it extends the day, and it creates a hearth, which is a place people gather and face each other. But **when** we controlled it is genuinely disputed:

| Evidence | Site | Reading |
|---|---|---|
| Burnt bone and ash, ~1 Ma | Wonderwerk, South Africa | Suggestive, deep in a cave |
| Hearths, ~780 ka | Gesher Benot Ya'aqov, Israel | Strong: repeated, structured burning |
| Ubiquitous hearths, <400 ka | Across Eurasia | Uncontested: routine use |

The methodological trap is that **fire happens naturally.** Lightning burns; a burnt layer proves burning, not control. What proves control is _structure_ — hearths in the same place, repeatedly, with fuel selection and stone rings. This is a superb illustration of the difference between an occurrence and a practice, and why archaeologists ask for patterns rather than instances.

## The Neanderthals Deserve Better

The cartoon Neanderthal — hunched, dumb, brutish — was partly built on one badly reconstructed skeleton of an arthritic old man, and it has taken a century to undo. The record says:

- **Bigger brains than ours on average** (though brain size is a crude proxy, and body mass matters).
- **Cold-adapted bodies**: compact, powerful, expensive to run — a Neanderthal needed thousands of calories a day.
- **Hafted composite tools** made with birch tar, which requires distilling tar from bark by heating it in a low-oxygen environment. This is not a simple recipe.
- **Care for the injured**: the Shanidar individual survived years with a withered arm, damaged eye socket, and healed fractures. Somebody fed him.
- **Burial** of at least some of their dead, and pigments; the disputed **Bruniquel** ring structures deep in a cave, built ~176,000 years ago, mean coordinated work by firelight far from daylight.

> **The killer fact:** they are inside us. Most people outside Africa carry 1–4% Neanderthal DNA, which means the "replacement" was never total — it was absorption. They did not simply lose to us. They interbred with us and then, as a distinct population, disappeared into a larger one. Extinction is rarely as clean as the word makes it sound.

## The Upper Paleolithic Revolution

Starting around **50,000–40,000 years ago** in Europe, the record detonates. Cave painting. Carved figurines. Bone flutes tuned to playable scales. Bead necklaces of shells carried hundreds of kilometres inland. Burials with grave goods. Spear-throwers, needles, harpoons — and toolkits that now **change every few millennia** instead of every million years.

This is usually called the **Upper Paleolithic Revolution**, or "the birth of behavioural modernity." And there is a serious problem with the label.

> **The African objection.** Our anatomy is ~300,000 years old, but the "revolution" is dated 40,000 years ago in _Europe_. Were we anatomically modern and behaviourally dull for a quarter of a million years? The African record says no: **Blombos Cave** (South Africa) has engraved ochre with deliberate crosshatching at ~75,000 years, and shell beads older still. Symbolic behaviour was already there. What looks like an explosion may be **a European sampling artefact** — more excavators, more limestone caves, better preservation — plus the arrival in Europe of people who already had all this.

That reframing is the modern consensus direction: less a switch flipping, more a long African build-up that becomes archaeologically loud when it reaches a continent full of caves and archaeologists. Notice that the "revolution" is partly a fact about **where researchers dug**, which is the kind of thing this course keeps insisting on.

## What the Art Is Not

The cave art is genuinely astonishing — **Chauvet** at ~36,000 years, **Lascaux**, **Altamira** — and it is easy to sentimentalize. So note what the record actually shows:

- **It is not decoration.** These paintings are usually deep inside caves, in places nobody lived, requiring lamps and a long crawl. Nobody painted a bison a kilometre underground to brighten a room.
- **It is not a picture of dinner.** The painted fauna do not match the bones in the rubbish heaps — Lascaux's people mostly ate reindeer and mostly painted horses, aurochs, and bison. The art is about what mattered, not what was eaten.
- **It is not "primitive."** Chauvet's lions use perspective, overlapping outlines to imply motion, and the cave wall's own contours to give an animal a shoulder. And it is **older** than Lascaux by 15,000 years — art history does not begin crude and improve.
- **It is signed, sort of.** Hand stencils appear everywhere, made by blowing pigment around a hand pressed to the wall; and analysis of hand proportions suggests **many were women's**, which quietly demolishes the default image of the male artist-hunter.

## The Machinery Behind the Symbols

Three innovations of this period matter beyond art:

| Innovation | Why it changes everything |
|---|---|
| **The eyed needle** | Fitted, sewn clothing — which is how humans occupy Siberia at all |
| **The atlatl (spear-thrower)** | A lever extending the arm; kills at distance, without closing with the animal |
| **Long-distance exchange** | Shells and amber hundreds of km from source — meaning networks of relationship, not just trade |

The needle deserves the same respect as the flute. Fitted clothing is a **portable microclimate**, and it is what let our species walk into the coldest inhabited places on Earth and stay there. Between 45,000 and 15,000 years ago, humans reach Australia across open water, occupy Siberia, and cross into the Americas. The species goes global — carrying needles, beads, and stories.

## The Mesolithic Transition

The Mesolithic is the most awkward label in prehistory. It means "middle stone age," and it exists mainly because European archaeologists needed a name for the inconvenient stretch between the glamorous Paleolithic hunters and the world-changing Neolithic farmers. It is a period defined by **not being the two periods either side of it.**

That is worth flagging as a methodological point in its own right: **periodization is a tool, not a discovery.** Nobody living in 8000 BCE knew they were between things. The gap in our scheme is a gap in our scheme.

But something real did happen, and the cause is the ice.

## When the World Warmed Up

Around **11,700 years ago** the last glaciation ended and the Holocene began — the warm, stable interval we still live in. The consequences arrived fast:

- **Sea levels rose** by more than a hundred metres over the following millennia, drowning coastlines and land bridges. **Doggerland** — a populated plain where the North Sea now is — went under, taking its people's homeland with it.
- **Forests advanced** over the open steppe-tundra. Herds of reindeer and horse gave way to solitary forest animals: red deer, boar, aurochs.
- **The megafauna went**, whether from warming, hunting, or both — and with them, the Paleolithic way of life that had tracked large herds across open country.

## The Broad-Spectrum Answer

You cannot hunt a forest the way you hunt a steppe. There are no herds to follow; the animals are dispersed, solitary, and hidden. So the toolkit and the strategy changed together:

| Innovation | What it solves |
|---|---|
| **Microliths** | Tiny standardized blades, mounted in rows on wood — a modular, repairable weapon system |
| **Bow and arrow** | Killing a solitary deer at distance in cover |
| **Dugouts, paddles, fish traps, nets** | The waterways become a larder |
| **The dog** | The first domesticated animal — and a hunting partner in dense cover |

Archaeologists call this the **broad-spectrum revolution**: instead of specializing on big game, people ate everything — fish, shellfish, nuts, birds, plants, deer. It looks like a downgrade if you rank foods by glamour. It is actually a **risk-management strategy**: a diet of twenty resources cannot fail the way a diet of one can.

> **And here is the part that upends the standard story.** In some rich places — estuaries, salmon rivers, the coasts — foraging was so productive that people **stopped moving before they started farming.** Sedentism came first. The **Natufians** of the Levant built stone-founded villages, ground wild cereals with heavy immovable mortars, and buried their dead beneath their houses — while remaining foragers. Nobody had to invent agriculture to settle down. Which means the famous causal arrow — farming produced villages — points, in these cases, exactly backwards: **villages came first, and farming was what people in villages eventually did.**

## The Neolithic Revolution

That is Jared Diamond's title, and it is deliberately provocative, and the skeletons partly agree with him.

Farming is the most consequential thing our species has ever done. It also, on the evidence of the people who first did it, made individual human lives **measurably worse**:

| Measure | What the bones show |
|---|---|
| **Height** | Early farmers are shorter than the foragers before them — sometimes by over 10 cm |
| **Teeth** | Cavities and abscesses explode; a starch diet rots the mouth |
| **Bones** | Anaemia (porotic hyperostosis), arthritis, spinal damage from grinding and hauling |
| **Disease** | Living beside animals and one's own waste seeds smallpox, measles, flu, TB |
| **Hours** | Foragers often worked fewer hours for a more varied diet |
| **Risk** | One crop failing is famine; twenty wild resources do not fail together |

So the question is not "why was farming better?" It is the far more interesting: **why did anyone keep doing it?**

## The Answer Is Arithmetic, Not Preference

Farming makes people **worse off and more numerous.** A field of grain feeds more mouths per hectare than the wild landscape it replaced, and cereals wean babies earlier, so births come closer together. Within a few generations the farming population is larger — and it is trapped: those extra people cannot be fed by going back.

This is a **ratchet**, not a choice. Nobody chose farming as a lifestyle upgrade; groups drifted into intensifying wild-cereal use, grew, and then could not reverse. The consequence is brutal and simple:

$$\text{more food per hectare} \rightarrow \text{more people} \rightarrow \text{no way back}$$

And the numbers were on farming's side in every conflict that followed. Foragers do not lose to farmers because farming is better. They lose because there are ten times as many farmers.

## What Domestication Actually Is

Domestication is not a decision. It is **evolution under human selection**, mostly unintentional at first:

- Wild wheat **shatters** — the ripe head explodes to scatter seed. Any mutant that _fails_ to shatter is a plant nobody's genes reach the ground. But a human harvester with a sickle collects precisely the heads that stayed intact — and replants them. Within centuries the field is full of a plant that **cannot reproduce without you.**
- Wild animals are selected for tameness, and tameness drags a whole syndrome with it: smaller brains, floppier ears, patchy coats, earlier breeding.

Neither party consented. **Wheat did to us what we did to wheat** — we cleared its competitors, watered it, spread it to every continent, and organized our entire society around its calendar. Which species domesticated which is a genuinely funny question with no comfortable answer.

> **It happened independently at least seven times:** the Fertile Crescent (wheat, barley, sheep, goats), China (rice, millet, pigs), Mesoamerica (maize, beans, squash), the Andes (potato, quinoa, llama), New Guinea (taro, banana), West Africa (sorghum, yams), and the eastern North American woodlands. Seven separate inventions on five continents, within a few thousand years of each other, after 300,000 years of nobody farming — which tells you the trigger was global. The end of the Ice Age gave every region a warm, stable Holocene at once, and in region after region, people walked into the same trap.

## Megaliths and Early Monumentality

For a century the story was settled: **farming → surplus → settlement → hierarchy → monuments.** You need agriculture to feed people who are not producing food, and only then can you build something enormous.

Then **Göbekli Tepe** came out of a Turkish hillside, and the order broke.

It is a complex of massive T-shaped limestone pillars — some over five metres and sixteen tonnes — carved with foxes, boars, scorpions, vultures, and arranged in rings. It was built around **9600 BCE**. That is roughly **six thousand years before Stonehenge**, and — this is the part that matters — the people who built it were **hunter-gatherers.** No domesticated crops. No village. No pottery. No metal. No writing. No kings, as far as anyone can tell.

> **The reversal:** the monument comes _before_ the farm, not after. Which raises a genuinely radical possibility, sometimes called the "temple-first" hypothesis: feeding the crowds who gathered to build and worship here may itself have driven the intensification of wild cereals — and some of the earliest domesticated wheat in the world comes from within 30 km of the site. Perhaps we did not settle down and then build temples. Perhaps **we built temples and had to settle down to feed the builders.** It remains contested — but the dates are not.

## Stonehenge Without the Nonsense

Stonehenge attracts more rubbish than any site on Earth, so start with what is actually known:

| Fact | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Built in stages** | ~3000–1500 BCE — a project spanning fifty generations |
| **Bluestones** | Hauled ~240 km from the Preseli hills of Wales |
| **Alignment** | Midsummer sunrise and midwinter sunset |
| **A landscape, not a monument** | Durrington Walls, avenues, hundreds of barrows — Stonehenge is one item in a vast ritual complex |

And what it was **not**: not Druids (they arrive some 2,000 years too late), not aliens, not a "calendar" in any useful sense. The feasting evidence at nearby Durrington Walls — pigs slaughtered in midwinter, brought from as far as Scotland — points at something more interesting: **mass gatherings**, seasonal, drawing people across an entire island.

## What a Monument Is For

The functional questions ("what did it do?") are mostly a dead end. The better question is what building it did to the builders:

- **Coordination**: hundreds of people, for generations, on a project no individual would live to see finished.
- **Cohesion**: the labour _is_ the point. Hauling a stone 240 km together is how a scattered population becomes a people.
- **Permanence**: a society with no writing still needs to say "we were here, this matters, remember." A stone is a sentence you cannot delete.

Which is why "before writing" does not mean "before history-making." These people were making claims about time — about ancestors, seasons, and the future — using the only medium that outlasts everything: rock, arranged on purpose, aimed at the sun.

## The Bronze Age and Metallurgy

Bronze is copper plus roughly **10% tin**, and that sentence contains the entire politics of the age.

Copper is common and soft. Add a tenth of tin and you get a metal hard enough for real weapons, castable into shapes no stone can take, and recyclable — melt a broken sword, pour a new one. The trouble is the tin.

**Tin is rare and almost nowhere near the copper.** The Mediterranean's copper came largely from Cyprus (the island's name and the word "copper" are entangled), while its tin came from Anatolia, Central Asia, and — astonishingly — as far as **Cornwall**. So making a Bronze Age sword in the Aegean required a supply chain thousands of kilometres long, running through polities that could cut it at any point.

> **This is the age's defining structure.** For the first time, a civilization's core military technology depended on **imports from strangers**. A palace in Mycenae could not arm itself from anything within a month's walk. That fact built the world's first long-distance trade system, first diplomatic correspondence between great kings, and first true strategic vulnerability. The **Uluburun shipwreck** off Turkey (c. 1300 BCE) is the age in one hull: ten tonnes of Cypriot copper, a tonne of tin, Canaanite jars, Egyptian gold, Baltic amber, African ebony, and Mycenaean pottery — one boat carrying a continent.

## Metal Makes Hierarchy

Metallurgy is not a craft you pick up. Smelting demands ores, fuel, furnaces that hold 1,100°C, and knowledge that takes years to acquire — so it produces **specialists**, and specialists must be fed by other people's food. The consequences ripple upward:

| Requirement | Social consequence |
|---|---|
| Ore only exists in specific places | Mining zones become strategic; some populations become suppliers |
| Smelting takes fuel and skill | Full-time craft specialists, supported by surplus |
| Tin comes from far away | Long-distance trade, diplomacy, and dependency |
| Bronze is valuable and portable | A store of wealth — accumulable, inheritable, and worth taking |

The last row is the one that changes societies. Grain rots; bronze does not. A metal that holds value indefinitely, can be hidden, moved, hoarded, and passed to a son is a machine for turning temporary advantage into **permanent inequality**. The rich Bronze Age burials — a warrior with a sword, a chariot, a hoard — are the archaeological signature of a world where wealth has learned how to persist.

## Iron Changes the Politics, Not Just the Metal

Iron ore is **everywhere**, and it needs no exotic partner element — but it needs far higher temperatures and much better technique, which is why it comes second despite being commoner.

Once mastered, it inverts the age's whole structure:

$$\text{bronze} = \text{rare} + \text{imported} + \text{elite} \quad \text{vs} \quad \text{iron} = \text{common} + \text{local} + \text{everyone}$$

Bronze arms an aristocracy; iron arms **a village**. A metal you can smelt from local ore, without a trade route through three kingdoms, cannot be monopolized by the people who control the routes. Historians argue about how directly this "democratization of metal" translated into political change — carefully, because technological determinism is a trap this course keeps warning about. But the direction is real: when the thing that makes a weapon stops being scarce, the people who controlled the scarcity lose their grip on it.

## The Bronze Age Collapse

Around **1200 BCE** the eastern Mediterranean held the most interconnected world that had ever existed. Egypt, the Hittites, Mycenaean Greece, Assyria, Babylon, Cyprus, and the Canaanite cities traded metal, grain, and luxury goods; their kings wrote to each other as "brother"; a merchant could sail from the Nile to the Aegean through a system of understood rules.

Within about **fifty years**, most of it was gone.

The Hittite empire — one of the great powers, a rival of Egypt — **vanishes completely**, capital burned, language lost until modern scholars dug it back up. Mycenaean palaces burn and are not rebuilt; Greece enters a "dark age" in which **Linear B is forgotten** and Greeks are illiterate for roughly four hundred years. Ugarit, a wealthy Syrian port, is destroyed so abruptly that a final tablet survives in the kiln where it was being fired — a letter reporting enemy ships and asking for help that never came. Egypt survives, barely, and never recovers its reach.

## The Story Egypt Told, and Why It Is Not Enough

Egypt's answer, carved at Medinet Habu, is the **"Sea Peoples"**: a coalition of foreigners who came from the sea, destroyed everything, and were repelled by Ramesses III. It is a vivid story, and it has one obvious problem — it is **an Egyptian victory monument**, exactly the kind of source this course has taught you to interrogate. Who made it? A king. Why? To be seen defeating chaos. What does it explain? Not much: the Sea Peoples appear to be as much _refugees_ as raiders — they arrive with women, children, and ox-carts, which is not how raiding parties travel. They look less like the cause of a collapse than **people already fleeing one**.

So the honest reading is that the Sea Peoples are a **symptom mistaken for a cause** — and identifying that mistake is the whole intellectual payoff of this lesson.

## Systems Collapse: When Connection Is the Vulnerability

The modern account, associated with Eric Cline's _1177 B.C._, invokes no single villain. It stacks stressors:

| Stressor | Evidence |
|---|---|
| **Drought** | Pollen cores and speleothems show a long, severe dry phase across the region |
| **Famine** | Hittite letters beg Egypt for grain shipments — "it is a matter of life or death" |
| **Earthquakes** | An earthquake storm damages multiple sites in the same window |
| **Movements of people** | Displaced populations on the move, arriving as the "Sea Peoples" |
| **Internal revolt** | Palace economies rest on coerced labour; famine breaks the bargain |
| **Trade disruption** | Cut the tin route and every palace's weapons supply fails |

Any one of these is survivable. Arriving together, they hit a system whose **interconnection was itself the fragility**. Bronze required tin from thousands of kilometres away; palaces required trade; trade required peace; peace required palaces. Each node depended on the others, so failures **cascaded** — and a network that efficient has no slack to absorb a shock.

> **The uncomfortable mirror.** This is why the Bronze Age collapse is the most-cited episode in ancient history right now. It is the clearest case of a globalized, efficient, tightly-coupled system that worked beautifully until several stresses coincided — and then unravelled faster than anyone in it could understand. Historians are wary of drawing the obvious modern lesson too crisply, because "civilizations that look like ours collapsed" is a rhetorical move, not an argument. But the structural point survives the caution: **connection is a strength and a transmission medium for failure, and it is never one without the other.**

## The Iron Age and the End of Prehistory

Iron is the fourth most abundant element in the Earth's crust. It is in almost every region on Earth, and it needs no rare partner. What it needs is **heat and skill** — and that is why it arrives second, despite being everywhere.

Bronze-age furnaces could not melt iron. Early ironworking produces a spongy **bloom** that must be hammered repeatedly to drive out slag, and the result is only better than bronze once you learn to add carbon and quench it. It is a harder craft rewarding a worse-looking metal — which is exactly why nobody bothered while the tin still flowed.

**Then the tin stopped flowing.** The Bronze Age collapse cut the long-distance routes, and suddenly a local ore that anyone could dig, worked by a smith in your own village, beat an imported alloy you could no longer get at any price. Necessity did what centuries of availability had not.

| | Bronze | Iron |
|---|---|---|
| **Ore** | Copper common, tin rare and distant | Everywhere |
| **Supply** | Long-distance trade through foreign powers | Local |
| **Skill** | Established, moderate | Higher — needs hotter furnaces and carbon control |
| **Politics** | Controllable — arms an elite | Uncontrollable — arms a village |

> **Handle the conclusion carefully.** It is tempting to say iron caused the fall of aristocracies and the rise of citizen armies, and there is something to it — a hoplite phalanx of farmers is affordable in a way a chariot elite is not. But this course keeps warning about technological determinism, and this is the temptation. Iron **made possible** a wider distribution of arms; it did not decide what any society would do with it. Assyria used iron to build the most frightening war machine yet seen, which is not a democratization of anything. The metal opened a door. People chose whether to walk through it, and in which direction.

## The Alphabet: Writing Escapes the Professionals

The other democratization of the age is quieter and larger. Cuneiform had hundreds of signs; Egyptian hieroglyphs demanded years of training. Both created a **scribal class** — writing was a profession, and professions have gates.

The **Phoenician alphabet** — around twenty-two consonantal signs — could be learned in weeks by a merchant with no priesthood behind him. The Greeks borrowed it and added vowels, and writing stopped being a career and became a **tool**.

That change is at least as consequential as iron. A technology of memory that any trader can use produces contracts, letters, poetry, laws posted in public, and eventually citizens who can read the law they are governed by. Every alphabet in use from Europe to South Asia today descends from that Phoenician handful of signs.

## Where Prehistory Ends — and Why the Line Is Not Real

We say prehistory ends with writing. Now look hard at what that sentence actually says:

$$\text{prehistory} \rightarrow \text{history} \iff \text{someone left us a document}$$

The line is **not in the past. It is in our archive.** Nothing changed for the people crossing it — a Gallic village in 100 BCE is "prehistoric" until a literate Roman writes about it, at which point the same village becomes "historic" without anyone in it doing anything differently. The threshold moves region by region, and it always moves at the speed of documentation, not of human development.

Worse, the line is drawn from the outside. Caesar makes Gaul "historical" by conquering and describing it, which means the moment a people enters history is often the moment someone else starts writing about them — usually the people invading them.

> **So treat the boundary as what it is:** a fact about the availability of sources, not a fact about human achievement. The people on the far side of it built Göbekli Tepe, crossed to Australia, domesticated seven regions' worth of crops, and painted Chauvet. Prehistory is not the prologue to the human story. It is roughly 95% of it, and the only thing it lacks is a narrator.

## Synthesis: The Human Journey

We walked upright for three million years carrying an ape's brain. Then, paid for by marrow and fire and the arms race of living in groups, the skull inflated — and the bill arrived at childbirth, so our children are born helpless and stay children a long time, which is why we learn so much. We were never alone: for almost all of that span several human species shared the planet, and being the only one left is a recent, temporary anomaly. When the ice retreated, seven regions independently walked into farming — a deal that made individuals shorter, sicker, and busier, and made populations too large to walk back out. Villages, then metal, then a supply chain for tin that made kings dependent on strangers; then everything burned at once, and a cheaper metal and a cheaper alphabet let a wider slice of humanity carry a weapon and read a law. Somewhere in there, someone wrote a receipt for barley, and we started calling it history.

## The Six Threads

Every unit of this course has been pulling on the same few strands:

| Thread | The pattern |
|---|---|
| **Sequences surprise us** | Feet before brains; villages before farming; temples before agriculture; monuments before writing |
| **Nothing optimizes** | Bipedalism wrecked our backs; farming made people ill; evolution and history both keep whatever survives, defects included |
| **Cost, not choice** | Farming spread by arithmetic, not preference; the ratchet needs nobody to want it |
| **Connection cuts both ways** | Tin built the Bronze Age world and transmitted its collapse |
| **Possible, not inevitable** | Iron opened a door; Assyria and the hoplite phalanx walked through it in opposite directions |
| **The archive is not the past** | The "Upper Paleolithic revolution" is partly where people dug; prehistory ends when a narrator shows up |

## What You Actually Learned

Not dates. The dates are looked up in seconds, and you will forget most of them by the weekend, which is fine.

What survives is a set of **habits**:

- When someone shows you a sequence, **check the order** — the intuitive one is wrong about as often as it is right, and the wrong ones are load-bearing.
- When someone explains an outcome with a villain, **look for the system** — the Sea Peoples were refugees, and the Republic was not murdered by Caesar.
- When someone says a technology caused a social change, **ask what it made possible** rather than what it forced.
- When someone hands you a source, **ask who made it and why** — Hammurabi's stele, Ramesses' wall, Ashoka's pillars, and Egypt's Sea Peoples all become more useful, not less, once you stop believing them and start reading them.
- When someone says a period "began," **ask whether the world changed or the evidence did.**

> **The last one is the whole course.** Prehistory is about 95% of the human story, and every threshold we have drawn through it — Paleolithic to Mesolithic, prehistory to history, primitive to civilized — is a line in our filing system that we keep mistaking for a line in the past. The people never crossed it. We drew it behind them.

## Why This Is Not a Story About the Past

You now have the deep frame the rest of history sits inside. Athens' lottery machine, Rome's private legions, the Han examination, Ashoka's carved regret, the khipu that ran an empire without writing — none of those look inevitable from here, because you have watched the same species produce seven agricultures, a million years of unchanging handaxes, and a temple built by foragers who had no business building one.

That is the point of the long view: it makes the present look **contingent**. Everything you live inside — the state, the calendar, the alphabet you are reading, the idea that a ruler needs to look just — was built by people improvising with what they had, and none of it had to turn out this way.

Which means it does not have to stay this way either. That is not a moral. It is just what six million years of evidence says.
