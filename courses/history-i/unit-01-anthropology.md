# Unit 1: Anthropology

## What is Anthropology?

**Anthropology** is the study of humans — all of them. Not one nation, not one century, not one social class: every society that exists or has ever existed, from Paleolithic bands to hedge funds. What makes it a single discipline rather than a pile of curiosities is a commitment called **holism**: the idea that no part of human life can be understood in isolation from the rest. Diet connects to ritual, ritual to politics, politics to kinship, kinship to diet again.

## The Four Fields

Anthropology traditionally organizes itself into four cooperating fields:

| Field | What it studies | Typical evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Cultural anthropology** | Living societies: beliefs, kinship, economy, ritual | Fieldwork, interviews |
| **Biological anthropology** | Human evolution and variation | Fossils, bones, genes |
| **Archaeology** | Past societies through what they left behind | Artifacts, sites, strata |
| **Linguistic anthropology** | Language in social life | Speech, texts, grammars |

The fields answer different questions with different tools, but they share the same subject — and the boundaries leak productively. A study of ancient diet may need bones (biological), cooking pots (archaeology), and food taboos recorded in living descendants (cultural).

## The Comparative Method

Anthropology earns its generalizations by **comparison**. A claim like "all societies regulate marriage" is only testable if you check it against many societies — which is why anthropologists treasure the full catalog of human variation. One documented exception beats a thousand armchair certainties.

> **The discipline's wager:** nothing human is self-evident. What feels natural — eating three meals, burying the dead, owning things — is one option among many that societies have actually tried.

## Neighbors, Not Rivals

History asks what happened, mostly from written records. Sociology asks how modern societies work, mostly from surveys and statistics. Anthropology overlaps both but keeps two distinguishing habits: it insists on the **full range** of human societies (including those that left no writing), and it prefers to learn by **being there** — the fieldwork tradition you will meet in Lesson 3.

## Culture and Cultural Relativism

In 1871 Edward B. Tylor gave anthropology its charter definition: **culture** is "that complex whole which includes knowledge, belief, art, morals, law, custom, and any other capabilities and habits acquired by man as a member of society." Two words carry the weight: _acquired_ — culture is learned, not inherited in the genes — and _whole_ — it hangs together as a system. By 1952, Kroeber and Kluckhohn could already catalog **164 competing definitions**; the concept had conquered the discipline.

## What Culture Is Made Of

| Component | What it is | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Values** | Shared ideas of what is good | Hospitality is sacred |
| **Norms** | Rules for behavior | Guests must be fed first |
| **Symbols** | Things that carry meaning | Bread and salt at the door |
| **Artifacts** | Material culture | The guest room itself |

Norms come in grades of seriousness. **Folkways** are everyday conventions (which hand holds the fork); breaking them earns a raised eyebrow. **Mores** carry moral weight (honesty with guests); breaking them brings real sanction. **Taboos** are the unthinkable (cannibalism); and **laws** are norms the state enforces.

## Ethnocentrism and Its Antidote

**Ethnocentrism** is judging other cultures by the standards of your own — assuming your food is cuisine and theirs is "weird stuff." Every culture teaches it; fieldwork is designed to break it. The antidote is **cultural relativism**: the methodological rule that a practice must first be understood _in its own context_ — what work does it do, what meanings does it carry for the people who live it?

> **Method, not morality:** relativism is a tool for understanding, not a vow of silence. Understanding why a practice exists is required _before_ judging it; it does not make judgment impossible. Anthropologists document, contextualize — and still condemned practices like forced marriage in their policy work.

## Culture Shock

Immersion in another culture typically runs through recognizable stages — fascination, frustration when the rules stop making sense, gradual adjustment, and finally functional adaptation. The frustration stage is data, not failure: it marks exactly where the visitor's unspoken norms collide with the hosts'.

## Fieldwork and the Ethnographic Method

Until the 1910s, most anthropology was written from the **veranda**: scholars read questionnaires filled in by missionaries and colonial officers, then theorized from the armchair. Bronisław Malinowski broke the pattern. Stranded in the Pacific during the First World War, he spent nearly two years (1915–1918, in two long stays) in the **Trobriand Islands** — pitching his tent in the village, learning the language, and recording daily life as it happened. His slogan became the discipline's mission statement: _to grasp the native's point of view, his relation to life, to realise his vision of his world_.

## Participant Observation

The core method is a deliberate contradiction: **participate** enough to understand from inside, **observe** enough to keep analytical distance. In practice it means:

| Practice | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Long-term residence (a year or more) | See full seasonal and ritual cycles |
| Learning the local language | Stop depending on interpreters’ filters |
| Building **rapport** | Move from staged answers to everyday behavior |
| Field notes, daily and verbatim | Capture what memory would edit away |
| Key informants | Gain expert guides to the culture’s inside view |

Rapport is not friendship on a schedule — it grows slowly, unevenly, and can collapse in an afternoon. The payoff curve is S-shaped: months of small talk, then a threshold where doors open, then diminishing returns as the field becomes familiar.

## Emic and Etic

Ethnographers distinguish two kinds of description. The **emic** account renders the insiders' own categories: _the yam house is where a chief's power is visible_. The **etic** account uses the analyst's cross-cultural categories: _the yam house functions as a public store of surplus that legitimates rank_. Good ethnography needs both — emic without etic is transcription; etic without emic is projection.

> **Ethics before access:** modern fieldwork requires **informed consent**, protection of informants (anonymity where it matters), and honesty about the research role. Data obtained by deception is not a trophy; it is a violation — and it poisons the field for everyone who comes after.

## Kinship and Family Systems

Before states, markets, or written law, **kinship** organized everything: who you could marry, where you lived, what you inherited, who avenged you. Anthropologists map it with a compact notation — triangles for men, circles for women, an equals sign for marriage, vertical lines for descent, horizontal brackets for siblings. Learn six symbols and you can diagram a dynasty.

## Descent: Who Counts as Family

Societies choose which parental line transmits membership:

| System | Membership passes through | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Patrilineal** | Fathers only | Traditional China |
| **Matrilineal** | Mothers only | Trobriand Islands; Akan |
| **Bilateral** | Both sides equally | Most of modern Europe |

A crucial surprise: in a matrilineal system, authority usually belongs not to the mother but to the **mother's brother** — descent through women is not rule _by_ women. Your father, in the Trobriands, is legally a kind of affectionate outsider to your lineage.

## Marriage Rules

**Exogamy** requires marrying outside a defined group (the clan, the village); **endogamy** requires marrying inside one (the caste, the faith). Marriage typically moves wealth: **bridewealth** flows from the groom's kin to the bride's; **dowry** accompanies the bride into the marriage. Where a couple lives has its own vocabulary — **patrilocal** (with his kin), **matrilocal** (with hers), **neolocal** (a new household).

> **Why cousins are not all alike:** many systems distinguish **parallel cousins** (children of same-sex siblings — father's brother's children) from **cross cousins** (children of opposite-sex siblings — father's sister's children). In some societies the first are siblings you cannot marry; the second are the _preferred_ spouses. The biology is identical; the kinship logic is not.

## Why It Matters to Historians

Kinship is the skeleton under recorded history: Habsburg marriage strategy, Chinese ancestral halls, Germanic feud law, and the inheritance disputes filling medieval archives are all kinship systems leaving paperwork.

## Economic Anthropology and Reciprocity

Textbook economics begins with barter: strangers swapping fish for arrows. Anthropology found something else on the ground. In most societies through most of history, goods moved through **social relationships** — as gifts, obligations, tribute, and feasts — and the "economy" was not a separate sphere at all. Karl Polanyi called this **embeddedness**, and distinguished three great modes of integration:

| Mode | Logic | Classic setting |
|---|---|---|
| **Reciprocity** | Gift and counter-gift between partners | Kin groups, allies |
| **Redistribution** | Goods flow to a center, then back out | Chiefdoms, temples, states |
| **Market exchange** | Prices negotiated between strangers | Bazaars, stock exchanges |

## The Gift That Must Be Returned

Marcel Mauss's _The Gift_ (1925) distilled the ethnography into one finding: the gift carries **three obligations — to give, to receive, and to repay**. Refusing any of them is a declaration of hostility. A gift left unreciprocated is not generosity enjoyed; it is rank asserted and accepted. Sahlins later graded reciprocity by social distance: **generalized** (no accounting — parents and children), **balanced** (equivalent return expected — trade partners), and **negative** (each side trying to win — strangers, hagglers).

## The Kula Ring

Malinowski's Trobriand masterpiece documented the **kula**: red shell necklaces (_soulava_) travel **clockwise** around a ring of islands, white shell armbands (_mwali_) travel **counterclockwise**, passing between lifelong partners. The valuables are never kept — holding one too long is disgrace — and never sold. What circulates is not wealth but **fame, alliance, and safe passage**; ordinary trade (_gimwali_) rides along in the kula canoes, made possible by the partnerships the shells create.

> **The potlatch escalation:** on the Northwest Coast, chiefs competed by _giving away_ (and sometimes destroying) mountains of blankets and coppers. The biggest giver won. Where market logic accumulates to gain status, gift logic can spend to gain it — same ambition, inverted arithmetic.

The animation sets the kula in motion: necklaces stream **clockwise**, armbands **counterclockwise**, island to island, partner to partner. Watch a single necklace: it never stops, never reverses, and never meets anything but armbands coming the other way — every exchange is necklace-for-armband, by rule.

## Political Organization

Every society solves the same problems — decisions, disputes, defense — but not with the same machinery. Elman Service's classic typology arranges the solutions by scale:

| Form | Typical size | Leadership | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Band** | Dozens | Informal headman; persuasion only | San, Inuit |
| **Tribe** | Hundreds–thousands | Big man; earned, unstable prestige | New Guinea highlands |
| **Chiefdom** | Thousands–tens of thousands | Hereditary chief; ranked lineages | Pre-contact Hawai'i |
| **State** | Tens of thousands + | Bureaucracy; monopoly of force | Inca, Rome, everyone today |

The types are analytic stops on a continuum, not an escalator every society must ride. Plenty of societies stayed bands for millennia — by choice as much as circumstance.

## Two Kinds of Getting Your Way

Anthropology splits **power** (the ability to compel) from **authority** (the recognized _right_ to decide). A band headman has authority and almost no power: he persuades, arbitrates, and sets an example, but cannot order anyone. A **big man** builds influence through generosity — financing feasts, brokering marriages — and his position dies with him. A **chief** inherits office; rank attaches to lineages, and tribute flows upward. A **state**, in Weber's definition, claims the **monopoly on the legitimate use of force** within its territory: now compulsion has an address.

## Law Before Courts

Stateless societies still have law. Disputes are settled by **mediation** (the Nuer leopard-skin chief, sacred and unarmed), by structured compensation (blood-wealth payments graded by injury), by **song duels** (Inuit adversaries mock each other before the camp until one is laughed down), or by the threat of feud — which works precisely because everyone's kin would be dragged in.

> **The historian's payoff:** when written sources say "king," always ask which machinery stood behind the word — a big man with a good year, a ranked chiefdom, or a taxing state. Misreading the type misreads every event that follows.

## Religion, Myth, and Ritual

Anthropology's question about religion is never "is it true?" but "**what does it do?**" — for the person, for the group, for the cosmos it draws. E. B. Tylor started with **animism**, the attribution of soul and agency to beings and things. Durkheim went further: in ritual, the group experiences its own force as something sacred — **collective effervescence** — so that worshipping the totem is, without knowing it, society worshipping itself.

## Rites of Passage

Arnold van Gennep (1909) noticed that ceremonies moving people between statuses — birth, initiation, marriage, funeral — share a three-beat structure:

| Phase | What happens | Example (initiation) |
|---|---|---|
| **Separation** | Detached from the old status | Boys taken from the village |
| **Liminality** | Betwixt and between; normal rules suspended | Seclusion, ordeals, secret knowledge |
| **Incorporation** | Re-entry with the new status | Return as men, feasted and renamed |

Victor Turner made the middle phase famous: in **liminality**, ranks dissolve and initiates share an intense equality he called **communitas** — the social bond experienced raw, before structure reasserts itself.

## Magic, Totem, Taboo

Frazer sorted magic by its logic: **imitative** (like produces like — stick pins in the effigy) and **contagious** (things once in contact keep acting on each other — guard your cut hair). A **totem** ties a descent group to an emblem species; a **taboo** fences off the sacred or polluting from ordinary touch. None of this is failed science — it is a grammar of meaning, and it runs on association exactly the way advertising still does.

> **Myth is not "false story":** in anthropological usage a myth is a _charter narrative_ — a story that grounds why the world, the ritual, or the clan's privileges are as they are. Malinowski: myth is not idle explanation but a "hard-worked active force," cited in disputes the way we cite constitutions.

## Language and Culture

Every human language is a complete system: a small set of meaningless sounds (**phonemes**) combines into meaningful units (**morphemes**), which grammar (**syntax**) assembles into infinite sentences. No language is primitive — the grammar of a hunting band's speech can be more inflected than Latin. Linguistic anthropology asks how this instrument and culture shape each other.

## Does Language Shape Thought?

The **Sapir-Whorf hypothesis** comes in two strengths:

| Version | Claim | Verdict of the evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Strong (determinism)** | Language _determines_ what can be thought | Rejected — translation and learning happen |
| **Weak (relativity)** | Language _influences_ habitual attention and memory | Supported in specific domains |

The supported cases are precise: languages with different color-term inventories show small differences in color memory at category boundaries; languages using absolute directions (north/south instead of left/right) produce speakers with compass-like orientation. Language is not a prison — it is a well-worn groove.

> **The snow-words legend:** the claim that "Eskimo has hundreds of words for snow" grew by journalistic inflation from Boas's modest observation of four lexical roots. Linguists call the genre "the great Eskimo vocabulary hoax." Treat any spectacular language-shapes-thought anecdote as unverified until a controlled study appears.

## Language Has a History

Languages change lawfully. Latin _pater_ and English _father_ differ by regular sound shifts (Grimm's law: p becomes f), which is how comparativists reconstructed **Proto-Indo-European** — a language with no texts, recovered entirely by triangulation. An innovation spreads through a speech community along an **S-curve**: slowly among pioneers, then explosively, then slowly again among holdouts. For the historian, loanwords are receipts: English _beef_ (from French) beside _cow_ (Germanic) records exactly who raised the animal and who ate it after 1066.

## Ethnicity and Identity

Common sense says an ethnic group is a bundle of shared stuff — language, dress, food, blood. Fredrik Barth (1969) inverted this: what persists is the **boundary**, the us/them line people maintain, while the cultural "stuff" inside it changes freely. Groups trade, intermarry, and swap customs for generations yet remain distinct — because members keep signalling and policing who counts. Ethnicity is a form of **social organization**, not a container of traits.

## Three Theories of Where Identity Comes From

| Position | Claim | Weak spot |
|---|---|---|
| **Primordialism** | Identities are ancient, given, quasi-natural | Cannot explain how identities appear and dissolve |
| **Instrumentalism** | Elites mobilize identity when it pays | Cannot explain why appeals _work_ so deeply |
| **Constructivism** | Identities are made historically, then feel natural | The mainstream synthesis |

## Nations Are Young

Benedict Anderson called the nation an **imagined community**: members will never meet, yet conceive of themselves moving through history together. His engine is **print capitalism** — newspapers and novels in standardized vernaculars let millions imagine simultaneity ("while I read this, my countrymen read it too"). Ernest Gellner tied nationalism to industrial society's need for interchangeable, schooled citizens; Eric Hobsbawm showed how much national tradition is **invented tradition** — the Scottish clan tartan, for instance, is substantially a 19th-century commercial creation, later backdated into timeless antiquity.

> **The historian's discipline:** never project modern nations backward. "France" in 1789 held millions who spoke no French; calling Vercingetorix a French patriot or ancient Macedon a modern nation-state is anachronism doing political work. Ask instead _when_ a given identity was assembled, by whom, and out of what.

## Applied Anthropology Today

**Applied anthropology** puts ethnographic method to work on practical problems. Its subfields track where culture and institutions collide:

| Subfield | Works on | Example finding |
|---|---|---|
| **Development anthropology** | Aid and infrastructure projects | Wells fail where water rights weren't mapped |
| **Medical anthropology** | Health, illness, healing systems | Epidemics spread along burial practices |
| **Business anthropology** | Workplaces, users, markets | Ethnography of use beats what users _say_ they do |

The founding lesson, paid for in decades of failed projects: **consult the users first**. The graveyard of development is full of technically perfect pumps nobody maintains, stoves nobody cooks on, and latrines used as grain stores — all designed for imaginary villagers instead of real ones. During the 2014 Ebola epidemic, transmission dropped when responders worked _with_ communities to redesign burial rites, rather than banning them — a masterclass in why cultural knowledge is operational knowledge.

## The Ethics of Showing Up

Intervention raises questions pure research can defer:

- **Informed consent** — participants must understand and accept the research, every time; no beneficial outcome excuses skipping it.
- **Do no harm** — the anthropologist's first obligation is to the people studied, above funders, governments, and publication.
- **Whose goals?** — "improvement" defined in a distant capital may be dispossession on the ground.

> **The cautionary tale:** the US Army's Human Terrain System (2007) embedded social scientists with combat units in Iraq and Afghanistan. The American Anthropological Association condemned it — when informants can be targeted, "do no harm" and counterinsurgency cannot be reconciled. The episode fixed the discipline's red line: ethnography must never become intelligence.
