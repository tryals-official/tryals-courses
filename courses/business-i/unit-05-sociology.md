# Unit 5: Sociology

## The Sociological Perspective

Sociology's founding move is a change of focal length: behind each individual story, find the **social pattern** producing thousands of similar stories.

C. Wright Mills called the skill the **sociological imagination**: connecting **personal troubles** to **public issues**. One person unemployed is a biography — perhaps bad luck, perhaps bad choices. Fifteen percent unemployed is _structure_: no possible arrangement of individual virtue puts everyone in jobs that don't exist.

> **Key idea:** One case is a biography; thousands of cases reveal a structure.

Émile Durkheim built the discipline on this: **social facts** — ways of acting and thinking that exist outside any individual and exert pressure on all of them. Marriage rates, consumption habits, career choices: stable, patterned, predictable at the group level even though every participant feels free.

**Three lenses** dominate the discipline:

- **Structural functionalism**: society as a system of parts (family, education, economy) each performing functions that keep the whole running.
- **Conflict theory**: society as an arena where groups compete for scarce resources; institutions tend to serve whoever holds power.
- **Symbolic interactionism**: the micro view — society built from everyday encounters, meanings, and symbols continuously negotiated.

The lenses are complements, not rivals: a supermarket is a food-distribution function, a battleground of suppliers and gig workers, _and_ a stage of tiny rituals at the checkout.

**Why managers need the imagination**: turnover, absenteeism, viral adoption, discrimination suits — all arrive as individual anecdotes and are all, at scale, structural. The manager who explains a 40% churn rate one resignation letter at a time will fix nothing; the one who sees the pattern can change the system producing it.

**Troubles vs issues**

| | Personal trouble | Public issue |
|---|---|---|
| Scale | One biography | A rate across thousands |
| Feels like | Bad luck, bad choices | — |
| Actually is | — | Structure: jobs that don't exist |
| Fix lives in | The individual's milieu | Institutions and policy |

> **Tip:** Durkheim's test for a social fact: it is _external_ to you (you didn't invent the marriage rate) and _coercive_ on you (deviate and feel the pressure). If both hold, you are looking at structure, not psychology.

## Culture and Organizational Values

**Culture** is everything a group learns, shares, and transmits: its **symbols**, **language**, **values**, **norms**, and **material artifacts**. If structure is a company's hardware — org charts, buildings, systems — culture is the software actually running on it.

**The working parts:**

- **Symbols**: anything carrying shared meaning — a logo, a corner office, who gets invited to which meeting.
- **Values**: shared judgments of what is _desirable_ — innovation over safety, seniority over merit, customer over process.
- **Norms**: the behavioral rules that operationalize values. **Folkways** (soft customs — dress, punctuality etiquette) versus **mores** (morally charged rules whose violation provokes outrage — honesty, non-discrimination).
- **Sanctions**: the enforcement system — from a raised eyebrow to dismissal (negative), from praise to promotion (positive).

> **The chain:** values → norms → sanctions — what we prize, how we act, how we enforce.

**The iceberg property**: artifacts and rituals are visible above the waterline; the values and tacit assumptions driving them sit massively below. A visitor sees the open-plan office (artifact); only insiders know whether speaking up in meetings is _actually_ safe (assumption).

**Two analytical hygiene rules**: avoid **ethnocentrism** (judging another culture purely by your own standards — the exporter's classic blunder) while keeping the ability to evaluate practices at all; and expect **subcultures** — engineering, sales, and finance inside one firm can differ more than two countries do.

**The management punchline**: 'culture eats strategy for breakfast' is a claim about _norms beating memos_. A written value that contradicts the felt norm loses every time: if the poster says 'work-life balance' and promotions go to midnight emailers, everyone learns the real value by Tuesday.

**Folkways vs mores**

| | Folkways | Mores |
|---|---|---|
| What they rule | Custom and convenience | Morally charged conduct |
| Violation provokes | Raised eyebrows | Outrage, expulsion |
| Business example | Dress code, punctuality | Honesty, non-discrimination |

> **Common pitfall:** Reading the culture off the values poster in reception. Espoused values are what the group _says_ it prizes; enacted values are what gets rewarded, promoted, and tolerated. When the two diverge, behavior follows the enacted ones — always.

## Socialization and Professional Identity

Nobody is born an accountant, a negotiator, or even a 'team player.' **Socialization** is the lifelong process by which people internalize their culture's values, norms, and roles — and build a self out of them.

**The agents**, in rough order of appearance:

- **Family**: first language, first norms, first sense of what people like us do.
- **School**: beyond curriculum, the **hidden curriculum** — punctuality, queuing, being graded, obeying impersonal rules. School is a rehearsal for bureaucracy.
- **Peers**: the first sphere where status must be _earned_ among equals.
- **Media**: models of success, consumption, and aspiration, on industrial scale.
- **Workplace**: the adult chapter — and business's home turf.

**The self is social.** Cooley's **looking-glass self**: we imagine how we appear to others, imagine their judgment, and feel pride or shame accordingly — the self is assembled from reflected appraisals. Mead added the **generalized other**: maturity is internalizing not one judge but the whole community's expectations.

**Professional socialization** is the same machinery aimed at an occupation: the medical student learns detachment, the consultant learns the deck-speak, the trainee auditor learns professional skepticism. It proceeds through **anticipatory socialization** (adopting the role's norms before holding it — the student already dressing like a banker), formal training, and informal absorption from colleagues.

**Resocialization** is the sharp version: **total institutions** (Goffman) — barracks, monasteries, and to a degree intense corporate onboarding programs — strip the old identity (uniforms, schedules, new language) and rebuild a new one.

> **Key idea:** The self = internalized others + rehearsed roles.

The managerial insight: onboarding is not paperwork — it is identity work. Firms that design it deliberately get members; firms that don't get employees who happen to badge in.

**The agents of socialization**

| Agent | What it teaches |
|---|---|
| Family | First language, first norms, first identity |
| School | The hidden curriculum: rules, grading, queuing |
| Peers | Status earned among equals |
| Media | Models of success and aspiration at scale |
| Workplace | Occupational selves — the adult chapter |

> **Tip:** Onboarding a new hire is textbook socialization: symbols (badge, laptop), rituals (first stand-up), language (the acronyms), sanctions (the look when you're late). Design it deliberately — it happens anyway.

## Social Interaction and Networks

Every interaction runs on invisible grammar: **statuses** (positions), **roles** (their scripts), and **networks** (the wiring between them).

**Status** is a social position, not prestige: 'intern' and 'CEO' are both statuses. **Ascribed** statuses are assigned at birth (nationality, family); **achieved** statuses are earned (auditor, founder). A **master status** dominates how others read you — 'the boss' colors every joke you make.

Each status carries a **role**: the expected behavior. Trouble comes in two flavors:

- **Role conflict** — clashing expectations _between_ statuses (parent's school play vs. manager's board meeting).
- **Role strain** — clashing expectations _inside one_ status (the manager must be both supportive coach and hard evaluator of the same person).

**Goffman's dramaturgy** treats interaction as theater: a **front stage** where we perform the role in costume (the client meeting), a **back stage** where the performance is prepared and dropped (the team room afterwards), and continuous **impression management** in between. A business lunch is not a meal; it is a stage with cutlery.

**Networks** are the wiring. Your contacts, and their contacts, form a structure with measurable properties: density (how many of your contacts know each other), reach, and bridges. Milgram's small-world experiments suggested any two people connect through roughly **six links** — networks are shallow but astonishingly wide.

> **The chain:** status → role → performance, wired together into networks.

For a manager, the payoff is diagnostic: when a good employee behaves badly, check the _role architecture_ — conflict, strain, wrong stage — before blaming character.

**Two ways a role can hurt**

| | Role conflict | Role strain |
|---|---|---|
| Clash between | Two different statuses | Expectations inside one status |
| Example | Parent's play vs board meeting | Coach _and_ hard evaluator of the same team |
| Fix | Prioritize or segregate statuses | Renegotiate the role's design |

> **Tip:** Ascribed vs achieved is a spectrum, not a wall: 'heir to the family firm' mixes both. What matters analytically is which component drives how others treat the person.

Granovetter's 1973 result is the most commercially useful sentence sociology ever produced: **new information travels through weak ties, not strong ones.**

**Strong ties** — family, close friends, the teammates you lunch with daily — form dense clusters where everyone knows everyone. Dense means **redundant**: your best friend reads the same job boards, hears the same gossip, knows the same recruiters you do. Information inside a cluster is already shared; the tie is warm but tells you little you don't know.

**Weak ties** — the former colleague, the acquaintance from a course — are your **bridges** to _other_ clusters. Each one taps a pool of information, contacts, and opportunities that your own circle simply cannot see. Granovetter found that most people who got jobs through contacts got them through people they saw _rarely_ — the acquaintance, not the best friend.

> **Key idea:** New information travels through ties that _bridge_, not ties that _bond_.

Burt sharpened the geometry: the value sits in **structural holes** — the empty spaces between clusters. Whoever spans a hole becomes the **broker**: first to hear, able to combine ideas from worlds that don't talk, and hard to replace precisely because the two sides connect only through them. Brokerage positions predict better ideas, faster promotion, and higher pay with uncomfortable reliability.

The arithmetic is brutal and simple. Four more strong ties inside your own cluster add almost nothing new — their contacts are your contacts. Four weak ties into four _different_ clusters, each opening a dozen fresh contacts, put roughly **48 new nodes** within two steps.

**Managerial applications**: hire through weak-tie referrals to escape the firm's echo chamber; rotate people across departments to plant bridges; and audit your own calendar — if every meeting is with the same five people, you have bonds, warmth, trust... and no periscope.

**Bonds vs bridges**

| | Strong ties | Weak ties |
|---|---|---|
| Who | Family, daily teammates | Acquaintances, former colleagues |
| Network shape | Dense cluster — everyone knows everyone | Bridge to other clusters |
| Information | Redundant — already shared | Novel — from pools you can't see |
| Best for | Trust, support, execution | Jobs, leads, new ideas |

> **Common pitfall:** Reading 'weak' as 'unimportant.' The tie is weak in emotional intensity, not in value — for genuinely new information it is strictly stronger than your strongest bond.

## Groups and Organizations

A **group** is people who interact and share identity — not a crowd in an elevator. Sociology sorts them by the work they do on us:

- **Primary groups** (Cooley): small, intimate, ends in themselves — family, close friends, the founding team at 2 a.m. They shape who we _are_.
- **Secondary groups**: larger, impersonal, goal-driven — the department, the committee. They shape what we _do_.
- **In-groups / out-groups**: 'us' and 'them' — the engine of team loyalty and of inter-departmental warfare alike.
- **Reference groups**: the group we _measure ourselves against_, member or not — analysts benchmarking themselves against bankers they've never met.

**Size is destiny.** A **dyad** (2) is intense and fragile — one exit kills it. A **triad** (3) invents politics: coalitions, mediators, majorities. And connections explode combinatorially: a team of $n$ people holds $n(n-1)/2$ possible pairwise ties — 3 people manage 3 relationships, 10 people manage 45. That arithmetic, not sentiment, is why small teams feel tight and large ones need process.

$$\text{ties} = \frac{n(n-1)}{2}$$

**Groupthink** (Janis): cohesive groups start prizing harmony over accuracy — dissent self-censors, doubts stay unspoken, and confident unanimity walks the group off a cliff. Antidotes are structural: assign a devil's advocate, collect opinions before discussion, let the boss speak last.

**Formal organizations** scale groups past friendship. Weber's **bureaucracy** is the pure type: hierarchy of offices, written rules, specialized division of labor, impersonality, and merit-based careers. It is history's most efficient coordination machine — and it breeds its own pathologies: red tape, goal displacement (rules become the goal), and Michels' **iron law of oligarchy**: even democratic organizations drift toward rule by the few who run the machinery.

The manager's double vision: every org chart (secondary, formal) runs on an invisible chassis of primary groups, in-group lines, and reference points — and both layers must be managed.

**The group taxonomy**

| Type | Nature | Shapes |
|---|---|---|
| Primary | Small, intimate, an end in itself | Who we _are_ |
| Secondary | Larger, impersonal, goal-driven | What we _do_ |
| In/out-groups | 'Us' vs 'them' | Loyalty and rivalry |
| Reference | The benchmark group | Aspirations |

> **Tip:** Size predicts dynamics before personality does: a dyad dies with one exit, a triad invents politics (two can outvote one), and past a dozen members every group grows formal roles whether it wants them or not.

## Social Stratification

Every society sorts its members into layers with unequal access to money, power, and prestige. **Stratification** is that layering as a _system_ — a property of the society, not of the individuals stacked in it.

**Systems differ in how sealed the layers are.** Caste systems fix position at birth (closed); class systems allow movement (open, in principle). Modern economies are class systems with sticky floors and ceilings.

**What exactly gets ranked?** Marx answered with one axis: relation to the means of production — owners versus workers. Weber split rank into **three currencies** that need not travel together:

- **Class** — market position: what your assets and skills fetch.
- **Status** — social honor: prestige, lifestyle, respectability.
- **Party** — power: capacity to impose your will through organization.

A crypto millionaire (high class, contested status), a broke aristocrat (the reverse), and a union secretary (party without class) are all Weberian case studies.

**Measuring the layers**: sociologists use **socioeconomic status (SES)** — a composite of income, education, and occupational prestige. The **Lorenz curve** draws the whole distribution at once: population share on one axis, cumulated income share on the other; the deeper it sags below the 45° line of perfect equality, the more unequal the society. The **Gini coefficient** compresses that sag into a single number from 0 (everyone equal) to 1 (one person has everything).

$$\text{Gini} = \frac{\text{area between Lorenz curve and equality line}}{\text{total area under equality line}}$$

**Mobility** is movement between layers: **intergenerational** (versus your parents), **intragenerational** (within your career), **structural** (the whole economy's shape shifted — farmhands' children became office workers because offices multiplied). The business relevance is immediate: stratification shapes who buys what, who applies to work for you, and which markets even exist.

**Weber's three currencies**

| Currency | What it is | Business face |
|---|---|---|
| Class | Market position — what assets and skills fetch | Salary, equity |
| Status | Social honor and lifestyle | Title, corner office, alma mater |
| Party | Organized power | Board seat, union, network |

> **Common pitfall:** Collapsing all three into income. A judge outranks a crypto trader in status while earning less; a union leader holds party without class. The currencies travel separately — that is Weber's whole point against Marx's single axis.

How open is a society _really_? Not a matter of opinion — there is an instrument: the **mobility table**. Rows are parents' class; columns are children's class; each cell counts who went from where to where. The whole debate about meritocracy lives inside that grid.

**Reading the diagonal.** Cells on the diagonal are _immobility_ — children landing in their parents' class. Off-diagonal cells are movement: above it, upward; below it, downward. A perfectly fluid society would fill the table as if parent and child class were independent — each column looking the same regardless of row.

**The trap of absolute numbers.** In the twentieth century, most children _did_ end up above their parents. Proof of open societies? Mostly not: the occupational structure itself moved — farms shrank, offices exploded. That is **structural (absolute) mobility**: the tide lifted everyone. The honest question subtracts the tide: given the new structure, do class origins still predict destinations? That residue is **relative mobility**, or social _fluidity_.

**Odds ratios are the sociologist's microscope.** Take two children, one born top, one born bottom. Compare their odds of _reaching_ the top versus _staying_ at the bottom:

$$OR = \frac{\text{odds}(\text{top} \to \text{top})}{\text{odds}(\text{bottom} \to \text{top})}$$

If the odds ratio is 1, origins don't matter — pure fluidity. Real economies run odds ratios well above 1: being born at the top multiplies the chances of staying there several-fold, even in societies whose _absolute_ mobility looks heroic. Odds ratios also have a useful property: they are immune to the tide, so they compare societies (or decades) whose occupational structures differ wildly.

**The Great Gatsby curve** closes the loop with the last lesson's tools: countries with higher Gini coefficients show _lower_ intergenerational mobility — inequality and stickiness travel together. Where the rungs are far apart, the ladder is harder to climb.

**For business readers**: mobility tables are market intelligence. A rigid society reproduces its consumer segments and its talent pools generation after generation; a fluid one keeps reshuffling them. Hiring 'from the best schools' in a low-fluidity country is not selecting talent — it is photocopying the parental generation's class structure into your payroll.

> **Tip:** Always split mobility into its two parts: **absolute** (how many moved — often driven by the occupational structure growing white-collar jobs) and **relative** (whose _chances_ improved — the odds ratios). Societies celebrate the first; meritocracy claims require the second.

> **Common pitfall:** Reading a full diagonal as proof of unfairness. Some immobility reflects inherited advantages, some reflects transmitted skills and preferences. The table shows the pattern; explaining it needs more than the table.

## Global Inequality

Zoom the stratification lens out to planetary scale: the strongest single predictor of a person's income is not talent or effort but **country of birth**. Global inequality is stratification _between_ societies stacked on top of stratification _within_ them.

**The map's vocabulary** has evolved: 'First/Third World' (Cold War politics) gave way to 'developed/developing', then to the World Bank's **income tiers** — high, upper-middle, lower-middle, low — measured by GNI per capita, usually corrected for **purchasing power parity (PPP)**, since a euro buys different amounts of life in Oslo and Nairobi. The **Human Development Index (HDI)** widens the lens beyond money to health and education.

**Why the gaps? Three big theory families:**

- **Modernization theory** (Rostow): development is a staircase every country can climb — traditional society, preconditions, **take-off**, drive to maturity, mass consumption. Poverty = not yet climbed; remedy = technology, investment, 'modern' institutions.
- **Dependency theory**: the poverty of the periphery is not a starting point but a _product_ — colonial and post-colonial trade locked poor economies into exporting cheap raw materials and importing expensive finished goods. The staircase is blocked from above.
- **World-systems analysis** (Wallerstein): one capitalist world economy with a **core**, a **periphery**, and a **semi-periphery** in between — countries can move between zones (the interesting cases: South Korea up, Argentina down), but the structure of unequal exchange persists.

**The empirical shock of recent decades** is convergence _in parts_: East Asia's growth pulled over a billion people out of extreme poverty, powered by growth-rate arithmetic — a poor economy compounding at 7% doubles each decade, while a rich one at 1-2% barely moves.

$$\text{years to double} \approx \frac{70}{\text{growth rate}}$$

For business, the tiers are a strategy map: where the next middle class emerges, where labor costs sit, where infrastructure gaps are the product — the 'bottom of the pyramid' is both a moral fact and a market.

**Three families of explanation**

| Theory | Gap exists because… | Prescription |
|---|---|---|
| Modernization | Some societies haven't yet modernized | Diffuse technology, education |
| Dependency | The core develops by underdeveloping the periphery | Restructure the world economy |
| Institutionalist | Inclusive vs extractive institutions | Build inclusive rules |

> **Tip:** Never compare living standards across countries without the PPP correction — a euro buys different amounts of life in Oslo and Nairobi, and uncorrected GNI figures overstate the gap by roughly half.

## Work and Economy

Work is not just production — it is one of society's main machines for distributing identity, status, and time. Sociology tracks how that machine has been rebuilt three times.

**The three sectors** tell the macro story:

- **Primary**: extracting from nature — agriculture, fishing, mining.
- **Secondary**: transforming — manufacturing, construction.
- **Tertiary**: services — trade, finance, education, care, code.

Every industrializing society replays the same handover: farms shed labor to factories, then factories to offices and shops. In most rich economies services passed industry in the mid-1970s and now employ three of every four workers — the **post-industrial society** (Bell), whose key resource is knowledge rather than raw material.

**Inside the workplace**, the classic diagnoses still apply:

- Marx's **alienation**: when workers control neither the product, the process, their relations, nor their own potential, work estranges instead of expressing. The assembly line is the textbook case; the micromanaged ticket queue is its modern echo.
- Weber's **rationalization**: work organized by calculation — rules, metrics, efficiency. Taylor's **scientific management** pushed it to the stopwatch; Ritzer's **McDonaldization** names its retail form: efficiency, calculability, predictability, control.
- The **informal economy**: work outside regulation and statistics — from street vending to undeclared care work — which in some economies exceeds the formal one.

**The current rebuild**: the **gig economy** re-slices jobs into tasks bid on through platforms. Flexibility rises; so does insecurity — the platform worker is formally free and structurally supervised by an algorithm, a combination Marx and Weber would each claim as evidence.

> **The handovers:** primary → secondary → tertiary — each one remakes class, cities, and firms.

Managers live inside these currents: every automation decision, metric dashboard, and contractor contract is a small vote on what work becomes next.

**The three sectors**

| Sector | Activity | Employment trend in rich economies |
|---|---|---|
| Primary | Extracting from nature | Shrunk to a few percent |
| Secondary | Transforming — manufacturing | Peaked mid-20th century, declining |
| Tertiary | Services — trade, finance, care, code | Three of every four workers |

> **Common pitfall:** Hearing 'service economy' as 'knowledge economy.' The tertiary sector spans both the algorithm designer and the delivery rider — service work is the most _polarized_ of the three, not the most glamorous.

## Population and Urbanization

Demography is sociology with the best data: births, deaths, and moves, each recorded and each remaking markets decades in advance.

**The core rates** (all per 1,000 people per year): the **crude birth rate**, the **crude death rate**, and their difference, the rate of **natural increase**. Add net migration and you have a country's whole population arithmetic.

$$\text{growth rate} = \frac{\text{births} - \text{deaths}}{1000} + \text{net migration rate}$$

A population growing at $g\%$ doubles in roughly $70/g$ years — the rule of 70 again, now applied to people.

**The demographic transition** is the master pattern of the last two centuries, in four stages:

1. **Pre-industrial**: high births, high deaths — population barely grows.
2. **Early transition**: deaths fall (sanitation, vaccines) while births stay high — the population explosion.
3. **Late transition**: births fall too (education, urban life, contraception) — growth slows.
4. **Post-transition**: both rates low; growth near zero, sometimes negative — the aging society.

Every country walks these stages; what differs is when and how fast. Europe took two centuries; parts of Asia compressed it into two generations.

**Population pyramids** photograph the transition: a wide-based triangle means stage 2 (young, exploding), a barrel means stage 4 (aging), an inverted base means shrinking cohorts — next decade's schools, workforces, and pension bills, visible today.

**Urbanization** is the transition's travel companion: since 2007, over half of humanity lives in cities, heading toward two-thirds. Cities concentrate labor markets, ideas, and consumption — Durkheim saw the shift from the village's **mechanical solidarity** (likeness) to the city's **organic solidarity** (interdependence of specialists).

For business, demography is destiny with a lag: baby booms become housing demand in 25 years and pension crises in 65 — the rare forecast a firm can actually bank on.

**The demographic transition**

| Stage | Births | Deaths | Population |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pre-industrial | High | High | Barely grows |
| Early transition | High | Falling | Explodes |
| Late transition | Falling | Low | Growth slows |
| Post-transition | Low | Low | Stabilizes or shrinks |

> **Common pitfall:** Expecting growth to stop when fertility falls. **Demographic momentum** keeps a young population growing for decades — the large cohorts already born still have their children ahead of them.

## Social Change and Environment

Societies feel permanent and are not: **social change** — the transformation of culture, institutions, and structure over time — is the discipline's final subject, and business lives or dies by reading it early.

**The engines of change:**

- **Technology**: the stirrup, the pill, the smartphone — each rewired institutions far beyond its inventors' intent (Ogburn's **cultural lag**: material change outruns the norms that must govern it; law and etiquette arrive years after the gadget).
- **Social movements**: organized collective efforts to promote or resist change — labor, suffrage, environmentalism, consumer rights. Movements typically pass through **emergence** (grievance), **coalescence** (organization), **bureaucratization** (staff and strategy), and **decline** — by success, co-optation, repression, or fading.
- **Diffusion**: change spreading between groups and societies.

**Rogers' diffusion of innovations** is the businessperson's slice of the theory. Adopters sort into a bell curve: **innovators** (2.5%), **early adopters** (13.5%), **early majority** (34%), **late majority** (34%), **laggards** (16%). Cumulative adoption therefore traces an **S-curve** — slow ignition, explosive middle, saturating end. The strategic moment is the **tipping point** around 16% cumulative adoption: innovators plus early adopters are aboard, and the contagion jumps from visionaries to the pragmatic majority — or dies in the chasm between them.

> **The S-curve:** adoption starts slow → turns explosive → saturates.

**Environment as the limit case**: industrial growth meets a finite biosphere. Concepts with market consequences: **sustainability** (meeting present needs without eating the future's), the **tragedy of the commons** (rational individuals overusing a shared resource nobody owns), and **environmental externalities** returning as regulation, carbon prices, and reputational risk.

The closing loop for this unit: the sociological imagination, pointed forward. Every trend deck is amateur sociology; the professional version asks _which_ structures are moving, _whose_ interests move them, and _where_ on the S-curve the moment sits.

**A movement's life cycle**

| Stage | What happens |
|---|---|
| Emergence | Shared grievance finds a voice |
| Coalescence | Organization, leaders, first wins |
| Bureaucratization | Staff, budgets, strategy |
| Decline | Success, co-optation, repression, or absorption |

> **Tip:** Ogburn's cultural lag is a standing business alert: material change outruns norms and law by years. The gap between a new technology and its eventual regulation is both your window of opportunity and your compliance risk.
